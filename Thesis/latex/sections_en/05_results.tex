\section{Results}

\subsection{Evaluation Map and Core Results}

Chapter~4 defined the penetration surrogate as a censoring-aware, low-dimensional probabilistic regression problem embedded in a larger optical-archive-to-screening workflow. Therefore, the results in this chapter are no longer organized as a single leaderboard, but rather as a set of interconnected evaluation views. The full-clean CDF protocol tests the selected in-domain point-wise surrogate on all cleaned held-out CDF points in Dataset~\#1. The uncensored CDF table serves as the primary censoring-controlled point-wise check in Dataset~\#2. The P50-observed and q1-extrapolated tables are condition-level checks designed to separate the representative behavior within the measurement window from the extrapolated continuation behavior. The Leave-One-Nozzle-Out (LONO) protocol tests the transferability to unseen nozzle families, and the final ablation section attributes the design choices that enable the MLP for downstream screening workflows.

The extraction and fitting workflow generated 295 cleaned fitted trajectory files across the processed campaign archives after converting a high-throughput Mie video archive into structured CSV/JSON tables, checkpoints, fitted targets, model artifacts, and diagnostic plots. Across the broader archive, the workflow covers 9 nozzle families, 266 condition subfolders, and 2,992 recording files; its practical value lies in making terabyte-scale raw optical inputs reproducible and auditable rather than leaving them as isolated post-processing products. This step is crucial because the surrogate is not trained directly on the raw and intermittently broken plume trajectories, but rather on a target that preserves the primary temporal structure while attenuating segmentation noise and mitigating late-stage censoring bias. Figure~\ref{fig:clean-fit-example} presents a representative example: the cleaned curves retain the plume-to-plume variation, while the fitted trajectories provide a smooth and censoring-aware target that remains faithful to the observed liquid-phase envelope.

\subsection{In-Domain CDF Accuracy and Uncertainty}

Table~\ref{tab:model-summary} summarizes the roles and held-out set performance of the three learning stages. Stage~1 is trained using only one representative curve per condition, serving as a stable mean anchor; its test MAE is \SI{2.91}{mm}, measured on the corresponding representative test set. Stage~2 tackles a more challenging task—learning the conditional variance and traversing the intra-condition plume-to-plume variations across the full filtered archive; its test MAE is \SI{5.63}{mm}, evaluated on a larger and more heterogeneous test population. Thus, the two figures are not directly comparable: the higher Stage~2 MAE reflects a harder supervision target rather than a degradation in accuracy, which consequently makes Stage~2 a suitable uncertainty-aware teacher model.

Under the refreshed full-clean CDF evaluation protocol, the selected anchor-off Stage~3 MLP screening surrogate—trained with the deployed distillation loss formulation (a mixture of mean MSE and log-variance MSE, with a variance weight \(\lambda_\sigma=5.0\)) across five seeds (\(\{7,17,42,99,2024\}\))—achieves an overall RMSE of \(\SI{4.735}{mm}\pm\SI{0.037}{mm}\), an MAE of \(\SI{3.293}{mm}\pm\SI{0.039}{mm}\), and a marginal positive bias of \(\SI{+0.626}{mm}\pm\SI{0.112}{mm}\) on 692,942 held-out time points from 24,316 trajectories per seed. Among these cleaned CDF trajectories, 9,630 are right-censored, corresponding to an SCR of 39.6\%; treating the last visible point as a physical terminal value would therefore introduce a material protocol error rather than a minor bookkeeping detail. Figure~\ref{fig:pred-vs-actual-best} demonstrates that over the entire measurement range, the held-out predictions closely track the identity line, with the scatter gradually widening as penetration increases. This widening is consistent with the heteroscedastic design: the model does not forcefully impose a single, fixed error bar for all timestamps and conditions.

Uncertainty calibration is equally meaningful in an engineering context. The final 5-seed average 1$\sigma$ and 2$\sigma$ coverages under the full-clean protocol are 86.7\% and 98.5\%, respectively, which are considerably more conservative than the nominal Gaussian values of 68.3\% and 95.4\%. Figure~\ref{fig:stage3-calibration-coverage} further expands this into a reliability diagram and a coverage curve binned by predicted standard deviation, rather than just comparing the mean standard deviation with the mean error. For early-stage design screening, this conservative bias is useful, but it should not be overread as a certified false-negative guarantee: it makes the model less prone to underestimating penetration uncertainty during late stages or poorly observed intervals, while harmful liquid impingement still requires engine-representative validation.

\subsection{P50/Q1 Condition-Level Checks}

Beyond the point-wise CDF metrics, two additional protocols further sharpen the contrast between the SVGP and the MLP. On a condition-level P50 set containing 4,213 points, an oracle q1 directly fitted to the P50 observations achieved a lower bound RMSE of \SI{1.010}{mm} (undeployable); among the learning models, the SVGP led (RMSE \SI{2.649}{mm}), closely followed by the selected MLP (\(\SI{2.848}{mm}\pm\SI{0.059}{mm}\)), while the diagnostic KD variant oriented toward extrapolation regularization performed the worst (\(\SI{3.212}{mm}\pm\SI{0.245}{mm}\)). However, on a q1 extrapolated grid containing 21,527 points outside the observed P50 window, the ranking reversed: the diagnostic KD variant achieved the lowest RMSE (\(\SI{10.358}{mm}\pm\SI{0.106}{mm}\)) and the smallest positive extrapolation bias (\SI{+6.269}{mm}), leading the SVGP (\SI{10.591}{mm}, bias \SI{+6.410}{mm}) and the selected MLP (\(\SI{10.864}{mm}\pm\SI{0.064}{mm}\), bias \SI{+6.753}{mm}). These two MLP variants thus explore a genuine trade-off: the selected anchor-off model acts as a conservative in-distribution surrogate, while the diagnostic KD variant is retained as an extrapolation-regularized version, remaining closer to the SVGP-style smooth prior outside the observation window. Detailed per-seed numerical values and execution paths are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:implementation_mapping}.

\subsection{Probabilistic Calibration}

Since coverage checks only test a few confidence intervals, this study further employs reliability diagrams, the Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS), and Probability Integral Transform histograms. The 5-seed selected anchor-off runs maintain significantly lower CRPS than the physical baselines, and this gain does not stem solely from widening the uncertainty intervals: it arises from a better joint trade-off between mean accuracy and uncertainty width.

Figure~\ref{fig:calibration-current} overlays the reliability diagram and the CRPS--sharpness plane after adding the Stage~3 SVGP into the identical full-clean diagnostic protocol. The 1$\sigma$/2$\sigma$ coverage for the three-stage MLP (anchor-off) (5-seed mean 0.867/0.985) is higher than the nominal Gaussian values, meaning it is intentionally conservative on the external clean set. The refreshed Stage~3 comparison in Table~\ref{tab:external-clean-headline} reveals that the SVGP is now the strongest point predictor (RMSE \SI{4.628}{mm}, 1$\sigma$/2$\sigma$ coverage 0.703/0.939) and achieves the lowest CRPS in the refreshed calibration diagnostics, whereas the selected MLP remains a more conservative in-domain screening surrogate (RMSE \SI{4.735}{mm}, coverage well above nominal). Therefore, these two models should be understood as distinct accuracy--coverage trade-offs rather than a single-winner scenario: the SVGP is closer to nominal coverage and stronger in point accuracy, whereas the MLP retains a wider uncertainty margin and a lower-overhead workflow for downstream screening.

\subsection{LONO Transfer and OOD Failures}

The Leave-One-Nozzle-Out (LONO) evaluation checks whether the model is merely interpolating within the nozzle families seen during training. Locking the Stage~2 teacher to the $\mu$-anchor-only configuration and adopting the refreshed 5-fold LONO protocol (held-out nozzle families Nozzle0, Nozzle1, Nozzle2, Nozzle4, Nozzle5), the winning Stage~3 MLP variant (mixed raw/KD supervision in the uncertain regime) yields a 5-fold average RMSE of \(\SI{12.55}{mm}\pm\SI{12.46}{mm}\), an MAE of \(\SI{9.80}{mm}\pm\SI{10.18}{mm}\), and an average 1$\sigma$/2$\sigma$ coverage of 0.575/0.770. The identical uncensored LONO evaluation measured the Stage~3 SVGP's RMSE at \(\SI{10.16}{mm}\pm\SI{9.57}{mm}\), MAE at \(\SI{7.87}{mm}\pm\SI{7.55}{mm}\), and coverage at 0.628/0.821. The conclusions across nozzle families are thus conditional: the SVGP is a stronger backbone for point transfer, while the MLP is retained based on its in-domain conservative coverage, injection onset auxiliary output, and ablation affordability (understood per the terminology conventions in the beginning of \S4).

The exceptionally high standard deviation is primarily driven by the Nozzle0 fold, which is the only factory-fresh (brand-new) nozzle in the entire campaign. Although Nozzle1 shares the same nominal geometry as Nozzle0, Nozzles 1--8 originate from different injectors and carry unmodeled injector-to-injector variability; this structural difference also explains why the control back pressure for Nozzle1--8 covers only the 4\,bar level (versus 1\,bar and 4\,bar for Nozzle0), and why the LONO protocol collapses on the Nozzle0 fold. Nozzle0 is therefore best understood as a cross-design-family extrapolation failure rather than a normal average performance fold: the Stage~3 MLP reaches an RMSE of \SI{34.76}{mm} on this fold, and the SVGP reaches \SI{26.99}{mm}, both exhibiting systematic underestimation. If Nozzle0 is excluded, the 4-fold average RMSE for the MLP drops to \(\SI{7.00}{mm}\pm\SI{1.18}{mm}\), while the SVGP achieves \(\SI{5.95}{mm}\pm\SI{1.98}{mm}\). At this point, the coverage comparison intertwines: the 1$\sigma$ coverage is virtually identical (MLP 0.696 vs. SVGP 0.697), whereas the MLP has a higher 2$\sigma$ coverage (0.923 vs. 0.890). Therefore, the LONO evidence refutes claims of comprehensive MLP superiority, prompting instead an evaluation that separates in-domain conservative deployment from cross-family transfer accuracy.

\subsubsection{Methodological Note: Dual Protocol Evaluation}
\label{sec:dual-protocol-en}

The two evaluation protocols utilized in this chapter—the LONO cross-validation reported above and the 5-seed production bootstrap reported later in Section~\ref{sec:kd-form-fix-en}—are intentionally distinguished to answer different questions. By holding out an entire nozzle family during training, the LONO protocol measures the model's ability to generalize to nozzle geometries completely unseen during training, thus reporting the model's \textbf{Out-of-Domain (OOD)} behavior. The production protocol retrains the identical configuration across the full dataset using five random seeds and evaluates on a non-right-censored test set; it measures the model's \textbf{in-domain fit quality} and its sensitivity to random initialization, serving as an appropriate protocol for reporting variance in the core tables.

These two protocols are reported side-by-side rather than consolidated into a single ranking because each isolates independent claims. A model that wins under the LONO protocol but fails under the production bootstrap implies good generalization but poor in-domain fitting; conversely, a model that wins in the production bootstrap but fails in LONO implies a tight in-domain fit but poor performance when extrapolating to new nozzle geometries. The production protocol supports the MLP as a conservative screening surrogate within the measured design space; the LONO protocol demonstrates that the SVGP alternative backbone is stronger in point accuracy for cross-family transfer. These are complementary claims rather than a declaration of total victory for either architecture.

\subsubsection{Visual Evidence of Difficult Conditions and Failure Mode Anatomy: The Case of Nozzle 3}

The Leave-One-Nozzle-Out (LONO) evaluation indicates that the surrogate model experiences degraded coverage and amplified errors on specific nozzle families such as Nozzle~3. To observe the morphology of these challenging conditions in the raw data, this study cross-referenced Phantom high-speed Mie scattering images with the corresponding plume-by-plume penetration curves (Figure~\ref{fig:failure-mode-nozzle3}).

The image sequence ($t \approx 0.6 \sim 1.0\,\si{ms}$) reveals pronounced inter-hole asymmetry: while the left-side plumes have already established stable, continuous jets, the three plumes at the bottom right (approximately 3 to 5 o'clock positions) appear short and discontinuous. Some orifices initially squeeze out a small, low-momentum leading liquid mass detached from the main body, which rapidly decelerates under ambient aerodynamic resistance; this visual observation corresponds to the "flat-top" feature seen in the penetration curves (e.g., the green line for Plume~2, the blue line for Plume~0) during the same time window. Subsequently ($t > 1.0\,\si{ms}$), a main jet emerges in the same azimuth and rapidly overtakes the leading liquid mass, causing a step-like steep rise in the curve.

This trajectory morphology of "leading liquid mass + overtaking main jet" is consistent with wake-driven catch-up/overtaking mechanisms reported in split- or multiple-injection literature: an earlier-arriving liquid mass can alter the penetration of trailing masses through wake effects and axial aerodynamic momentum preconditioning. Because this experiment lacked simultaneous gas velocity measurements, internal flow visualization, or liquid mass identity tracking, this case can only be interpreted as image evidence "consistent with" such mechanisms; mechanical causes such as needle wobble or transient orifice throttling are equally plausible candidate explanations that current data cannot rule out.

What the data can directly support are modeling inferences. The reduced-order fitting target ($k_{1/4}t^{1/4}$) is monotonic and concave by construction, making it unable to envelope step-like trajectories where the second derivative abruptly changes. Imposing a smooth, monotonic prior on such trajectories distorts the target, which is an identifiable reason for the coverage degradation in Stage~2 and Stage~3 networks on affected nozzle families. Modeling such extreme transient sprays, therefore, requires either a more flexible shape prior or explicitly conditioning on the true mechanism once diagnostic tools capable of resolving it are in place.

\subsection{MLP Ablation Evidence and Screening Demonstration}

The preceding sections established cross-architecture results: the SVGP currently prevails in point accuracy (especially under the LONO transfer protocol), whereas the selected MLP serves as a more conservative in-domain screening surrogate. The subsequent evidence explains why the MLP tech stack is retained as an operational workflow: its training curriculum can be cheaply ablated, its injection onset auxiliary output supports GUI prototypes, and its regime-aware distillation makes the conservative screening behavior traceable rather than accidental.

\subsubsection{Stage-2 Anchor Schedule Ablation Experiment}
\label{sec:stage2-anchor-ablation-en}

Stage~2 introduces two optional early anchor penalty terms on top of the heteroscedastic NLL: a quadratic penalty that pulls \(\mu(t{\to}0)\to 0\) (weight \(\lambda_\mu\)) and a quadratic penalty that suppresses excess standard deviations beyond a fixed lower bound \(\sigma_{0}\) via \(\max(0,\,\sigma(t{\to}0)-\sigma_{0})\) (weight \(\lambda_\sigma\)). Both are integrated with a triangular kernel weighting \(w(t)=\max(0,\,1-t/\tau)\) acting over an early time window of \(\tau=\SI{0.15}{ms}\). To investigate whether these anchors help or harm cross-family generalization, this study compared three Stage~2 variants under the same LONO protocol as Stage~1: (i)~no anchor (\(\lambda_\mu=\lambda_\sigma=0\)); (ii)~$\mu$-anchor only (\(\lambda_\mu=10^{-2}\), \(\lambda_\sigma=0\)); (iii)~$\mu$+$\sigma$ double anchor (\(\lambda_\mu=10^{-2}\), \(\lambda_\sigma=10^{-3}\)). The three variants shared the winning production 7-feature configuration from Stage~1 ($\alpha=0.5$ with pressure residual) as a warm start, the same 5-fold LONO split, the identical Stage~3 anchor-off distillation chain, the same network architecture and optimizer, and a single fixed seed (42); the only varied axis was the Stage~2 anchor configuration. The held-out set metrics in this section are evaluated on the untruncated CDF~P50 points of the held-out nozzle families following the complete Stage~1$\to$2$\to$3 pipeline (evaluation point counts per fold are: Nozzle0 311,149, Nozzle2 60,369, Nozzle5 33,017, Nozzle4 28,139, Nozzle1 25,705).

This ablation experiment tests two coupled hypotheses. First, whether a mean anchor at time zero can inject useful physical priors without incurring conditional mean biases elsewhere; the comparison between no anchor and $\mu$-anchor-only isolates this trade-off. Second, whether forcefully suppressing early \(\sigma\) toward its lower bound harms uncertainty calibration on held-out nozzle families; the comparison between $\mu$-anchor-only and the double $\mu$+$\sigma$ anchor strips this axis away from the confounding effects of the mean anchor.

Verification of both hypotheses points to supporting the \(\mu\)-anchor only configuration. Activating the \(\mu\)-anchor reduces the average RMSE by \SI{1.81}{mm} (from 14.54 down to \SI{12.73}{mm}), lowers MAE by \SI{0.94}{mm}, increases 2\(\sigma\) coverage from 0.763 to 0.775, and narrows the systematic underestimation bias from \SI{-8.50}{mm} to \SI{-7.11}{mm}. The improvements are concentrated on the Nozzle2 fold: without the anchor, the NLL is free to exploit the sparsely populated window near \(t{=}0\) as a slack space, causing the no-anchor variant to collapse to an RMSE of \SI{19.18}{mm} here; the anchor removes this degree of freedom. For the three nozzles from the same test-campaign series (Nozzle1/4/5, each with ~25k--33k evaluation points), the results across all three variants lie within \SI{1}{mm} of each other, demonstrating that the value of the anchor only surfaces when a LONO fold genuinely poses an OOD challenge.

Superimposing a \(\sigma\)-anchor further tightens the average predicted standard deviation from \SI{5.68}{mm} to \SI{5.49}{mm} but yields no compensatory gains: instead, the RMSE rises to \SI{13.05}{mm} and the 2\(\sigma\) coverage drops to 0.764. This phenomenon can be explained as follows: pushing \(\sigma\) toward a lower bound near \(t{=}0\) propagates through Stage~3 distillation into a more confident yet slightly biased \(\mu\) prediction, thereby simultaneously compromising both accuracy and calibration. Therefore, the Stage~3 ablation experiment reported in the next subsection fixes the Stage~2 teacher to the $\mu$-anchor-only configuration (\(\lambda_\mu=10^{-2}\), \(\lambda_\sigma=0\)); any conclusions regarding Stage~3 regime weighting or KD forms are predicated upon this anchor choice. \subsubsection{Diagnostics on Distillation Loss Formulation and Near-FOV Fix}
\label{sec:kd-form-fix-en}

Upon switching to the "amplitude scaled plus pressure residual" feature variant—which takes the log pressure residual directly as an input—slicing Stage~3 residuals by the near-FOV indicator \(\mathrm{pen}\ge 0.9\,\mathrm{cap}_\mathrm{nozzle}\) exposed a systematic performance degradation in the high-penetration range: although the Stage~2 teacher was virtually unbiased near the FOV cap (bias \SI{-0.59}{mm}, RMSE \SI{4.82}{mm}), the distilled anchor-off student deteriorated to a bias of \SI{-2.70}{mm} and an RMSE of \SI{6.11}{mm} on the same slice. This drift cannot be attributed to the Stage 1/2 fitting—the robust cleaning pipeline employed (Hampel filtering and monotonic difference lower-bound detection, detailed in \S3) did not induce a downward drift in the teacher near the cap—hence, it must originate entirely within the Stage~3 distillation step.

To pinpoint the mechanism, this study executed a set of seven univariate ablations while keeping the aforementioned teacher fixed: deactivating the raw NLL, disabling the first/second-order shape penalties, forcing the confidence weight \(\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}}/\sigma_{\mathrm{teacher}}\) in the forward KL to 1, substituting the forward KL with the reverse KL, replacing the full KL with a $\mu$-only MSE, shortening the training to 10 epochs, and doubling the KD weight in both the uncertain and teacher-only regimes. Two variants stood out: the diagnostic variant disabling the raw NLL almost precisely replicated the teacher's near-cap behavior (bias \SI{-0.53}{mm}); the diagnostic variant replacing the full KL with a $\mu$-only MSE pulled the near-cap bias back to \SI{-0.91}{mm} and achieved the lowest full-domain RMSE among all variants. Both point to two shared causes: (i)~the raw NLL pulls the student \(\mu\) toward the lower-bound observations from the frame immediately succeeding FOV truncation; (ii)~the forward KL term \((\mu_t-\mu_s)^{2}/\sigma_s^{2}\) permits the student to evade matching the teacher \(\mu\) simply by inflating its own \(\sigma\). The remaining five variants left the near-cap bias trapped in the \([-2.7,-2.0]\) mm range, thus being ruled out as primary mechanisms.

The $\mu$-only MSE diagnostic variant severed the \(\sigma\) arbitrage pathway but simultaneously lost the implicit supervision of the KL divergence on the student \(\sigma\), causing the \(\sigma\) calibration to drift within the KD-only intervals (the uncertain and teacher-only regimes). Consequently, this study proposes a mixed distillation loss combining mean MSE and log-variance MSE:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KD}} \;=\; (\mu_s-\mu_t)^{2} \;+\; \lambda_\sigma\,\bigl(\log\sigma_s^{2} - \log\sigma_t^{2}\bigr)^{2},
\label{eq:kd-mse-mu-plus-sigma}
\end{equation}
which adds an explicit log-variance MSE on top of the mean MSE to properly re-supervise \(\sigma\), while simultaneously avoiding the reintroduction of the \(\sigma\)-arbitrage permitted by the KL formulation. Scanning across \(\lambda_\sigma\in\{0.5,1,2,5\}\), \(\lambda_\sigma=5.0\) was selected as the new Stage~3 default: under this setting, the near-cap RMSE and bias achieved a joint optimum (\SI{4.89}{mm} and \SI{-0.66}{mm}), becoming statistically indistinguishable from the teacher's \(\SI{-0.59}{mm}\); meanwhile, the point-wise \(|\sigma_s-\sigma_t|\) median on the teacher-only regime slice dropped to \SI{0.27}{mm}. Figure~\ref{fig:kd-overlay} overlays the bias--truth curves for the teacher, the baseline forward-KL student, and the new mixed-distillation student; the improvements are focused in the upper penetration regime, while the lower half remains undisturbed.

\subsubsection{Methodological Addendum: Ablation Affordability as a Unique Advantage of the MLP Architecture}
\label{sec:mlp-ablation-affordability-en}

A direct consequence of choosing the MLP as the penetration surrogate architecture is that the systematic ablations reported in this thesis are computationally affordable on conventional hardware. There are three independent ablation matrices in this work: Stage~1's 5-fold LONO across 9 engineered feature variants (Section~\ref{sec:stage1-lono-ablation}), Stage~2's LONO comparison across 3 anchor schedules under the same protocol, and Stage~3's combinatorial search over 4 regime weighting variants and 7 univariate KD loss formulation ablations. The complete LONO~$\times$~Ablation~$\times$~Seed grid required to back the core tables consumes roughly tens of GPU hours on a single consumer-grade accelerator.

The core argument lies not in the run time of a single training pass, but rather in the alignment between the task structure and the architectural inductive biases. The objective of this surrogate model is to regress $(\mu,\sigma)$ as point-wise functions of low-dimensional condition descriptors (time, scaled amplitude, log pressure residual, one-hot nozzle family indicators), with inputs exhibiting neither regular spatial grids nor long-range sequence dependencies. The local translation-invariance bias that motivates CNN designs and the long-range attention bias driving Transformers are mismatched with such a point-wise low-dimensional regression task structure; they would inject unwarranted expressiveness and parameter footprints. Building upon this, the combinatorial scan of feature engineering, anchor schedules, regime weights, and KD formulations mandated by our ablation design—amounting to dozens of configurations~$\times$~5-fold LONO~$\times$~multiple seeds—requires independent full training for every variant. Introducing more complex spatio-temporal backbones would simultaneously inflate single-run costs and restrict the number of comparable configurations, ultimately meaning that within a Master's-level compute budget, one could only report a single configuration rather than disentangle the marginal contributions of various design choices. Thus, the architecture-task alignment and ablation affordability collectively establish the MLP as a principled choice, not a mere workaround adopted for convenience. Without this alignment, the systematic ablation evidence presented in Chapters~4 and 5 would remain inaccessible under a Master's-level compute budget. This qualitative conclusion does not hinge upon specific numerical assertions regarding CNN/Transformer training overhead, nor does this thesis conduct direct benchmarking of them.

\subsubsection{Prototype Screening Outputs and Evidence Boundaries}

The final surrogate can be coupled with a simplified wall-proximity screening geometry. The current GUI prototype leverages the MLP because it natively exposes the injection onset auxiliary output required for temporal screening displays; an SVGP could substitute the $\mu,\sigma$ penetration channels, but it inherently lacks a direct replacement for this onset prediction head. Under the default toy inputs:
\[
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
d_n &= \SI{0.34}{mm},\\
p_{\mathrm{inj}} &= \SI{2000}{bar},\\
p_{\mathrm{amb}} &= \SI{15}{bar},\\
\theta_{\mathrm{tilt}} &= 20^{\circ},\\
N_{\mathrm{plume}} &= 10,\\
t_{\mathrm{inj}} &= \SI{800}{\micro s},\\
\mathrm{RPM} &= 250,
\end{aligned}
\right.
\]
the prototype generates non-negative mean predictions $\mu_{+}(t)\in[\SI{1.70}{mm},\,\SI{112.95}{mm}]$ and standard deviations $\sigma_{\parallel}(t)\in[\SI{4.39}{mm},\,\SI{17.66}{mm}]$ within the 0--\SI{5}{ms} window for plotting. The rank-oriented instantaneous near-wall interception fraction peaks at $t=\SI{4.237}{ms}$ with $P_{\mathrm{hit}}=\SI{77.66}{\percent}$, and trapezoidal integration yields a cumulative geometric exposure of $E=\SI{2.475}{ms}$. These numerical values illustrate that the end-to-end pipeline is already capable of mapping penetration distributions into time-resolved screening scores; they do not represent measured probabilities of harmful liquid-state wall wetting. Figure~\ref{fig:impingement-preview-en} provides a spatial snapshot at the peak moment, and Figure~\ref{fig:impingement-summary-en} summarizes the probabilistic geometric time history over the \SI{5}{ms} window.

The evidence boundaries are equally critical. The aforementioned interception scores should solely be interpreted as pipeline demonstrations and rank-oriented geometric surrogate metrics; they cannot be directly equated with the actual probabilities of harmful liquid-phase wall impingement occurring in a real engine. The validated contribution of this work consists of the penetration surrogate over its measured domain and its coupling with a probabilistic geometric screening interface, rather than a phase-resolved predictor of detrimental liquid impingement. All numerical claims reported in this thesis are traceable to the cleaned trajectory tables, saved checkpoints, and exported evaluation plots archived in the project repository.
