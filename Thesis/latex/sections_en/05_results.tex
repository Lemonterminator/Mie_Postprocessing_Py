\section{Results}

\subsection{Trajectory targets after extraction and fitting}

The extraction and fitting workflow produced 295 cleaned fitted trajectory files across the processed campaign archive. This stage matters because the surrogate is not trained on raw, intermittently broken plume traces; it is trained on targets that retain the main temporal structure while damping segmentation noise and reducing late-time censoring bias. Figure~\ref{fig:clean-fit-example} shows a representative example: the cleaned curves preserve plume-to-plume differences, while the fitted trajectories provide smooth targets that remain faithful to the observed liquid envelope.

\subsection{Overall surrogate accuracy and uncertainty behaviour}

Table~\ref{tab:model-summary} summarises the role and held-out performance of the three learning stages. Stage~1 achieves low error on one representative curve per operating condition and therefore acts as a stable mean anchor rather than as the final screening model. Stage~2 tackles a harder task: the full filtered dataset with within-condition plume variation and a learned variance output. Its test MAE therefore rises from \SI{8.05}{mm} to \SI{9.28}{mm}, reflecting the shift from a median-trajectory problem to a conditional-distribution problem and making Stage~2 useful as the uncertainty-aware teacher.

Under the original archived training and evaluation split, the anchor-off Stage~3 model improves both accuracy and bias, reaching an overall RMSE of~\SI{6.68}{mm}, MAE of~\SI{4.83}{mm}, and residual bias of~\SI{-0.02}{mm} over 519{,}570 held-out time points from 21{,}470 trajectories. Figure~\ref{fig:pred-vs-actual-best} shows that the held-out predictions remain close to the identity line over the full measured range, with the scatter broadening gradually as penetration grows. That broadening is consistent with the heteroscedastic design: the model is not forcing one constant error bar onto all time instants and operating conditions.

The uncertainty calibration is also meaningful in engineering terms. The final 1$\sigma$ and 2$\sigma$ coverages are 72.24\% and 97.03\%, respectively, which are slightly more conservative than the nominal Gaussian values of 68.3\% and 95.4\% (evaluated against the smoothed fitted targets rather than raw CDF observations, which sets a lower bar than raw-signal coverage would). Figure~\ref{fig:stage3-calibration-coverage} further expands this point through reliability plots and coverage curves binned by predicted standard deviation, rather than only comparing mean standard deviation with mean error. For early design screening this conservative bias is desirable: the model is somewhat more cautious than an exactly calibrated Gaussian and therefore less likely to understate late-time or weakly observed penetration uncertainty.

\subsection{Extended evaluation under the external-cleaning protocol}

The held-out results above describe the Stage~3 behaviour under the original archived training and evaluation split. To check whether the conclusion depends on that single split, this thesis also introduces an external-cleaning evaluation protocol. This protocol contains 795{,}561 prediction points and 33{,}845 trajectories, and all models are evaluated on the same target set. Table~\ref{tab:external-clean-headline} summarises the main surrogate and three baselines: a calibrated Hiroyasu--Arai empirical correlation, a delayed Naber--Siebers correlation, and a sparse heteroscedastic Gaussian process; their functional forms, image-feature source, and fitting protocol are given in Section~\ref{sec:baseline-fitting-protocol-en}. Across four repeated runs with the same anchor-off configuration, the transition-region-scaled three-stage MLP reaches a mean RMSE of \SI{6.094}{mm}, MAE of \SI{4.465}{mm}, P95 absolute error of \SI{12.888}{mm}, and conservative 1$\sigma$/2$\sigma$ coverage of 0.783/0.982. This reduces RMSE by 38.9\%, 31.1\%, and 11.7\% relative to Hiroyasu--Arai, Naber--Siebers, and the Gaussian process, respectively, showing an accuracy and tail-error advantage even against a non-parametric machine-learning baseline with uncertainty output. The resulting predicted distributions are then projected onto the simplified two-dimensional engine-geometry model in Figures~\ref{fig:impingement-preview}--\ref{fig:impingement-summary}, yielding a probabilistic wall-impingement screening proxy rather than a deterministic pass/fail threshold.

\subsection{Extended probability calibration, CRPS, and leave-one-nozzle-family evaluation}

Coverage checks only test a small number of confidence intervals, so this thesis further uses reliability diagrams, the continuous ranked probability score (CRPS), and probability-integral-transform histograms. The four repeated anchor-off runs have a mean CRPS of \SI{3.174}{mm}, lower than the physical baselines and the previous reference model. The improvement therefore does not come simply from widening uncertainty intervals; it comes from a better joint trade-off between mean accuracy and uncertainty width.

Figure~\ref{fig:calibration-current} shows the reliability diagram and the CRPS--sharpness plane. The three-stage MLP (anchor-off) has 1$\sigma$/2$\sigma$ coverage of 0.783/0.982, which is above the nominal Gaussian values and therefore slightly conservative on the external-cleaning set. The Gaussian process coverage of 0.670/0.950 is closer to nominal coverage, but its RMSE, MAE, and tail error are all higher than those of the three-stage MLP. Thus, the Gaussian process is a strong uncertainty baseline, but under the current protocol it does not replace the accuracy advantage of the three-stage model.

\subsection{Visual evidence and failure mode analysis of hard cases: Nozzle 3 example}

The leave-one-nozzle-family evaluation reveals that the surrogate model experiences under-coverage and amplified errors on specific nozzle families such as Nozzle 3. To investigate the physical root cause of the model's underperformance in these hard cases, this thesis extracts the raw Phantom high-speed Mie scattering images and cross-references them with the corresponding per-plume penetration trajectories (Figure~\ref{fig:failure-mode-nozzle3}).

The image sequence ($t \approx 0.6 \sim 1.0\,\si{ms}$) exposes extreme hole-to-hole asymmetry: while the plumes on the left establish stable continuous jets, the three plumes on the bottom right (approximately 3 to 5 o'clock positions) appear severely stunted. Some of these holes only manage to squeeze out detached puffs of low-momentum liquid, which rapidly decelerate against the ambient gas. This visual observation perfectly matches the flat "plateau" feature seen in the penetration curves (e.g., the green curve for Plume 2 and the blue curve for Plume 0) within the same time window. Subsequently ($t > 1.0\,\si{ms}$), the main jet on the bottom right suddenly bursts through and rapidly overtakes the initial puff, appearing as an unphysical step-like surge in the penetration curve.

This localised "lead-dissipation followed by main-jet surge" phenomenon strongly points to needle eccentricity and wobble during the extreme transients of the pilot injection. During the micro-lift stage, the lateral displacement of the needle severely throttles the valve seat clearance on the bottom right side. Only after the needle is fully lifted can the high-pressure fuel flow smoothly through all holes.

This finding provides a direct physical explanation for the limitations of the surrogate model. Although the computer-vision pipeline faithfully captures this micro-hydrodynamic anomaly, the reduced-order fit target ($k_{1/4}t^{1/4}$) is mathematically incapable of enveloping an "S-shaped" step trajectory that features such aggressive second-derivative discontinuities. Forcing a smooth, monotonic prior onto this highly transient physical reality inevitably leads to massive target distortion, which is the root cause of the degraded coverage of the Stage-2 and Stage-3 networks on these nozzle families. This proves that future modelling of extreme transient sprays cannot rely solely on smooth empirical shape priors, but must explicitly account for mechanical asymmetries such as needle wobble.

\subsection{Ablation evidence for the final tuning choice}

The ablation suite compared four Stage~3 configurations:
(i)~the baseline configuration with $w_{\mathrm{kd}}=0.25$ in the high-confidence raw regime bins and $\lambda_{\mathrm{anchor}}=0.01$;
(ii)~\textbf{the raw-only reliable regime variant}, which removes KD blending in reliable bins;
(iii)~\textbf{the blended uncertain regime variant}, which reintroduces partial raw supervision in the uncertain regime; and
(iv)~\textbf{the anchor-free distillation variant}, which sets $\lambda_{\mathrm{anchor}}=0$ while leaving the raw/KD regime design unchanged.
Table~\ref{tab:stage3-ablation-ranking} and Figure~\ref{fig:ablation-best} show the ranking. The anchor-off variant is the best model by overall RMSE and also reduces the residual bias from \SI{-1.37}{mm} in the baseline to \SI{-0.02}{mm}. This indicates that the early zero-penetration anchor, although physically plausible as a prior, was slightly over-constraining the learned mean and creating a global under-prediction.

It should be stated explicitly that Table~\ref{tab:stage3-ablation-ranking} supports the choice of the anchor-off variant and the raw/KD mixing strategy, but does not constitute a full hyperparameter search over the 70\%/20\% coverage boundaries, \(\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}}=\SI{10}{mm}\), or all possible weight combinations. These values are reported as a reproducible conservative design configuration; additional local sensitivity configurations should be used to check their stability once the full data and training environment are restored.

\subsection{Prototype screening output and evidence boundary}

The final surrogate can be coupled to a simplified collision-screening geometry. On the default toy input,
\[
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
d_n &= \SI{0.34}{mm},\\
p_{\mathrm{inj}} &= \SI{2000}{bar},\\
p_{\mathrm{amb}} &= \SI{15}{bar},\\
\theta_{\mathrm{tilt}} &= 20^{\circ},\\
N_{\mathrm{plume}} &= 10,\\
t_{\mathrm{inj}} &= \SI{800}{\micro s},\\
\mathrm{RPM} &= 250,
\end{aligned}
\right.
\]
the prototype produces plotting-time non-negative means $\mu_{+}(t)\in[\SI{1.70}{mm},\,\SI{112.95}{mm}]$ and $\sigma_{\parallel}(t)\in[\SI{4.39}{mm},\,\SI{17.66}{mm}]$ over the 0--\SI{5}{ms} window. The instantaneous near-wall interception score peaks at $P_{\mathrm{hit}}=\SI{77.66}{\percent}$ at $t=\SI{4.237}{ms}$, and trapezoidal integration gives a cumulative exposure of $E=\SI{2.475}{ms}$. These numbers show that the end-to-end chain can propagate a penetration distribution into a time-resolved screening score; Figure~\ref{fig:impingement-preview} shows a spatial snapshot at the peak frame, and Figure~\ref{fig:impingement-summary} provides a probabilistic temporal summary within the \SI{5}{ms} time window.

The evidence boundary is equally important. The reported collision probabilities should be read only as a pipeline demonstration and ranking-oriented proxy, not as direct probabilities of harmful liquid-wall contact in a real engine. The validated contribution in this thesis is the penetration surrogate within its measured domain and its coupling interface to a probabilistic geometry screen, not a phase-resolved predictor of harmful liquid-wall contact. The numerical claims reported here are traceable to cleaned trajectory tables, stored checkpoints, and exported evaluation figures included in the project archive.
