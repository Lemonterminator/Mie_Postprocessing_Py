\section{Results}

Chapter~4 defined the penetration surrogate as a censoring-aware probabilistic
regression problem embedded in an optical-archive-to-screening workflow. This
chapter systematically builds the evaluation evidence and gives the
quantitative verdict: the evidence does not point to a single leaderboard
winner, but instead separates into three claims -- fixed-table in-domain
accuracy, transfer to unseen nozzles, and usability as a conservative
screening surrogate.

The fixed-table numbers in this chapter are taken from a refreshed evaluation
bundle. The bundle spans two cleaned dataset roots -- the second-level
cleaned root and the third-level quality-gated root -- and regenerates four
fixed-table evaluation protocols on top of them: the uncensored CDF
protocol, the censoring-inclusive full-clean CDF protocol, the observed P50
protocol, and the SGQR continuation grid. Uncensored and full-clean are not
two separate datasets but two protocol views of the same held-out
population: the former excludes right-censored regions as the primary
evaluation lens, while the latter retains them as a censoring-inclusive
supplementary check. All four protocols are primarily reported against the
third-level quality-gated root; the second-level root is used only as a
dataset-sensitivity check, not the main verdict. In this thesis, ``QC''
means quality control that applies numerical constraints to each trajectory
through a pre-training plume gate; its concrete rules are given in
Section~\ref{sec:qc-gate-heuristics-rewrite}.

The boundary of evidential scope is therefore explicit: the refreshed bundle
supports the fixed-table in-domain comparison after the canonical protocols;
the transfer claim -- together with the curriculum-ablation evidence that
supports it -- and the screening claim continue to use their archived thesis
artifacts. The refreshed run also exports cluster-bootstrap 95\% confidence
intervals for the fixed-table point metrics, so headline gaps can be checked
against trajectory-level resampling uncertainty rather than seed spread
alone; seed standard deviations are still reported where multiple training
seeds define the relevant summary statistic.

Table~\ref{tab:metric-vocabulary-rewrite} defines the metric vocabulary used in
the chapter. RMSE and MAE measure point accuracy, bias measures signed
systematic error, and tail metrics such as P95 absolute error identify rare but
large misses. Coverage, PIT reliability, ECE, CRPS, NLL, and sharpness describe
the predicted distribution rather than only the predicted mean.

\subsection{Evaluation Map, Protocols, and Artifact Contract}
\label{sec:results-map-protocols}

The chapter uses a layered evidence contract. Layer~1 is numeric evaluation:
given a dataset root and checkpoint roster, the pipeline exports canonical
point tables, grouped CSV summaries, reliability tables, PIT histograms,
coverage curves, and a wide metric table. Layer~2 is plotting: figures read
only the Layer~1 artifacts and do not reload checkpoints. This separation is
important for the thesis because it allows figure styling to change without
changing the metric definitions.

Table~\ref{tab:protocol-map-rewrite} defines the protocols used below. The
primary fixed-table results use the third-level quality-gated dataset root
because it is the most conservative cleaned production set available in the
refreshed evaluation bundle. The second-level cleaned root remains a
dataset-sensitivity check rather than the main verdict. The censoring-inclusive
full-clean CDF protocol is regenerated on the same refreshed roots and reported
as a supplementary check: it shows how the comparison behaves when
right-censored regions are included, while the main fixed-table verdict
deliberately remains the uncensored protocol.

The metrics are also separated by evidential role. RMSE, MAE, bias, and tail
absolute error judge point accuracy. Coverage, reliability curves, PIT
histograms, CRPS, NLL, and interval scores judge probabilistic behavior.
Condition-level and time-bin CSVs diagnose where errors concentrate. This
prevents one model from being declared globally superior when it only wins one
axis.

\subsection{Primary In-Domain Verdict}
\label{sec:primary-in-domain-verdict}
\label{sec:in-domain-accuracy-en}
\label{sec:in-domain-accuracy-zh}

The primary fixed-table comparison is the QC-gated uncensored CDF protocol
(540{,}367 held-out CDF points per seed).
Table~\ref{tab:primary-leaderboard-rewrite} deliberately keeps the mainline
verdict separate from the post-hoc residual arms. Within the deployed-model and
baseline comparison, the single-output thesis SVGP is the strongest point
predictor, with an RMSE of \SI{4.087}{mm} and an MAE of \SI{2.857}{mm}. The
five-seed production MLP mean is close behind at \SI{4.185}{mm} RMSE and
\SI{2.978}{mm} MAE, while both physical curve-fitting baselines are much less
accurate.

While the single-output SVGP delivers higher point accuracy, the production
MLP offers more conservative coverage. Specifically, the five-seed mean
\(1\sigma/2\sigma\) coverage is 0.888/0.990 for the MLP, compared with
0.717/0.946 for the SVGP. The mainline comparison therefore indicates an
accuracy--coverage trade-off: the SVGP backbone improves point prediction,
whereas the production MLP serves as a conservative and operationally
convenient screening surrogate. The residual-SVGP and residual-MLP extensions
are analyzed in Section~\ref{sec:posthoc-residual-rewrite} as post-hoc
adaptation evidence rather than as part of the deployed-surrogate verdict.

\subsubsection{Censoring-Inclusive Full-Clean Check}
\label{sec:full-clean-supplement}

The full-clean CDF protocol is the censoring-inclusive counterpart of the
primary table: it evaluates the same held-out population without excluding
right-censored regions. In the refreshed bundle this protocol is regenerated on
the same QC-gated root as the primary verdict, so the two tables differ only in
whether right-censored regions are included. It remains a supplementary check
rather than the headline verdict because the primary comparison deliberately
excludes regions where the target itself is shaped by field-of-view censoring.

On the QC-gated root, the refreshed full-clean table contains 689{,}162
held-out CDF prediction points, of which 148{,}795 (21.6\%) lie in
right-censored regions. In the earlier archived bundle, the same censoring
pressure was quantified at the trajectory level: 9{,}630 of 24{,}316 cleaned
trajectories (39.6\%) were right-censored. This is the spatial
censoring ratio audited in Chapter~4 and cited in the data chapters. Both the
point-level and trajectory-level views show that treating the last visible
measurement as a physical terminal penetration would constitute a material
protocol error rather than a bookkeeping detail. On the refreshed censoring-inclusive
view, the five-seed production MLP reaches \SI{4.584}{mm} RMSE, \SI{3.211}{mm}
MAE, \SI{+0.829}{mm} bias, and conservative \(1\sigma/2\sigma\) coverage of
0.869/0.986. The Stage~3 SVGP is again the stronger point predictor (RMSE
\SI{4.440}{mm} with 95\% interval [4.373, 4.499], MAE \SI{3.109}{mm}) but with
a sharper predictive distribution and less conservative coverage
(0.706/0.942), and both physical baselines
remain far behind (\SI{11.187}{mm} and \SI{10.442}{mm} RMSE, corresponding to
MLP reductions of 59.0\% and 56.1\%). The censoring-inclusive ordering is
therefore the same as the primary uncensored verdict.

\subsection{Calibration and Distributional Behavior}
\label{sec:calibration-distributional}

Coverage at a few sigma levels is only a coarse summary, so the refreshed
pipeline also exports reliability curves, PIT histograms, CRPS, NLL, interval
scores, and sigma-bin calibration tables for the probabilistic fixed-table
sets. These diagnostics reinforce the same trade-off. The production MLP is
conservative: its mean predicted standard deviation is about
\SI{5.96}{mm}, and its \(1\sigma\) coverage is about 0.888. The mainline
single-output SVGP is sharper, with mean predicted standard deviation about
\SI{3.73}{mm}, lower CRPS (\SI{2.063}{mm}), and lower RMSE, but less
conservative \(1\sigma\) coverage. The post-hoc QC-retrained residual-SVGP
pushes CRPS lower again (\SI{1.988}{mm}) while still remaining less
conservative than the production MLP.

This distinction is important for the downstream screening interface. A model
with a more accurate mean prediction is not automatically a safer rank-oriented
screening surrogate if its predictive distribution is under-dispersed for edge
conditions. Conversely, the MLP's conservative empirical coverage reflects a
deliberately cautious predictive distribution under the measured protocol; it
does not constitute a certified bound on false-negative rates.

\subsection{Cross-Nozzle Transfer and Failure Boundaries}
\label{sec:lono-boundaries-rewrite}
\label{sec:lono-transfer-en}
\label{sec:lono-transfer-zh}
\label{sec:svgp-lono-comparison-en}
\label{sec:svgp-lono-comparison-zh}

The fixed-table results above test in-domain performance on held-out points
from the cleaned dataset root. They do not answer whether the surrogate can
generalize to an entirely unseen injector. That question is handled by the
Leave-One-Nozzle-Out protocol retained from the archived thesis artifacts.

Transfer performance is evaluated using a five-fold LONO protocol that holds
out Nozzle0, Nozzle1, Nozzle2, Nozzle4, and Nozzle5 in turn. Across all five
folds, the Stage~3 SVGP achieves a lower average RMSE than the MLP
(\(\SI{10.16}{mm}\pm\SI{9.57}{mm}\) versus
\(\SI{12.55}{mm}\pm\SI{12.46}{mm}\)). The same ordering remains after the
out-of-design-family Nozzle0 fold is excluded: the SVGP gives
\(\SI{5.95}{mm}\pm\SI{1.98}{mm}\), compared with
\(\SI{7.00}{mm}\pm\SI{1.18}{mm}\) for the MLP.

The empirical coverage results are mixed. After Nozzle0 is excluded, the
\(1\sigma\) coverage values are nearly identical (0.697 for the SVGP and 0.696
for the MLP), whereas the MLP retains higher \(2\sigma\) coverage (0.923 versus
0.890). The \(\pm\) terms denote cross-fold standard deviations from the
archived single-seed LONO run, not multi-seed training variability.

The LONO results therefore show a clearer point-transfer difference than the
in-domain comparison: the SVGP is the more accurate point-transfer backbone.
The production MLP remains the retained model in the active workflow because
it provides conservative in-domain uncertainty estimates, exposes the
auxiliary onset output required by the screening GUI, and supports affordable
ablation of the training curriculum.

\subsubsection{Physical Failure-Mode Check: Nozzle 3}
\label{sec:nozzle3-failure-rewrite}

The LONO averages identify transfer boundaries, but they do not show the raw
spray morphology behind difficult cases. An archived Nozzle~3 condition
provides a useful failure anatomy by pairing Phantom high-speed Mie images with
the corresponding plume-by-plume penetration curves
(Figure~\ref{fig:failure-mode-nozzle3-rewrite}). In the early window from
\SI{0.6}{ms} to about \SI{1.0}{ms}, several bottom-right plumes remain short
and discontinuous while other holes have already formed continuous jets. The
penetration curves show the same event as a flat-top or step-like trajectory:
a small leading liquid mass appears first, decelerates, and is later overtaken
by a faster main jet.

This image evidence is consistent with wake-driven catch-up or transient
orifice-throttling interpretations, but it is not a causal diagnosis. The
experiment does not contain simultaneous gas velocity, internal-flow
visualization, or liquid-mass identity tracking. The direct modeling conclusion
is narrower and more useful: a smooth monotone continuation target cannot
represent abrupt step-like curvature changes without distorting the target or
the predictive uncertainty. These cases explain why some within-family
conditions remain difficult even after the global LONO and fixed-table metrics
look acceptable.

\subsubsection{Heuristic Data Quality Control: Plume-Level QC Gate}
\label{sec:qc-gate-heuristics-rewrite}

The third-level quality-gated dataset is materialized from the second-level
cleaned export by auditing CDF wide-series traces before any surrogate is
trained. The gate operates on whole condition--plume rows: it removes a
plume under an operating condition when the measured trace shows repeated
shape pathologies or systematically low penetration relative to the other
holes under the same condition. The decision rests on two heuristic rule
families:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Shape pathology}: each trace is checked for negative steps,
  spike-like steps or first-/second-derivative jumps, and ``plateau-then-jump''
  behavior (a run of small steps followed by a sudden large jump); when the
  fraction of traces exhibiting these pathologies within a condition--plume
  group exceeds a threshold, the group is flagged as a recurrent shape
  pathology (thresholds in Table~\ref{tab:qc-shape-thresholds-rewrite}).
  \item \emph{Systematic low penetration}: at several early time grid points,
  each plume's median penetration is compared to the condition-level median
  across the other holes under the same condition, giving a ratio and a
  MAD-based robust $z$-score; if the ratio falls below threshold at enough
  time points, the plume is flagged as systematically (or severely) low. If
  the same plume\_idx on the same nozzle repeatedly earns this flag across
  enough conditions, it additionally earns repeat support across conditions
  (thresholds in Table~\ref{tab:qc-lowpen-thresholds-rewrite}).
\end{itemize}

The hard-drop rule is an OR over three conditions: an optional manual
image-evidence flag; systematic low penetration with either repeat support
or the severe threshold; and recurrent shape pathology. The drop decision is
then applied to the CDF, Cartesian-width, and polar-width export tables, as
well as the CDF censoring-point table, for that condition--plume pair. The
criterion is independent of any trained surrogate -- it rests only on
measured trace shape, same-condition cross-hole comparison, and
cross-condition repeat support (plus optional manual image evidence) --
so QC here is a pre-training data-quality filter, not a post-hoc selection
based on model performance.

\subsection{Why the MLP Workflow Was Retained}
\label{sec:why-mlp-retained-rewrite}
\label{sec:mlp-ablation-affordability-en}
\label{sec:mlp-ablation-affordability-zh}

The preceding sections make the model trade-off explicit. The SVGP family is
stronger for point prediction and LONO transfer. The MLP workflow is retained
for a different reason: it makes the staged curriculum, uncertainty behavior,
onset auxiliary output, and screening deployment auditable within a Master's
thesis compute budget. The ablation evidence explains that choice.

\subsubsection{Curriculum Ablations}
\label{sec:curriculum-ablations-rewrite}
\label{sec:stage2-anchor-ablation-en}
\label{sec:stage2-anchor-ablation-zh}

The Stage~2 anchor ablation tested whether an early-time mean anchor and an
early-time sigma anchor improve the teacher used by Stage~3. The
\(\mu\)-anchor-only variant is the most defensible teacher setting: it lowers
the five-fold LONO RMSE from \SI{14.54}{mm} to \SI{12.73}{mm}, improves MAE,
and avoids the overconfident behavior introduced by the double

which is precisely where a physically motivated anchor should matter; the full
archived tables, including the per-fold breakdown and the bias and coverage
columns, are collected in Appendix~\ref{app:evaluation-diagnostics}.

teacher guidance should be blended. The uncertain-regime mixed variant obtains
the lowest average LONO RMSE and MAE, but the margin between Stage~3 variants
is only about \SI{0.21}{mm}, far below the cross-fold standard deviation. This
should be read as a mild selection preference, not as a sweeping architectural
claim.

\subsubsection{Post-Hoc Adaptation: Residual, FiLM, and SVGP Extensions}
\label{sec:posthoc-residual-rewrite}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{images/residual_family_head_20260608/Architecture.png}
\caption{Three injector-family conditioning mechanisms in increasing adaptation depth (left to right): direct family heads, residual family heads, and residual-FiLM.}
\label{fig:residual-architecture-rewrite}
\label{fig:residual-architecture}
\end{figure}

The residual/FiLM/SVGP post-hoc adaptation study tests a specific physical
hypothesis. As documented in Appendix~\ref{app:evaluation-diagnostics},
Nozzle~1--8 share a single refurbished injector while Nozzle~0 is a new,
factory-fresh reference unit; this hardware-state difference survives the
\(t^{1/4}\) (\(A\)-scaling) normalization, and no recorded operating feature
encodes it. The binary injector-family identifier (fresh reference versus the
pooled refurbished families, the family ID below) is therefore the only
channel that carries this signal. On top of the Stage~1--3 trunk of
Section~\ref{sec:mlp-curriculum-en}, this subsection tests three mechanisms of
increasing adaptation depth for injecting that channel into the MLP
(Figure~\ref{fig:residual-architecture-rewrite}):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Direct family heads:} replace the single mean head after the shared trunk with one head per family
  (\(\hat\mu = w_{\mu,f}^{\top} h(x)\)); the family ID only selects the output head and never enters the feature vector.
  \item \textbf{Residual family heads:} freeze the trunk and the shared mean head, then route the family ID to a
  zero-initialized additive correction (\(\hat\mu = w_{\mu}^{\top} h(x) + \Delta_f(h(x))\)).
  \item \textbf{Residual-FiLM:} stack an identity-initialized last-block affine modulation
  (\(\tilde h = \gamma_f \odot h + \beta_f\)~\cite{Perez2018FiLM}) on the residual family head while retaining the
  \(\Delta_f\) branch.
\end{itemize}
For an unseen family, the residual and FiLM variants fall back to the shared
predictor (\(\Delta_f=0\); \(\gamma=I,\beta=0\)), whereas a direct family head
must hard-route to an existing ID. The figure also identifies which components
are frozen or updated and gives each level's warm-start source. The shared
log-variance head remains shared and trainable in all three mechanisms. This is
the lightweight-adapter (PEFT) deployment pattern referenced in the Chapter~2
workflow mapping (Table~\ref{tab:workflow-mapping-en})~\cite{Han2024PEFT}; the
SVGP residual variants test the same routed-residual idea on a kernel backbone
(below).

The remainder of this subsection follows three evidence chains: the controlled
MLP adaptation chain on the fixed table, the residual-SVGP in-domain
point-error frontier, and the residual-MLP within-family LONO follow-up.

The evidence in this subsection comes from two training lineages that must be
read separately. The earlier adaptation study (archived 2026-06-08)
established the family-head \(\to\) residual \(\to\) FiLM ladder on an
independent single-seed lineage that differs from the five-seed production MLP
in feature variant, Stage~2 teacher form, onset auxiliary head, and
trunk-freezing policy (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:residual-lineage-disclosure});
its numbers therefore support within-lineage comparisons only, and direct
deltas against the production mean would misread lineage differences as
architecture effects.

\textbf{Controlled retraining lineage.} To isolate architectural effects from
training-lineage differences, a single-head pooled baseline without family
routing (the ``Stage~3 pooled baseline''; code label P0) was warm-started from
the production Stage~2 winner. It was retrained on the QC-gated data with the
raw-CDF/KD mixed supervision of Chapter~4 Stage~3, omitting only the Stage~3
onset auxiliary head. This supplies a common-protocol starting point for the
subsequent architectural changes rather than defining another training stage.
The controlled chain then proceeds as pooled baseline \(\to\) direct family
head \(\to\) residual \(\to\) FiLM.

Unlike the pooled baseline, which is initialized from the Stage~2 teacher,
each subsequent level is initialized from its predecessor in a
function-preserving way. The maximum output difference remains below
\(10^{-6}\) after bridging, and the FiLM identity initialization preserves the
function exactly. Each level introduces one new mechanism and distills from
its predecessor. The chain is single-seed and is evaluated on the same
540{,}367-point QC-gated uncensored CDF fixed table as the primary comparison;
for this controlled retraining lineage, the table includes training
trajectories. Table~\ref{tab:posthoc-residual-rewrite} is therefore a controlled
architecture comparison on a common physical scale, not an independent
generalization test. The controlled pooled baseline (\SI{4.192}{mm}) is
consistent with the five-seed production mean (\SI{4.185}{mm}), indicating
that the chain starts from a representative operating point.

The controlled chain settles three points.

\textbf{First, direct family heads bring no fixed-table benefit.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Point error:} relative to the pooled baseline, the paired trajectory-cluster bootstrap gives
  \(\Delta\)RMSE \(=+0.028\;[+0.022,+0.034]\), with a confidence interval excluding zero.
  \item \textbf{Probabilistic scores:} \(\Delta\)CRPS \(=+0.052\;[+0.049,+0.054]\),
  \(\Delta\)NLL \(=+0.035\), and \(\Delta\)ECE \(=+0.003\) likewise show no improvement.
  \item \textbf{Training objective versus fixed table:} its internal Stage~3 test loss falls from 2.362 to 2.231,
  yet the physical-scale metrics degrade; an improvement of the composite distillation loss does not imply an
  improvement on the fixed-table evaluation.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Second, residual conversion is a bias-repair channel.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Overall bias:} it drops from \(+0.508\) to \(+0.050\), with paired
  \(\Delta=-0.458\;[-0.467,-0.450]\).
  \item \textbf{P50 observed-window bias:} it vanishes monotonically along the chain:
  \(+0.935 \to +0.786 \to +0.379 \to +0.004\).
  \item \textbf{Injector-family slice:} the repair happens almost entirely on the refurbished family. The pooled
  baseline is nearly unbiased on the fresh reference family (57.6\% of fixed-table rows; bias \(-0.019\)), but leaves
  \(+1.223\) systematic over-prediction to the refurbished family; the residual head cuts the latter to \(+0.253\),
  with within-family paired \(\Delta\)RMSE \(=-0.072\), significant.
  \item \textbf{Point-error trade-off:} the reference family degrades slightly but significantly (\(+0.063\)), so the
  overall RMSE gain is only \(-0.011\;[-0.020,-0.000]\). The families' biases differ by about \SI{1.24}{mm}, which
  quantifies the family-conditional bias under pooled training and shows why only a family-routing channel can repair it.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Third, FiLM is a calibration channel rather than a point-error channel.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Probabilistic quality:} relative to the pooled baseline, CRPS improves from 2.283 to 2.192, NLL from
  2.852 to 2.760, and ECE from 0.074 to 0.052; all paired confidence intervals exclude zero. This ordering also occurs
  in the earlier study lineage and is the only result that replicates stably across both lineages.
  \item \textbf{Sharpness:} the mean predictive standard deviation narrows from \SI{5.79}{mm} to \SI{4.98}{mm}.
  \item \textbf{Point error and bias:} FiLM's RMSE difference against the pooled baseline is not significant
  (\(+0.005\;[-0.009,+0.019]\), the only null paired delta in the chain). Its mean correction is nearly identical on
  both families (\(-0.293/-0.290\)), closer to a global shift than to family discrimination, and overshoots the overall
  bias to \(-0.292\). The FiLM-versus-residual RMSE ordering also flips between the two lineages, so RMSE differences
  at this level carry no information.
  \item \textbf{Extrapolation boundary:} no level of the chain reaches it; all four models have RMSE 10.66--10.74 on
  the extrapolated SGQR grid, and central-90 coverage remains conservative (0.963--0.977).
\end{itemize}

The residual multi-task SVGP is an additive routed-residual model. In scaled
penetration space its mean has the form
\(\hat{y}(x,f)=g_{\mathrm{shared}}(x)+\Delta g_f(x)\), where
\(g_{\mathrm{shared}}\) is a frozen shared Stage~3 SVGP and \(\Delta g_f\) is a
zero-mean residual SVGP trained only for injector family \(f\). The family
identifier is deliberately not part of the kernel input vector; it only routes
each sample to the corresponding residual process. If a future family has no
trained residual process, the prediction falls back to the shared SVGP with
\(\Delta g_f=0\). The implementation also trains a final shared log-variance GP
on the residual errors, so the mean correction and uncertainty model remain
separate.

Among the reported fixed-table artifacts, the residual multi-task SVGP has the
lowest point-error values: RMSE 3.921, reaching \SI{3.848}{mm} after QC
retraining (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:residual-climb}). However, these results
come from a different training lineage and are not differenced against the
controlled MLP chain, so they do not isolate a causal kernel-backbone effect.
The residual-SVGP was not LONO-tested in the
same way as the main Stage~3 MLP and SVGP comparison, so transfer claims
continue to rest on the MLP arms. The refreshed design-comparison, climb,
lineage-disclosure, and family-slice tables, together with the archived
adaptation-study figures, are collected in
Appendix~\ref{app:evaluation-diagnostics}.

The residual-MLP LONO follow-up uses the earlier study-lineage protocol
(Appendix Table~\ref{tab:residual-lono}). Within the modified injector family,
the residual conversion repairs much of the bias of the direct family head and
reduces RMSE across the in-family folds from about \SI{7.44}{mm} to about
\SI{5.16}{mm}. This result supports within-family adaptation but does not
establish zero-shot generalization to a genuinely new injector family.

The broader conclusion is that injector identity is a useful conditioning
variable, but its value is channel-specific. In the controlled MLP chain,
family identity does not materially improve fixed-table point error: all four
levels lie between 4.18 and 4.22 with overlapping confidence intervals.
Instead, the residual head repairs family-linked systematic bias, while FiLM
improves uncertainty quality. These variants therefore act as lightweight
post-training adapters around a shared base surrogate.

The residual multi-task SVGP provides a parallel routed-family adaptation on a
kernel backbone. Its reported fixed-table values define the in-domain
point-error frontier, but the lineage difference prevents attributing that gap
to the kernel backbone alone. The deployed pooled production MLP needs no
identity input for point accuracy, whereas deployments targeting bias or
calibration may consider residual/FiLM-style adaptation. The residual-SVGP has
not been evaluated under a
matching LONO protocol and therefore cannot yet be treated as the preferred
transfer architecture. Because these adapters rely on known family
identifiers, this adaptation is distinct from zero-shot transfer. The Nozzle0
and Nozzle6 stress cases illustrate the boundary: an injector identifier or
output residual can shift a learned representation, but it cannot synthesize
trunk-level features for an unseen design family that the shared surrogate
never represented; the extrapolated SGQR region is likewise unaffected by any
conditioning mechanism.

\label{sec:screening-boundary-rewrite}
\label{sec:prototype-screening-en}
\label{sec:prototype-screening-zh}

The final purpose of the surrogate is not just to minimize penetration error.
It is to feed a probabilistic geometric screening interface. The current GUI
prototype uses the MLP because the deployed MLP workflow also provides an
injection-onset auxiliary output for temporal screening displays. An SVGP could
replace the \(\mu,\sigma\) penetration channels, but it does not directly
provide this auxiliary onset output.

Under the default toy inputs used in the archived demonstration, the screening
prototype maps the predicted penetration distribution into a time-resolved
geometric interception score. For Design~A, the rank-oriented instantaneous
piston score peaks at \(P_{\mathrm{hit}}=77.66\%\), and the trapezoidal
cumulative exposure reaches \(E=\SI{2.475}{ms}\) by \SI{5}{ms}. Changing only
the geometry and kinematics changes the verdict: a wider bowl at the same
\SI{250}{rpm} increases \(E\) to \SI{3.364}{ms}, while a faster Hermite-profile
piston at \SI{2250}{rpm} reduces the cumulative exposure to
\SI{1.446}{ms} despite a high instantaneous opening.
