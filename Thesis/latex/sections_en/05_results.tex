\section{Results}

\subsection{Evaluation Map and Core Results}

Chapter~4 defined the penetration surrogate as a censoring-aware, low-dimensional probabilistic regression problem embedded in a larger optical-archive-to-screening workflow. Therefore, the results in this chapter are no longer organized as a single leaderboard, but rather as a set of interconnected evaluation views. The uncensored CDF table is the primary in-domain, censoring-controlled point-wise check in Dataset~\#2. The full-clean CDF protocol additionally evaluates the surrogate on all cleaned held-out CDF points in Dataset~\#1 including right-censored regions, and is reported as a censoring-inclusive supplementary view. The P50-observed and q1-extrapolated tables are condition-level checks designed to separate the representative behavior within the measurement window from the extrapolated continuation behavior. The Leave-One-Nozzle-Out (LONO) protocol tests the transferability to unseen nozzles, and the final ablation section attributes the design choices that enable the MLP for downstream screening workflows. Table~\ref{tab:results-claims-map} maps every distinct evaluation claim in this chapter to its protocol, prediction target, sample size, seed count, models compared, and headline conclusion, so the multiple protocols can be kept apart.

\subsection{In-Domain Penetration Accuracy and Uncertainty}
\label{sec:in-domain-accuracy-en}

Table~\ref{tab:model-summary} summarizes the roles and held-out set performance of the three learning stages. Stage~1 is trained using only one representative curve per condition---directly selecting, for each condition, the single trajectory (one specific hole, one specific repetition) whose q1-extrapolated penetration at \SI{5}{ms} lies at the condition median---serving as a stable mean anchor; its test MAE is \SI{2.91}{mm}, measured on the corresponding representative test set. Stage~2, by contrast, tackles a more challenging task: it spans both the intra-condition plume-to-plume variations and the shot-to-shot variability across the full filtered archive---repetitions of the same plume under the same condition enter training as independent samples---and learns the conditional variance; its test MAE is \SI{5.63}{mm}, evaluated on a larger and more heterogeneous test population. Thus, the two figures are not directly comparable: the higher Stage~2 MAE reflects a harder supervision target rather than a degradation in accuracy, which consequently makes Stage~2 a suitable uncertainty-aware teacher model.

The headline in-domain result is straightforward. Under the conservative uncensored CDF protocol, the selected anchor-off Stage~3 MLP screening surrogate achieves an RMSE of \(\SI{4.265}{mm}\pm\SI{0.040}{mm}\), an MAE of \(\SI{3.032}{mm}\pm\SI{0.048}{mm}\), and a marginal positive bias of \(\SI{+0.562}{mm}\pm\SI{0.119}{mm}\). These values are averaged across five training seeds \(\{7,17,42,99,2024\}\) using the deployed mixed mean-MSE/log-variance-MSE distillation loss with \(\lambda_\sigma=5.0\). The protocol contains 542,565 held-out time points per seed and is the primary in-domain point-wise metric.

As a censoring-inclusive supplementary check, the same model evaluated on the full cleaned CDF set yields an RMSE of \(\SI{4.735}{mm}\pm\SI{0.037}{mm}\) on 692,942 held-out time points from 24,316 trajectories per seed. Among these cleaned CDF trajectories, 9,630 are right-censored, corresponding to an SCR of 39.6\%. Treating the last visible point as a physical terminal value would therefore introduce a material protocol error rather than a minor bookkeeping detail. On the primary uncensored CDF subset, Figure~\ref{fig:pred-vs-actual-best} demonstrates that over the entire measurement range, the held-out predictions closely track the identity line, with the scatter gradually widening as penetration increases. This widening is consistent with the heteroscedastic design: the model does not forcefully impose a single, fixed error bar for all timestamps and conditions.

Uncertainty calibration is equally meaningful in an engineering context. The final 5-seed average 1$\sigma$ and 2$\sigma$ coverages are 88.7\% and 98.9\% on the uncensored protocol (86.7\% and 98.5\% on the censoring-inclusive full set), both considerably more conservative than the nominal Gaussian values of 68.3\% and 95.4\%. Figure~\ref{fig:stage3-calibration-coverage} further expands this into a reliability diagram and a coverage curve binned by predicted standard deviation, rather than just comparing the mean standard deviation with the mean error. For early-stage design screening, this conservative bias is useful, but it should not be overread as a certified false-negative guarantee: it makes the model less prone to underestimating penetration uncertainty during late stages or poorly observed intervals, while harmful liquid impingement still requires engine-representative validation.

\subsection{Condition-Level Checks: Observed Median and Fitted Continuation}
\label{sec:condition-level-checks-en}

Beyond the point-wise CDF metrics, two additional protocols further sharpen the contrast between the SVGP and the MLP. On a condition-level P50 set containing 4,213 points, an oracle q1 directly fitted to the P50 observations achieved a lower bound RMSE of \SI{1.010}{mm} (undeployable); among the learning models, the SVGP led (RMSE \SI{2.649}{mm}), closely followed by the selected MLP (\(\SI{2.848}{mm}\pm\SI{0.059}{mm}\)), while the diagnostic KD variant oriented toward extrapolation regularization performed the worst (\(\SI{3.212}{mm}\pm\SI{0.245}{mm}\)). However, on a q1 extrapolated grid containing 21,527 points outside the observed P50 window, the ranking reversed: the diagnostic KD variant achieved the lowest RMSE (\(\SI{10.358}{mm}\pm\SI{0.106}{mm}\)) and the smallest positive extrapolation bias (\SI{+6.269}{mm}), leading the SVGP (\SI{10.591}{mm}, bias \SI{+6.410}{mm}) and the selected MLP (\(\SI{10.864}{mm}\pm\SI{0.064}{mm}\), bias \SI{+6.753}{mm}). These two MLP variants thus explore a genuine trade-off: the selected anchor-off model acts as a conservative in-distribution surrogate, while the diagnostic KD variant is retained as an extrapolation-regularized version, remaining closer to the SVGP-style smooth prior outside the observation window. \subsection{Leave-One-Nozzle-Out (LONO) Transfer Performance and Out-of-Distribution Failures}
\label{sec:lono-transfer-en}

\paragraph{Methodological note: dual-protocol evaluation.}
\label{sec:dual-protocol-en}
The two evaluation protocols used in this chapter—the LONO cross-validation reported below and the 5-seed production bootstrap reported later in Section~\ref{sec:kd-form-fix-en}—are intentionally distinguished to answer different questions. By holding out an entire nozzle during training, the LONO protocol measures the model's ability to generalize to nozzle geometries completely unseen during training, thus reporting the model's \textbf{Out-of-Domain (OOD)} behavior. The production protocol retrains the identical configuration across the full dataset using five random seeds and evaluates on a non-right-censored test set; it measures the model's \textbf{in-domain fit quality} and its sensitivity to random initialization, serving as an appropriate protocol for reporting variance in the core tables.

These two protocols are reported side-by-side rather than consolidated into a single ranking because each isolates independent claims. A model that wins under the LONO protocol but fails under the production bootstrap implies good generalization but poor in-domain fitting; conversely, a model that wins in the production bootstrap but fails in LONO implies a tight in-domain fit but poor performance when extrapolating to new nozzle geometries. The production protocol supports the MLP as a conservative screening surrogate within the measured design space; the LONO protocol demonstrates that the SVGP alternative backbone is stronger in point accuracy for transfer to unseen nozzles. These are complementary claims rather than a declaration of total victory for either architecture.

The Leave-One-Nozzle-Out (LONO) evaluation checks whether the model is merely interpolating within the nozzles seen during training. Locking the Stage~2 teacher to the $\mu$-anchor-only configuration and adopting the refreshed 5-fold LONO protocol (held-out nozzles Nozzle0, Nozzle1, Nozzle2, Nozzle4, Nozzle5), the winning Stage~3 MLP variant (mixed raw/KD supervision in the uncertain regime) yields a 5-fold average RMSE of \(\SI{12.55}{mm}\pm\SI{12.46}{mm}\), an MAE of \(\SI{9.80}{mm}\pm\SI{10.18}{mm}\), and an average 1$\sigma$/2$\sigma$ coverage of 0.575/0.770. The identical uncensored LONO evaluation measured the Stage~3 SVGP's RMSE at \(\SI{10.16}{mm}\pm\SI{9.57}{mm}\), MAE at \(\SI{7.87}{mm}\pm\SI{7.55}{mm}\), and coverage at 0.628/0.821. The conclusions under LONO are thus conditional: the SVGP is a stronger backbone for point transfer, while the MLP is retained based on its in-domain conservative coverage, injection onset auxiliary output, and ablation affordability (understood per the terminology conventions in the beginning of \S4).

The exceptionally high standard deviation is primarily driven by the Nozzle0 fold, which is the only factory-fresh (brand-new) nozzle in the entire campaign. Although Nozzle1 shares the same nominal geometry as Nozzle0, Nozzles 1--8 originate from different injectors and carry unmodeled injector-to-injector variability; this structural difference also explains why the control back pressure for Nozzle1--8 covers only the 4\,bar level (versus 1\,bar and 4\,bar for Nozzle0), and why the LONO protocol collapses on the Nozzle0 fold. Nozzle0 is therefore best understood as a cross-design-family extrapolation failure rather than a normal average performance fold: the Stage~3 MLP reaches an RMSE of \SI{34.76}{mm} on this fold, and the SVGP reaches \SI{26.99}{mm}, both exhibiting systematic underestimation. If Nozzle0 is excluded, the 4-fold average RMSE for the MLP drops to \(\SI{7.00}{mm}\pm\SI{1.18}{mm}\), while the SVGP achieves \(\SI{5.95}{mm}\pm\SI{1.98}{mm}\). At this point, the coverage comparison intertwines: the 1$\sigma$ coverage is virtually identical (MLP 0.696 vs. SVGP 0.697), whereas the MLP has a higher 2$\sigma$ coverage (0.923 vs. 0.890). Therefore, the LONO evidence refutes claims of comprehensive MLP superiority, prompting instead an evaluation that separates in-domain conservative deployment from cross-design-family transfer accuracy.

\subsubsection{Visual Evidence of Difficult Conditions and Failure Mode Anatomy: The Case of Nozzle 3}

The Leave-One-Nozzle-Out (LONO) evaluation indicates that the surrogate model experiences degraded coverage and amplified errors on specific nozzles such as Nozzle~3. To observe the morphology of these challenging conditions in the raw data, Phantom high-speed Mie scattering images were cross-referenced with the corresponding plume-by-plume penetration curves (Figure~\ref{fig:failure-mode-nozzle3}).

The image sequence ($t \approx 0.6 \sim 1.0\,\si{ms}$) reveals pronounced inter-hole asymmetry: while the left-side plumes have already established stable, continuous jets, the three plumes at the bottom right (approximately 3 to 5 o'clock positions) appear short and discontinuous. Some orifices initially squeeze out a small, low-momentum leading liquid mass detached from the main body, which rapidly decelerates under ambient aerodynamic resistance; this visual observation corresponds to the "flat-top" feature seen in the penetration curves (e.g., the green line for Plume~2, the blue line for Plume~0) during the same time window. Subsequently ($t > 1.0\,\si{ms}$), a main jet emerges in the same azimuth and rapidly overtakes the leading liquid mass, causing a step-like steep rise in the curve.

This trajectory morphology of "leading liquid mass + overtaking main jet" is consistent with wake-driven catch-up/overtaking mechanisms reported in split- or multiple-injection literature: an earlier-arriving liquid mass can alter the penetration of trailing masses through wake effects and axial aerodynamic momentum preconditioning. Because this experiment lacked simultaneous gas velocity measurements, internal flow visualization, or liquid mass identity tracking, this case can only be interpreted as image evidence "consistent with" such mechanisms; mechanical causes such as needle wobble or transient orifice throttling are equally plausible candidate explanations that current data cannot rule out.

What the data can directly support are modeling inferences. The reduced-order fitting target ($k_{1/4}t^{1/4}$) is monotonic and concave by construction, making it unable to envelope step-like trajectories where the second derivative abruptly changes. Imposing a smooth, monotonic prior on such trajectories distorts the target, which is an identifiable reason for the coverage degradation in Stage~2 and Stage~3 networks on affected nozzles. Modeling such extreme transient sprays, therefore, requires either a more flexible shape prior or explicitly conditioning on the true mechanism once diagnostic tools capable of resolving it are in place.

\subsection{Neural Surrogate Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ablation-study-en}

The preceding sections established cross-architecture results: the SVGP currently prevails in point accuracy (especially under the LONO transfer protocol), whereas the selected MLP serves as a more conservative in-domain screening surrogate. The subsequent evidence explains why the MLP tech stack is retained as an operational workflow: its training curriculum can be cheaply ablated, its injection onset auxiliary output supports GUI prototypes, and its regime-aware distillation makes the conservative screening behavior traceable rather than accidental.

\subsubsection{Stage-2 Anchor Schedule Ablation Experiment}
\label{sec:stage2-anchor-ablation-en}

Stage~2 introduces two optional early anchor penalty terms on top of the heteroscedastic NLL: a quadratic penalty that pulls \(\mu(t{\to}0)\to 0\) (weight \(\lambda_\mu\)) and a quadratic penalty that suppresses excess standard deviations beyond a fixed lower bound \(\sigma_{0}\) via \(\max(0,\,\sigma(t{\to}0)-\sigma_{0})\) (weight \(\lambda_\sigma\)). Both are integrated with a triangular kernel weighting \(w(t)=\max(0,\,1-t/\tau)\) acting over an early time window of \(\tau=\SI{0.15}{ms}\). To investigate whether these anchors help or harm cross-family generalization, three Stage~2 variants were compared under the same LONO protocol as Stage~1: (i)~no anchor (\(\lambda_\mu=\lambda_\sigma=0\)); (ii)~$\mu$-anchor only (\(\lambda_\mu=10^{-2}\), \(\lambda_\sigma=0\)); (iii)~$\mu$+$\sigma$ double anchor (\(\lambda_\mu=10^{-2}\), \(\lambda_\sigma=10^{-3}\)). The three variants shared the winning production 7-feature configuration from Stage~1 ($\alpha=0.5$ with pressure residual) as a warm start, the same 5-fold LONO split, the identical Stage~3 anchor-off distillation chain, the same network architecture and optimizer, and a single fixed seed (42); the only varied axis was the Stage~2 anchor configuration. The held-out set metrics in this section are evaluated on the untruncated CDF~P50 points of the held-out nozzles following the complete Stage~1$\to$2$\to$3 pipeline (evaluation point counts per fold are: Nozzle0 311,149, Nozzle2 60,369, Nozzle5 33,017, Nozzle4 28,139, Nozzle1 25,705).

This ablation experiment tests two coupled hypotheses. First, whether a mean anchor at time zero can inject useful physical priors without incurring conditional mean biases elsewhere; the comparison between no anchor and $\mu$-anchor-only isolates this trade-off. Second, whether forcefully suppressing early \(\sigma\) toward its lower bound harms uncertainty calibration on held-out nozzles; the comparison between $\mu$-anchor-only and the double $\mu$+$\sigma$ anchor strips this axis away from the confounding effects of the mean anchor.

Verification of both hypotheses points to supporting the \(\mu\)-anchor only configuration. Activating the \(\mu\)-anchor reduces the average RMSE by \SI{1.81}{mm} (from 14.54 down to \SI{12.73}{mm}), lowers MAE by \SI{0.94}{mm}, increases 2\(\sigma\) coverage from 0.763 to 0.775, and narrows the systematic underestimation bias from \SI{-8.50}{mm} to \SI{-7.11}{mm}. The improvements are concentrated on the Nozzle2 fold: without the anchor, the NLL is free to exploit the sparsely populated window near \(t{=}0\) as a slack space, causing the no-anchor variant to collapse to an RMSE of \SI{19.18}{mm} here; the anchor removes this degree of freedom. For the three nozzles from the same test-campaign series (Nozzle1/4/5, each with ~25k--33k evaluation points), the results across all three variants lie within \SI{1}{mm} of each other, demonstrating that the value of the anchor only surfaces when a LONO fold genuinely poses an OOD challenge.

Therefore, the Stage~3 ablation experiment reported in the next subsection fixes the Stage~2 teacher to the $\mu$-anchor-only configuration (\(\lambda_\mu=10^{-2}\), \(\lambda_\sigma=0\)); any conclusions regarding Stage~3 regime weighting or KD forms are predicated upon this anchor choice. \subsubsection{Fixing Systematic Under-Prediction Near the Field-of-View Boundary}
\label{sec:kd-form-fix-en}

Upon switching to the "amplitude scaled plus pressure residual" feature variant—which takes the log pressure residual directly as an input—slicing Stage~3 residuals by the near-FOV indicator \(\mathrm{pen}\ge 0.9\,\mathrm{cap}_\mathrm{nozzle}\) exposed a systematic performance degradation in the high-penetration range: although the Stage~2 teacher was virtually unbiased near the FOV cap (bias \SI{-0.59}{mm}, RMSE \SI{4.82}{mm}), the distilled anchor-off student deteriorated to a bias of \SI{-2.70}{mm} and an RMSE of \SI{6.11}{mm} on the same slice. This drift cannot be attributed to the Stage 1/2 fitting—the robust cleaning pipeline employed (Hampel filtering and monotonic difference lower-bound detection, detailed in \S3) did not induce a downward drift in the teacher near the cap—hence, it must originate entirely within the Stage~3 distillation step.

To pinpoint the mechanism, seven univariate ablations were run while keeping the aforementioned teacher fixed: deactivating the raw NLL, disabling the first/second-order shape penalties, forcing the confidence weight \(\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}}/\sigma_{\mathrm{teacher}}\) in the forward KL to 1, substituting the forward KL with the reverse KL, replacing the full KL with a $\mu$-only MSE, shortening the training to 10 epochs, and doubling the KD weight in both the uncertain and teacher-only regimes. Two variants stood out: the diagnostic variant disabling the raw NLL almost precisely replicated the teacher's near-cap behavior (bias \SI{-0.53}{mm}); the diagnostic variant replacing the full KL with a $\mu$-only MSE pulled the near-cap bias back to \SI{-0.91}{mm} and achieved the lowest full-domain RMSE among all variants. Both point to two shared causes: (i)~the raw NLL pulls the student \(\mu\) toward the lower-bound observations from the frame immediately succeeding FOV truncation; (ii)~the forward KL term \((\mu_t-\mu_s)^{2}/\sigma_s^{2}\) permits the student to evade matching the teacher \(\mu\) simply by inflating its own \(\sigma\). The remaining five variants left the near-cap bias trapped in the \([-2.7,-2.0]\) mm range, thus being ruled out as primary mechanisms.

The $\mu$-only MSE diagnostic variant removed this variance-inflation pathway but simultaneously lost the implicit supervision of the KL divergence on the student \(\sigma\), causing the \(\sigma\) calibration to drift within the KD-only intervals (the uncertain and teacher-only regimes). Consequently, a mixed distillation loss combining mean MSE and log-variance MSE is proposed:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KD}} \;=\; (\mu_s-\mu_t)^{2} \;+\; \lambda_\sigma\,\bigl(\log\sigma_s^{2} - \log\sigma_t^{2}\bigr)^{2},
\label{eq:kd-mse-mu-plus-sigma}
\end{equation}
which adds an explicit log-variance MSE on top of the mean MSE to properly re-supervise \(\sigma\), while simultaneously avoiding the variance inflation under teacher--student mean mismatch permitted by the KL formulation. Scanning across \(\lambda_\sigma\in\{0.5,1,2,5\}\), \(\lambda_\sigma=5.0\) was selected as the new Stage~3 default: under this setting, the near-cap RMSE and bias achieved a joint optimum (\SI{4.89}{mm} and \SI{-0.66}{mm}), becoming statistically indistinguishable from the teacher's \(\SI{-0.59}{mm}\); meanwhile, the point-wise \(|\sigma_s-\sigma_t|\) median on the teacher-only regime slice dropped to \SI{0.27}{mm}. Figure~\ref{fig:kd-overlay} overlays the bias--truth curves for the teacher, the baseline forward-KL student, and the new mixed-distillation student; the improvements are focused in the upper penetration regime, while the lower half remains undisturbed.

The discrepancy between \(-0.590\,\si{mm}\) and the teacher's \SI{-0.59}{mm} on the same slice is smaller than a single-seed standard deviation, verifying that the distillation-induced distortion is fully remedied and robust to random initialization.

\subsubsection{Methodological Addendum: Ablation Affordability as a Unique Advantage of the Compact Neural Architecture}
\label{sec:mlp-ablation-affordability-en}

A direct consequence of choosing the MLP as the penetration surrogate architecture is that the systematic ablations reported in this thesis are computationally affordable on conventional hardware. There are three independent ablation matrices in this work: Stage~1's 5-fold LONO across 9 engineered feature variants (Section~\ref{sec:stage1-lono-ablation}), Stage~2's LONO comparison across 3 anchor schedules under the same protocol, and Stage~3's combinatorial search over 4 regime weighting variants and 7 univariate KD loss formulation ablations. The complete LONO~$\times$~Ablation~$\times$~Seed grid required to back the core tables consumes roughly tens of GPU hours on a single consumer-grade accelerator.

The core argument lies not in the run time of a single training pass, but rather in the alignment between the task structure and the architectural inductive biases. The objective of this surrogate model is to regress $(\mu,\sigma)$ as point-wise functions of low-dimensional condition descriptors (time, scaled amplitude, log pressure residual, one-hot nozzle indicators), with inputs exhibiting neither regular spatial grids nor long-range sequence dependencies. The local translation-invariance bias that motivates CNN designs and the long-range attention bias driving Transformers are mismatched with such a point-wise low-dimensional regression task structure; they would inject unwarranted expressiveness and parameter footprints. Building upon this, the combinatorial scan of feature engineering, anchor schedules, regime weights, and KD formulations mandated by our ablation design—amounting to dozens of configurations~$\times$~5-fold LONO~$\times$~multiple seeds—requires independent full training for every variant. Introducing more complex spatio-temporal backbones would simultaneously inflate single-run costs and restrict the number of comparable configurations, ultimately meaning that within a Master's-level compute budget, one could only report a single configuration rather than disentangle the marginal contributions of various design choices. Thus, the architecture-task alignment and ablation affordability collectively establish the MLP as a principled choice, not a mere workaround adopted for convenience. Without this alignment, the systematic ablation evidence presented in Chapters~4 and 5 would remain inaccessible under a Master's-level compute budget. This qualitative conclusion does not hinge upon specific numerical assertions regarding CNN/Transformer training overhead, nor does this thesis conduct direct benchmarking of them.

\subsection{Optional Follow-Up: Residual Nozzle-Head Adaptation}
\label{sec:residual-family-head-en}

\emph{This section is an optional post-training follow-up; it explores extensibility and within-design-family adaptation, but it does not change the deployed-surrogate verdict of the preceding sections---the MLP/SVGP trade-off and the deployment choice stand. Its strongest result is in-distribution only and was not LONO-tested.} The screening surrogate established above conditions on injector through a one-hot injector indicator in its input feature set. Two questions the preceding sections leave open motivate this optional analysis. First, \textbf{deployment and extensibility}: a one-hot family input offers no clean structural answer for a genuinely new injector, requiring either a hard-coded fallback identity or a freshly retrained head. Second, \textbf{in-family adaptation}: can the same censoring-aware protocol be adapted within the supported injector design family without abandoning the deployed backbone? This section reports a self-contained follow-up evaluated on the identical conservative uncensored CDF point table (\num{542565} points; the protocol of Section~\ref{sec:evaluation-protocol-en}); it does \emph{not} revise the deployed-surrogate verdict established above.

The construction common to every variant is an \textbf{additive per-nozzle residual on a frozen, hot-started backbone}:
\begin{equation}
\mu(x,f) = \underbrace{\mu_{\mathrm{shared}}(h(x))}_{\text{frozen, hot-started}} + \underbrace{\Delta_f(h(x))}_{\text{trained, zero-initialised}}, \qquad \Delta_f \equiv 0 \ \text{at initialisation.}
\label{eq:residual-family-head-en}
\end{equation}
The shared backbone is copied from the deployed Stage~3 checkpoint and frozen; only small per-nozzle residual heads and the log-variance head are trained (\num{49923} trainable parameters against \num{351361} frozen backbone parameters). Because $\Delta_f$ is zero at initialisation, an unseen nozzle defaults to the shared model---a structural fallback with no hand-coded identity---and a shrinkage prior (an $L_2$ penalty on $\Delta_f$, weight $\lambda_\Delta$) trades per-nozzle fit against proximity to the shared map.

\subsubsection{Three Designs for Making the Model Aware of the Injector}
\label{sec:residual-family-designs-en}

Three ways to make the surrogate nozzle-aware were compared on the uncensored CDF table (Table~\ref{tab:residual-family-designs}). Feeding the nozzle index as a one-hot input to a single shared head---the deployed configuration---scores \SI{4.265}{mm}, but has no structural answer for an unseen injector. Splitting into independent per-nozzle decoders, the obvious extensible design, fragments the training data and degrades to \SI{5.010}{mm} while still lacking a fallback. The residual head of Equation~\eqref{eq:residual-family-head-en} recovers most of that gap, to \SI{4.631}{mm}, \emph{and} supplies the structural fallback: each family learns only a correction on top of the shared map. \subsubsection{Robustness: LONO Transfer and Dataset Sensitivity}
\label{sec:residual-robustness-en}

Two robustness checks frame the climb. \textbf{LONO transfer.} Re-running the leave-one-nozzle-out protocol of Section~\ref{sec:svgp-lono-comparison-en} with the residual conversion applied to the MLP arms---holding out the modified-family nozzles N1, N2, N4, N5, N6 one at a time, with N0 kept in training and each fold trained from scratch---shows the residual head also generalises to unseen \emph{in-family} nozzles. Across the four in-family folds (excluding N6), the deployed nozzle head averages $\SI{7.44}{mm}\pm\SI{1.46}{mm}$ RMSE; the residual head and residual-FiLM both drop to $\approx\SI{5.16}{mm}\pm\SI{0.65}{mm}$, a $\approx$31\% reduction with a much tighter spread (Table~\ref{tab:residual-lono}, Figure~\ref{fig:residual-lono}). The improvement is largely \textbf{bias repair}: the nozzle head's mean bias of $+\SI{5.07}{mm}$ collapses to $+\SI{0.78}{mm}$, with the largest per-fold gains on N5 ($-\SI{3.43}{mm}$) and N2 ($-\SI{3.37}{mm}$). Notably, the in-distribution edge of FiLM over the plain residual head (\num{4.510} vs \SI{4.631}{mm}) does \emph{not} transfer: under LONO the two arms are within \SI{0.01}{mm}, so the cheap output residual is as good as the representation adapter for unseen in-family nozzles. \textbf{Dataset sensitivity.} Re-evaluating the curated checkpoints on an explicit Level-3 QC-gated dataset and decomposing the change into a data-cleanup term and a model-retrain term shows the residual SVGP benefits most from QC gating: a $-\SI{0.067}{mm}$ cleanup plus a $-\SI{0.073}{mm}$ retrain gain compound to a $-\SI{0.140}{mm}$ net improvement (to \SI{3.922}{mm}), against near-flat retrain gains for the MLP arms---so the follow-up gain is not an artefact of the dataset choice.

\subsubsection{Where the Residual Adapter Fails: Two Cases at the Edge of Its Scope}
\label{sec:residual-boundary-en}

The residual win is bounded by injector design family, and two cases mark the edge. \textbf{Nozzle6} is a data-sparse (\num{20758}-point) fold on which every MLP arm collapses to \SIrange{12}{14}{mm}; the residual arms are $\approx\SI{2.3}{mm}$ \emph{worse} than the plain nozzle head there and systematically under-predict (bias $\approx-\SI{10.5}{mm}$)---over-specialisation rather than transfer. \textbf{Nozzle0} is the only first-generation injector (BC2022); even after momentum normalization its early-time penetration response is $\approx$2.5$\times$ that of the modified nozzles and never crosses below it (Figure~\ref{fig:injector-comparison}), so it occupies an early-time envelope the frozen trunk never represented (Figure~\ref{fig:injector-scatter} isolates this branch). Held out entirely, N0 is a genuine zero-shot case: the residual surrogate scores \SI{33.16}{mm} RMSE, consistent with the \SI{34.76}{mm} Nozzle0 LONO failure of the deployed MLP reported in Section~\ref{sec:svgp-lono-comparison-en}.

A few-shot study quantifies how far a cheap per-family correction can recover N0 (Figure~\ref{fig:n0-fewshot}). Freezing the entire N0-held-out model except the N0 residual head and fine-tuning only that head on $k$ N0 conditions, RMSE falls from \SI{33.16}{mm} ($k=0$) but plateaus at $\approx\SI{15.7}{mm}$ even with all 203 conditions---far above the $\approx\SI{5}{mm}$ the same architecture reaches on in-family LONO folds. Two effects bound it. First, the frozen log-variance head, badly mis-scaled on out-of-design-family N0 features, throttles the NLL fine-tune, which plateaus $\approx\SI{4}{mm}$ higher than an MSE fit that bypasses it; a deployable adapter must therefore also adapt the variance head. Second, even with the mean fit unthrottled, the delta head removes the global bias ($-27\to\approx0$ mm by $k=2$) but cannot close the residual spread---a per-family output offset cannot synthesise a trunk-level representation the backbone never learned. An out-of-design-family injector therefore needs trunk-level capacity (unfreezing, FiLM across all trunk blocks, or retraining), not merely a new output head.

In summary, this optional follow-up shows that additive residuals on a frozen backbone can improve adaptation within the injector design family. The residual multi-task SVGP gives the strongest in-distribution follow-up result on the primary uncensored CDF table (\SI{3.987}{mm} RMSE, \SI{2.08}{mm} P50, ECE $0.026$), below the thesis' strongest mainline predictor on both point error and calibration; the deployment and extensibility gap is closed structurally \emph{within the injector design family} by the zero-initialised residual and its automatic shared-model fallback; and the MLP's LONO weakness is partly repaired ($\approx$31\% in-family). The same fixed penetration prediction can be projected onto different downstream piston-bowl geometries and kinematics, since the screening interface deliberately decouples the penetration surrogate (Chapter~4) from the geometric projection layer. Holding the spray-side inputs fixed and only changing the piston-bowl design and crank kinematics shows the cumulative score is geometry- and kinematics-sensitive even though the underlying penetration prediction does not change. Figure~\ref{fig:impingement-design-comparison} contrasts two further cases against the Design~A baseline above: a wider bowl at the same \SI{250}{rpm} (Design~B) raises the cumulative piston-impact integral from $E=\SI{2.475}{ms}$ to $\SI{3.364}{ms}$, i.e.\ bowl shape alone shifts the screening verdict by $\approx 36\%$ at identical kinematics; a faster Hermite-profile piston at \SI{2250}{rpm} instead \emph{opens and re-closes} the impact window---the instantaneous piston-impact probability rises to a peak of $\approx\SI{59}{\percent}$ near $t\approx\SI{2.4}{ms}$ and then falls back below \SI{5}{\percent} by \SI{5}{ms} as the crown retracts, giving a much smaller cumulative exposure ($E=\SI{1.446}{ms}$) than either slower-piston case despite the higher instantaneous peak. This non-monotonic Hermite case is exactly the behavior the geometric coupling is designed to capture: the piston motion is taken into account, while the swirl it induces is deliberately left unmodeled (Section~\ref{sec:future-validation-pathways-en}).

The evidence boundaries are equally critical. The aforementioned interception scores should solely be interpreted as pipeline demonstrations and rank-oriented geometric surrogate metrics; they cannot be directly equated with the actual probabilities of harmful liquid-phase wall impingement occurring in a real engine. The validated contribution of this work consists of the penetration surrogate over its measured domain and its coupling with a probabilistic geometric screening interface, rather than a phase-resolved predictor of detrimental liquid impingement. All numerical claims reported in this thesis are traceable to the cleaned trajectory tables, saved checkpoints, and exported evaluation plots archived in the project repository.
