\section{Results}

\subsection{Evaluation map and headline results}

Chapter~4 defined the penetration surrogate as a censoring-aware low-dimensional probabilistic regression problem. The results are therefore organised as a set of linked evaluation views rather than as a single leaderboard. The full-clean CDF protocol tests the deployed pointwise surrogate on all cleaned held-out CDF points from Dataset~\#1. The uncensored CDF table is the primary censoring-controlled pointwise check from Dataset~\#2. The P50-observed and q1-extrapolated tables are condition-level checks that separate in-window representative behaviour from continuation behaviour. The leave-one-nozzle-family (LONO) protocol tests transfer to unseen nozzle families, and the final ablation block attributes the design choices that make the MLP useful for the downstream screening workflow.

The extraction and fitting workflow produced 295 cleaned fitted trajectory files across the processed campaign archive. This stage matters because the surrogate is not trained on raw, intermittently broken plume traces but on targets that retain the main temporal structure while damping segmentation noise and reducing late-time censoring bias. Figure~\ref{fig:clean-fit-example} shows a representative example: the cleaned curves preserve plume-to-plume differences, and the fitted trajectories provide smooth targets that remain faithful to the observed liquid envelope.

\subsection{In-domain CDF accuracy and uncertainty}

Table~\ref{tab:model-summary} summarises the role and held-out performance of the three learning stages. Stage~1 trains on one representative trajectory per operating condition and acts as a stable mean anchor; its test MAE of \SI{2.91}{mm} is measured on a corresponding representative test set. Stage~2 tackles a harder task --- the full filtered dataset spanning within-condition plume-to-plume variation with a learned variance output --- and its test MAE of \SI{5.63}{mm} is measured on that larger, more heterogeneous test population. The two figures are therefore not directly comparable: the higher Stage~2 MAE reflects the harder supervision target rather than degraded accuracy, and makes Stage~2 the appropriate uncertainty-aware teacher.

Under the refreshed full-clean CDF protocol with the production \texttt{kd\_mse\_mu\_plus\_sigma} Stage~3 loss form (\(\lambda_\sigma=5.0\)) trained over five seeds \(\{7,17,42,99,2024\}\), the anchor-off Stage~3 model reaches an overall RMSE of \(\SI{4.735}{mm}\pm\SI{0.037}{mm}\), MAE of \(\SI{3.293}{mm}\pm\SI{0.039}{mm}\), and a small positive bias of \(\SI{+0.626}{mm}\pm\SI{0.112}{mm}\) over each seed's 692{,}942 held-out time points from 24{,}316 trajectories. Figure~\ref{fig:pred-vs-actual-best} shows that the held-out predictions remain close to the identity line over the full measured range, with the scatter broadening gradually as penetration grows. That broadening is consistent with the heteroscedastic design: the model is not forcing one constant error bar onto all time instants and operating conditions.

The uncertainty calibration is also meaningful in engineering terms. The final 5-seed mean 1$\sigma$ and 2$\sigma$ coverages on the full-clean protocol are 86.7\% and 98.5\%, distinctly more conservative than the nominal Gaussian values of 68.3\% and 95.4\%. Figure~\ref{fig:stage3-calibration-coverage} expands this point through reliability plots and coverage curves binned by predicted standard deviation, rather than comparing only mean standard deviation with mean error. For early-design screening, this conservative bias is a feature rather than a defect: the model is deliberately more cautious than an exactly calibrated Gaussian and therefore less likely to understate late-time or weakly observed penetration uncertainty.

\subsection{P50/Q1 condition-level checks}

Two further protocols sharpen the SVGP-vs-MLP comparison beyond the pointwise CDF metric. On the 4{,}213-point condition-level P50 set, an oracle q1 fit to the P50 observations reaches RMSE \SI{1.010}{mm} as a non-deployable lower bound; among learned models the SVGP leads at RMSE \SI{2.649}{mm}, the production MLP follows at \(\SI{2.848}{mm}\pm\SI{0.059}{mm}\), and the diagnostic \(\mu\)-anchor/raw-uncertain variant is the worst at \(\SI{3.212}{mm}\pm\SI{0.245}{mm}\). On the 21{,}527-point q1 extrapolation grid outside the observed P50 window, however, the ranking inverts: the \(\mu\)-anchor variant attains the lowest RMSE (\(\SI{10.358}{mm}\pm\SI{0.106}{mm}\)) and the smallest positive extrapolation bias (\SI{+6.269}{mm}), ahead of the SVGP (\SI{10.591}{mm}, bias \SI{+6.410}{mm}) and the production MLP (\(\SI{10.864}{mm}\pm\SI{0.064}{mm}\), bias \SI{+6.753}{mm}). The two MLP variants therefore probe a genuine trade-off: the production anchor-off model is a conservative in-distribution surrogate, while the \(\mu\)-anchor/raw-uncertain model is retained as an extrapolation-regularised diagnostic that is closer to the SVGP-style smoothing prior outside the observed window. Detailed per-seed numbers and run roots are recorded in \codepath{Thesis/slides/slides\_production\_mlp\_svgp\_eval/}.

\subsection{Probability calibration}

Coverage checks only test a small number of confidence intervals, so this thesis further uses reliability diagrams, the continuous ranked probability score (CRPS), and probability-integral-transform histograms. The five-seed production anchor-off runs maintain a CRPS materially lower than the physical baselines, and the gain does not come from simply widening uncertainty intervals: it comes from a better joint trade-off between mean accuracy and uncertainty width.

Figure~\ref{fig:calibration-current} shows the reliability diagram and the CRPS--sharpness plane after adding the Stage~3 SVGP to the same full-clean diagnostic. The three-stage MLP (anchor-off) has 1$\sigma$/2$\sigma$ coverage above the nominal Gaussian values (0.867/0.985 over the five-seed mean) and is therefore intentionally conservative on the external-cleaning set. The refreshed Stage~3 comparison in Table~\ref{tab:external-clean-headline} shows that the SVGP is now the strongest point predictor (RMSE \SI{4.628}{mm}, 1$\sigma$/2$\sigma$ coverage 0.703/0.939) and also has the lowest CRPS in the refreshed calibration diagnostic, while the production MLP remains the more conservative screening surrogate (RMSE \SI{4.735}{mm}, but coverage well above nominal). The two models should therefore be read as different accuracy--coverage trade-offs rather than as a single-winner result: SVGP sits closer to nominal coverage, while MLP keeps wider uncertainty margins for downstream screening.

\subsection{LONO transfer and OOD failure}

The leave-one-nozzle-family evaluation checks whether the model is merely interpolating within nozzle families already seen during training. Under the refreshed five-fold LONO protocol (held-out families Nozzle0, Nozzle1, Nozzle2, Nozzle4, Nozzle5) with the Stage~2 teacher locked to \texttt{mu\_anchor}, the LONO-winning Stage~3 MLP variant \texttt{raw\_uncertain\_raw05\_kd025} attains a five-fold mean RMSE of \(\SI{12.55}{mm}\pm\SI{12.46}{mm}\), MAE of \(\SI{9.80}{mm}\pm\SI{10.18}{mm}\), and mean 1$\sigma$/2$\sigma$ coverage of 0.575/0.770. The same uncensored LONO evaluation gives the Stage~3 SVGP RMSE \(\SI{10.16}{mm}\pm\SI{9.57}{mm}\), MAE \(\SI{7.87}{mm}\pm\SI{7.55}{mm}\), and coverage 0.628/0.821. The cross-family conclusion is therefore conditional: SVGP is the stronger point-transfer backbone, while the MLP is retained for its conservative in-domain screening behaviour, onset side output, and inexpensive ablation workflow.

The large standard deviations are dominated by the Nozzle0 fold, which is the only first-generation injector geometry in the campaign. Nozzle0 is better interpreted as an out-of-design-family failure case than as an ordinary average-performance fold: the Stage~3 MLP reaches RMSE \(\SI{34.76}{mm}\) there and the SVGP reaches RMSE \(\SI{26.99}{mm}\), both with systematic under-prediction. Excluding Nozzle0, the MLP four-fold mean RMSE drops to \(\SI{7.00}{mm}\pm\SI{1.18}{mm}\), while the SVGP reaches \(\SI{5.95}{mm}\pm\SI{1.98}{mm}\). Coverage then becomes mixed rather than one-sided: the MLP and SVGP have nearly identical 1$\sigma$ coverage (0.696 vs.\ 0.697), while the MLP has higher 2$\sigma$ coverage (0.923 vs.\ 0.890). The LONO evidence therefore argues against claiming universal MLP dominance, and instead separates in-domain conservative deployment from nozzle-family transfer accuracy.

\subsubsection{Methodological note: dual-protocol evaluation}
\label{sec:dual-protocol-en}

The two evaluation protocols used in this chapter --- the LONO cross-validation reported above and the five-seed production bootstrap reported in Section~\ref{sec:kd-form-fix} --- are deliberately distinct and answer different questions. The LONO protocol holds out an entire nozzle family during training; it measures whether the surrogate generalises to a nozzle geometry never seen during training and therefore reports the model's \emph{out-of-distribution} behaviour. The production protocol retrains the same configuration under five random seeds on the full dataset and evaluates on a non-right-censored test set; it measures the model's \emph{in-distribution} fit quality together with its sensitivity to random initialisation, and is the appropriate protocol for variance reporting in the headline table.

The two protocols are reported in parallel rather than as a single ranking because each isolates a separate claim. A model that performs best under LONO but loses under the production bootstrap would generalise well but fit poorly within the supported domain; a model that performs best under the production bootstrap but loses under LONO would fit the training domain tightly but extrapolate poorly to new nozzle geometries. The production protocol supports the MLP as a conservative screening surrogate within the measured design space. The LONO protocol shows that the SVGP alternative backbone is stronger in nozzle-family transfer point accuracy. These are complementary claims rather than a single architecture-wide victory statement.

\subsubsection{Visual evidence and failure mode analysis of hard cases: Nozzle 3 example}

The leave-one-nozzle-family evaluation shows under-coverage and amplified errors on specific nozzle families such as Nozzle~3. To examine what these hard cases look like in the raw data, the Phantom high-speed Mie scattering images are cross-referenced with the corresponding per-plume penetration trajectories (Figure~\ref{fig:failure-mode-nozzle3}).

The image sequence ($t \approx 0.6 \sim 1.0\,\si{ms}$) shows pronounced hole-to-hole asymmetry: while the plumes on the left establish stable continuous jets, the three plumes on the bottom right (roughly 3--5 o'clock) appear short and fragmented. Some of these holes initially emit detached, low-momentum liquid packets that decelerate quickly against the ambient gas. This visual observation matches the flat ``plateau'' feature seen in the penetration curves (for example the green curve for Plume~2 and the blue curve for Plume~0) within the same time window. Subsequently ($t > 1.0\,\si{ms}$), a main jet appears at the same azimuthal positions and rapidly overtakes the initial packet, producing a step-like surge in the penetration curve.

The shape of the trace --- a slow leading packet followed by a faster main jet that catches up and overtakes it --- is consistent with the wake-driven catch-up and overtake mechanism reported in split- and multiple-injection studies, in which a preceding liquid packet can modify the penetration of a following packet through wake effects and axial gas-momentum preconditioning. Because the present experiments do not include simultaneous gas-velocity measurements, internal-flow visualisation, or packet-identity tracking, the case can only be interpreted as image evidence \emph{consistent with} such a mechanism; mechanical causes such as needle wobble or transient hole throttling are equally plausible candidates that the available data cannot adjudicate.

What the data \emph{do} support is the modelling consequence. The reduced-order fit target ($k_{1/4}t^{1/4}$) is monotone and concave by construction, so it cannot envelope a step trajectory with a second-derivative discontinuity of this kind. Imposing the smooth monotone prior on such traces distorts the target, and this distortion is one identifiable contributor to the degraded coverage of Stage~2 and Stage~3 on the affected families. Modelling extreme transient sprays of this kind will therefore require either a more flexible shape prior or explicit conditioning on whichever physical mechanism turns out to be responsible, once a diagnostic that can resolve it becomes available.

\subsection{MLP ablation evidence and screening demonstration}

The previous sections establish the cross-architecture result: SVGP is currently the point-accuracy winner, especially under LONO transfer, while the production MLP is the more conservative in-domain screening surrogate. The remaining evidence explains why the MLP stack is still kept as the operational workflow: its curriculum can be ablated cheaply, its onset side output supports the GUI prototype, and its regime-aware distillation makes the conservative screening behaviour traceable rather than accidental.

\subsubsection{Stage-2 anchor-schedule ablation}
\label{sec:stage2-anchor-ablation-en}

Stage~2 introduces two optional early-time anchor terms on top of the heteroscedastic NLL: a quadratic penalty pulling \(\mu(t{\to}0)\to 0\) (weight \(\lambda_\mu\)) and a quadratic penalty on the excess standard deviation \(\max(0,\,\sigma(t{\to}0)-\sigma_{0})\) above a fixed floor \(\sigma_{0}\) (weight \(\lambda_\sigma\)). Both terms integrate against a triangular kernel \(w(t)=\max(0,\,1-t/\tau)\) over an early-time window of \(\tau=\SI{0.15}{ms}\). To check whether these anchors help or hurt cross-nozzle generalisation, three Stage~2 variants are compared under the same LONO protocol as Stage~1: (i)~\texttt{no\_anchor} (\(\lambda_\mu=\lambda_\sigma=0\)); (ii)~\texttt{mu\_anchor} (\(\lambda_\mu=10^{-2}\), \(\lambda_\sigma=0\)); (iii)~\texttt{mu\_sigma\_anchor} (\(\lambda_\mu=10^{-2}\), \(\lambda_\sigma=10^{-3}\)). All three variants share the Stage~1 winner (\texttt{a\_dp050\_plus\_pressures}) as warm start, the same five-fold LONO split, the same Stage~3 anchor-off distillation chain, the same architecture, optimiser, and a single fixed seed (42); the only varying axis is the Stage~2 anchor configuration. Held-out metrics in this section are evaluated on the held-out nozzle's uncensored CDF~P50 points after the full Stage~1$\to$Stage~2$\to$Stage~3 chain (eval-point counts per fold: 311{,}149 / 60{,}369 / 33{,}017 / 28{,}139 / 25{,}705 for Nozzle0 / Nozzle2 / Nozzle5 / Nozzle4 / Nozzle1, respectively).

The ablation tests two coupled hypotheses. First, whether the zero-time mean anchor injects a usable physical prior at the cost of biasing the conditional mean elsewhere; the comparison \texttt{no\_anchor} vs.\ \texttt{mu\_anchor} isolates this trade-off. Second, whether forcing the early-time \(\sigma\) toward the floor damages calibration on held-out nozzle families; the comparison \texttt{mu\_anchor} vs.\ \texttt{mu\_sigma\_anchor} isolates this second axis without confounding by mean-anchor presence.

Both hypotheses are resolved in favour of the \(\mu\)-only anchor. Activating the \(\mu\)-anchor reduces mean RMSE by \SI{1.81}{mm} (\(14.54\to12.73\,\si{mm}\)) and MAE by \SI{0.94}{mm}, while raising 2\(\sigma\) coverage from 0.763 to 0.775 and shrinking the systematic under-prediction bias from \SI{-8.50}{mm} to \SI{-7.11}{mm}. The improvement is concentrated on the Nozzle2 fold, where \texttt{no\_anchor} collapses to \SI{19.18}{mm} RMSE because the NLL is free to exploit the data-poor near-\(t{=}0\) window as a slack pool; the anchor removes this degree of freedom. The three modern-design folds (Nozzle1/4/5, $\sim$25--33k eval points each) all sit within \SI{1}{mm} of one another across the three variants, so the anchor's value materialises only when the LONO fold creates a genuine OOD challenge.

Adding the \(\sigma\)-anchor on top tightens the mean predicted standard deviation from \SI{5.68}{mm} to \SI{5.49}{mm} but produces no compensating gain: RMSE rises to \SI{13.05}{mm} and 2\(\sigma\) coverage drops back to 0.764. The interpretation is that pushing \(\sigma\) toward its floor near \(t{=}0\) propagates through Stage~3 distillation as a more confident but slightly biased \(\mu\) prediction, which hurts both accuracy and calibration. The Stage-3 ablations reported in the next subsection therefore lock the Stage-2 teacher to the \texttt{mu\_anchor} configuration (\(\lambda_\mu=10^{-2}\), \(\lambda_\sigma=0\)); any conclusion about Stage~3 regime weighting or KD form is conditional on this anchor choice. The full per-fold breakdown and raw aggregate files are available under \codepath{MLP/runs\_mlp/stage2\_lono\_ablation\_20260520\_235200/}.

\subsubsection{Distillation loss form: diagnosis and near-FOV bias fix}
\label{sec:kd-form-fix}

After switching to the \texttt{a\_plus\_pressures} feature variant, slicing the Stage~3 residuals by the near-FOV indicator \(\mathrm{pen}\ge 0.9\,\mathrm{cap}_\mathrm{nozzle}\) exposed a systematic degradation in the upper-penetration regime: even though the Stage~2 teacher was nearly unbiased near the FOV cap (bias \SI{-0.59}{mm}, RMSE \SI{4.82}{mm}), the distilled anchor-off student deteriorated on the same slice to a bias of \SI{-2.70}{mm} and an RMSE of \SI{6.11}{mm}. This drift could not be attributed to the Stage~1/2 fits---the Hampel + DIFF\_LOWER cleaning pipeline does not undershoot the teacher near the cap---so the loss must have been introduced inside the Stage~3 distillation step itself.

To localise the mechanism we ran seven single-variable ablations on top of the unchanged \texttt{a\_plus\_pressures} teacher: turning off raw NLL, turning off the first/second-order shape penalties, forcing the forward-KL confidence weight \(\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}}/\sigma_{\mathrm{teacher}}\) to unity, swapping forward KL for reverse KL, replacing the full KL with an MSE on \(\mu\) only, shortening training to 10 epochs, and doubling the KD weight in the \texttt{raw\_uncertain} and \texttt{teacher\_only} regimes. Two variants stood out: \texttt{diag\_raw\_off} reproduced the teacher's near-cap behaviour almost exactly (bias \SI{-0.53}{mm}), and \texttt{diag\_kd\_mse\_mu} pulled the near-cap bias back to \SI{-0.91}{mm} and attained the lowest overall RMSE among all variants. Together they point at two causes: (i) the raw NLL drags the student \(\mu\) toward DIFF\_LOWER-truncated observations sitting one frame below the FOV plateau, and (ii) the forward-KL term \((\mu_t-\mu_s)^{2}/\sigma_s^{2}\) lets the student escape matching the teacher \(\mu\) by simply inflating its own \(\sigma\). The other five variants left the near-cap bias in the \([-2.7,-2.0]\) mm range, ruling them out as the dominant mechanism.

\texttt{diag\_kd\_mse\_mu} alone severs the \(\sigma\)-arbitrage path but also loses the KL's implicit supervision on student \(\sigma\), leaving the KD-only regions (\texttt{raw\_uncertain} and \texttt{teacher\_only}) with drifting \(\sigma\) calibration. We therefore propose the hybrid \texttt{kd\_mse\_mu\_plus\_sigma} loss
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KD}} \;=\; (\mu_s-\mu_t)^{2} \;+\; \lambda_\sigma\,\bigl(\log\sigma_s^{2} - \log\sigma_t^{2}\bigr)^{2},
\label{eq:kd-mse-mu-plus-sigma}
\end{equation}
which adds an explicit log-variance MSE on top of the mean MSE so that \(\sigma\) is supervised again, but without reintroducing the \(\sigma\)-arbitrage that the KL form allowed. A single-variable sweep over \(\lambda_\sigma\in\{0.5,1,2,5\}\) selected \(\lambda_\sigma=5.0\) as the new Stage~3 default: near-cap RMSE and bias reached their joint best (\SI{4.89}{mm} and \SI{-0.66}{mm}), statistically indistinguishable from the teacher's \(\SI{-0.59}{mm}\), and the median pointwise \(|\sigma_s-\sigma_t|\) on the \texttt{teacher\_only} slice dropped to \SI{0.27}{mm}. Figure~\ref{fig:kd-overlay} overlays the bias-versus-truth curves for the teacher, the baseline forward-KL student, and the new \texttt{kd\_mse\_mu\_plus\_sigma} student; the gain is concentrated in the upper half of the penetration range while the lower half is left intact.

\subsubsection{Methodological note: ablation affordability as an MLP-specific advantage}
\label{sec:mlp-ablation-affordability-en}

A direct consequence of choosing an MLP architecture for the penetration surrogate is that the systematic ablation suite reported in this thesis is computationally tractable on commodity hardware. Three independent ablation matrices appear in earlier sections: Stage~1 over nine engineered-feature variants under five LONO folds (Section~\ref{sec:stage1-lono-ablation}), Stage~2 over three anchor schedules under the same LONO protocol, and Stage~3 over four regime-weighting variants combined with seven single-variable KD-form ablations. The full LONO~$\times$~ablation~$\times$~seed grid required to support the headline tables completes in tens of GPU-hours on a single consumer-grade accelerator. The same ablation matrix applied to a CNN- or Transformer-style spatio-temporal surrogate would scale by one to two orders of magnitude in training cost, and a master-level computational budget would in practice force the author to report a single configuration without isolating the marginal contribution of each design choice. Reporting design-decision attribution at this granularity is therefore not an architecture-independent capability but a direct dividend of choosing a lightweight backbone.

Computational affordability is not the only justification for the MLP choice. The surrogate target in this thesis is a pointwise regression of $(\mu,\sigma)$ as a function of low-dimensional condition descriptors (time, scaled amplitude, log-pressure residuals, and the nozzle-family one-hot indicator), not a spatio-temporal field prediction. The inductive biases that motivate CNNs (local translation invariance over a regular spatial grid) and Transformers (long-range sequence modelling) are therefore not aligned with the task structure; both add representational capacity that the task does not require and pay for it in calibration noise and parameter count. Taken together, architecture--task matching and ablation affordability make the MLP the principled rather than the merely convenient choice, and the systematic ablation evidence presented in Chapters~4 and~5 would not have been reachable under a master-level computational budget without that match.

\subsubsection{Prototype screening output and evidence boundary}

The evidence boundary is equally important. The reported collision probabilities should be read only as a pipeline demonstration and ranking-oriented proxy, not as direct probabilities of harmful liquid-wall contact in a real engine. The validated contribution in this thesis is the penetration surrogate within its measured domain and its coupling interface to a probabilistic geometry screen, not a phase-resolved predictor of harmful liquid-wall contact. The numerical claims reported here are traceable to cleaned trajectory tables, stored checkpoints, and exported evaluation figures included in the project archive.
