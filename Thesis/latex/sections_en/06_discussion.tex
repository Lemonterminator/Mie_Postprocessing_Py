\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Positioning of the Stage-3 Surrogate as a Screening Model}

The synthetic dataset is generated by the reduced-order \(q_1\) fitting in Section~4.1: raw CDF trajectories are first subjected to cleansing and temporal alignment, then fitted to the \(q_1\) form, providing smooth extrapolation targets within the specified time window.

Stage~1 extracts stable representative mean trajectories condition by condition from the complete synthetic dataset, validating the $A$-scaling hypothesis.
However, because only one representative trajectory is retained per operating condition, utilizing Stage~1 in isolation neglects plume-to-plume and shot-to-shot variability, rendering it unsuitable as a definitive screening model.

Stage~2 extends the supervision to the entire synthetic dataset, concurrently learning the conditional mean and conditional dispersion within the extrapolation window via two output heads.
This also explains its slightly higher MAE compared to Stage~1: Stage~2 faces a harder supervision problem that more closely matches the final screening requirement.

Stage~3 combines the advantages of the preceding two stages.
During the early temporal periods where raw observations are relatively dense, it learns directly from the measured CDF data;
in the late temporal periods where observations become sparse as time progresses, it aligns with the conditional mean and variance furnished by the Stage~2 teacher according to the right-censoring rationale established in Chapter~2, utilizing a mixed distillation loss comprising mean MSE and log-variance MSE (see Section~\ref{sec:kd-form-fix-en}).
Given that both teacher and student output unimodal Gaussian distributions, this distillation loss possesses a closed-form solution, eliminating the need for Monte Carlo sampling and avoiding the introduction of additional sampling noise.
This hybrid supervision explains the early-stage fidelity and the more conservative late-stage behavior of Stage~3.

\subsection{Validated Findings and Boundaries of Applicability}

Within the scope articulated in this thesis, four conclusions are substantiated by the results.
First, the optical workflow is capable of extracting repeatable penetration targets from multi-hole Mie videos at an archival scale, turning raw videos, configuration files, and metadata into a reproducible chain of CSV/JSON tables, checkpoints, fitted trajectories, saved models, loss curves, and screening visualizations.
Second, the reduced-order fitting concurrently provides trajectory smoothing and target construction oriented towards censored observations.
Third, the staged surrogate advances from representative mean prediction to uncertainty-aware full-dataset prediction, ultimately attaining a screening performance refined by the raw CDF.
Fourth, the final uncertainty intervals are sufficiently conservative to support cautious early-stage engineering ranking, rather than mere point prediction.

The industrial value proposition should therefore be stated narrowly.
The workflow is a low-cost, auditable, uncertainty-aware pre-screening layer for pilot-spray geometric risk: it can reuse existing optical archives, reduce the number of nozzle and operating-condition candidates forwarded to CFD or engine tests, and prioritize cases whose Mie-defined liquid envelope approaches representative walls.
It does not quantify return on investment by itself, because the current work does not measure downstream reductions in test-cell hours, CFD runs, prototype hardware, or redesign cycles.
Those quantities are deployment metrics to be collected once the screening layer is embedded in an industrial decision process.

This thesis explicitly delineates the applicability domain of the conclusions.
The extracted spray boundaries are interpreted as liquid-phase envelope observables defined by Mie imaging under a specific optical protocol, rather than the complete in-cylinder liquid-phase ground truth \cite{ecn_liquid_penetration,ecn_liquid_penetration_accessory}.
The model is primarily tailored for interpolative prediction within the training operating condition domain, as opposed to unconstrained extrapolation;
moreover, it is not intended to supplant CFD, engine testing, or validated physical models of spray-wall impingement, but rather acts as an auditable early-stage engineering screening tool to narrow the design space before initiating higher-fidelity validation.

The target definition itself inherently harbors limitations.
The reduced-order fitting converges to an extension dominated by the quarter-power branch, and the neural surrogate is constrained to be monotonically increasing via regularization.
These shape priors facilitate the acquisition of intuitively plausible trajectory growth and mend short-term extrapolations at the tail end of the observation window, yet they do not simulate the imaging mechanism of Mie scattering.
This thesis further hypothesizes that, in room-temperature, non-reacting Mie experiments, the late-stage attenuation of the image signal primarily originates from droplet breakup, spatial diffusion, localized reduction in droplet number density, scattering intensity dropping below the detection threshold, and detection side-effects induced by the brightness multiplier mask, rather than the rapid evaporative disappearance of the fuel within a millisecond-scale window.
Consequently, even if the plume leading edge progressively becomes invisible before reaching the boundary of the observation window, this thesis still interprets it as a decline in the visibility of the liquid-phase geometric envelope, rather than the physical termination of droplet motion.
This interpretation accords with the physical intuition of relatively slow diesel evaporation at room temperature, though this thesis lacks synchronous measurements of droplet size, mass flux, or evaporation rate to validate it directly.
Thus, the derived penetration should be construed as the continuation of the Mie-defined geometric envelope, rather than direct evidence of a robust liquid-phase spray tip, localized liquid mass, droplet size, phase state, or the potential for wall film formation \cite{ecn_liquid_penetration,ecn_liquid_penetration_accessory}.

This extrapolation hypothesis still lacks direct comparative validation.
The originally planned validation pathway was to utilize wide-field single-plume Mie videos with an approximately twofold field of view, identical nozzle types, and similar operating conditions provided by an industrial collaborator, to comprehensively observe the penetration trajectory until the plateau region;
subsequently, the field of view would be artificially truncated, fitting the \(q_1\) model solely with the pre-truncation time series, and comparing the extrapolated curve pointwise with the full measured trajectory that remains visible post-truncation.
This experiment would directly scrutinize the core hypothesis of the knowledge distillation (KD) chain in this thesis: that the parametric form \(\sigma\!\left((t-t_0)/s\right)\cdot k_{1/4}\,t^{1/4}\) remains a credible extension beyond the boundary of the field of view, rather than a deviation primarily governed by shape priors.
Due to the collaborator's inability to provide such single-plume data, this validation could not be completed within the scope of this thesis; therefore, the reliable time range for extrapolation using the \(q_1\) hypothesis remains an open empirical question.

The aforementioned boundaries are critical for downstream wall-impingement screening.
The interaction between the spray and the wall is not invariably deleterious: if the majority of the liquid phase has evaporated prior to reaching the wall, the residual wall interaction might be less detrimental, and under certain conditions, it might even facilitate entrainment and mixing.
What truly warrants prioritized attention is direct liquid droplet impingement and continuous wall film formation \cite{MorieiraMotaPanao2010,liu2024marine_impingement,peraza2022swi,ahmad2025fueldilution}.
From this perspective, the present room-temperature, non-reacting, non-evaporating Mie dataset can be interpreted as a conservative stress case for liquid-envelope persistence: if a candidate spray remains well separated from representative metal surfaces under these cold optical conditions, faster evaporation in an engine-representative hot environment would generally strengthen, rather than weaken, the early low-risk indication.
This argument is useful for upstream screening, but it is not a proof of engine safety.
Real in-cylinder operation also introduces moving boundaries, pressure waves, squish, swirl, crossflow, temperature gradients, and spray deflection; these mechanisms can change the relative path between the liquid envelope and nearby metal surfaces even while evaporation shortens the liquid lifetime.
Accordingly, the model in this thesis should be more accurately interpreted as a conservative screening surrogate for the probable proximity of the Mie-defined spray envelope to the wall, rather than a phase-resolved predictor of harmful liquid-state impingement.
The correspondence between the liquid-phase envelope geometry and temperature, evaporation, local droplet mass flux, and wall film formation has not been modeled or validated in this thesis;
an elevated wall-interaction score merely denotes a higher risk of geometric proximity, and cannot be directly equated with harmful liquid impingement or the quantity of wall wetting.
Likewise, conservative coverage of penetration uncertainty is not identical to false-negative safety: a ranking tool can be cautious in its geometric envelope while still missing harmful wall wetting if evaporation, droplet inertia, cone-angle evolution, or in-cylinder flow changes the actual liquid mass reaching the surface.

The downstream engine geometric coupling is similarly a simplified model.
The current prototype exclusively translates the piston wall mask utilizing slider-crank kinematics, without back-incorporating time-varying chamber pressure, pressure wave dynamics, or large-scale bulk gas motion into the penetration prediction.
This simplification is inherited from the OSCC measurement itself, as every injection event occurs in a rigid chamber with a fixed back pressure;
consequently, what the surrogate learns is the penetration under quasi-static chamber conditions, rather than the fully coupled in-cylinder transient state.
Considering the sensitivity of diesel spray penetration to ambient gas density, this decoupling should be apprehended as a pragmatic modeling choice, rather than a proof that pressure and gas flow effects are negligible \cite{NaberSiebers1996}.
In the target scenario of this thesis, namely short pilot injection windows near top dead center on a millisecond timescale, this approximation remains acceptable as a first-order engineering screen: under the current toy piston setup, the crown displacement is less than \SI{1}{mm} within a \SI{5}{ms} window, and the present requirement is geometric ranking, not crank-angle-resolved in-cylinder ground truth prediction.
When the engine speed increases, the injection timing deviates further from top dead center, or the in-cylinder transient pressure and gas velocity significantly alter the ambient conditions perceived by the spray, the current wall-interaction score can only be construed as a decoupled screening proxy.

Another pertinent limitation relates to the spray cone angle.
Although a cone-angle proxy was extracted during the image processing phase, this thesis neither systematically analyzed it across different campaigns, nor elevated it to a learning target for the final surrogate.
Therefore, the half-cone angle \(\alpha\) in the downstream wall-interaction model is a fixed input, rather than a validated, time-varying observable.
Existing spray literature indicates that the spreading angle varies with transient development, ambient gas density, injection conditions, and nozzle geometry \cite{NaberSiebers1996,JungManinSkeenPickett2015}.
The current geometric screening solely learns the uncertainty in the axial penetration direction; the transverse envelope determined by \(\alpha\) remains postulated rather than predicted.

Finally, the results also prescribe the following specific boundaries, grouped below by the type of constraint they impose.

\subsubsection*{Generalization and Out-of-Distribution Bounds}
\begin{itemize}
	\item The surrogate is strictly trained within the operating condition domain jointly supported by experimental and synthetic data.
	      Under genuinely out-of-distribution pressures, nozzle geometries, ambient temperatures, or injection strategies, its predictions should not be accorded equivalent credibility.

	\item The leave-one-nozzle-family-out evaluation is dominated by the Nozzle0 fold---the sole factory-fresh reference nozzle, with injector-to-injector variation not modeled---on which every Stage~3 variant collapses (RMSE \(\sim\SI{35}{mm}\), systemic underestimation; per-fold breakdown in Figure~\ref{fig:svgp-lono-comparison}); the five-fold mean RMSE of \SI{12.55}{mm} is therefore a worst-case figure carried by this one out-of-design-family fold.
	      With that fold excluded, the in-family four-fold mean (\SIrange{7.0}{7.2}{mm}) falls below both physical baselines under the identical protocol (Chapter~5). The interpretive point is that the model's cross-family risk is localized to genuinely off-design geometry, not to in-family penetration prediction.
	      The image evidence of Nozzle~3 (Figure~\ref{fig:failure-mode-nozzle3}) further correlates the residual errors within the design family to leading liquid slugs, chasing and overtaking main jets, inter-hole asymmetry, and step-wise penetration. This trajectory morphology is consistent with the wake-driven chasing/overtaking mechanism reported in the split/multiple-injection literature.
	      However, as this thesis possesses neither synchronous gas velocity measurements nor internal flow visualization or liquid slug identity tracking, it can only interpret Nozzle~3 as image evidence consistent with this mechanism; mechanical causes such as needle wobble or transient nozzle hole throttling remain equally viable candidate explanations that the data cannot exclude.
	      The principal cross-family risk of the current model is the inadequate generalization to geometries genuinely outside the design family (Nozzle0), rather than a holistic failure to support average penetration prediction within the design family.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Statistical and Sample-Independence Constraints}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Seed-to-seed RMSE is stable across the five anchor-off repeated training runs (std \SI{0.040}{mm}, Chapter~5), so the headline mean is not a single-seed artefact.
	      It is still not a full statistical certification, however: bootstrap confidence bands have not yet been reported.

	\item The held-out RMSE and MAE are computed based on 542,565 point-wise samples, but the temporal point errors within the same trajectory are mutually correlated.
	      Thus, the number of independent samples is significantly smaller than the number of point-wise samples, and any confidence statements based on point-wise counts should be understood in conjunction with this correlation.
	      Condition-level confidence intervals, preferably clustered by recording, condition, and nozzle family, are still required before the tool can support formal acceptance thresholds.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Comparative-Baseline Boundaries}
\begin{itemize}
	\item The staged surrogate has been juxtaposed with the calibrated Hiroyasu--Arai, the delayed Naber--Siebers, the SVGP baseline, and its own Stage~3 ablations under the same external cleansing protocol.
	      These comparisons encompass the primary disparities between physical correlations, non-parametric probabilistic regression, and the supervision strategy of this thesis.
	      Nonetheless, lightweight tabular regressors such as random forest, gradient boosting, or quantile boosting have not been systematically incorporated;
	      hence, this thesis does not claim that the neural network architecture possesses optimal marginal utility relative to all deterministic machine learning alternative models.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Structural Trade-off Between the Staged MLP and SVGP Baselines}

The comparison between MLP and SVGP in Chapter~5 supports a structural rather than a unilateral interpretation, and the structure reads most clearly backwards from the deployment decision. The MLP is retained as the screening backbone not because it is the stronger regressor---it is not---but because of three components the kernel baselines do not provide: the onset-CDF output head required for the probabilistic screening interface of Section~4.7; the regime-aware raw/KD distillation chain, which lets one network combine censoring-aware synthetic targets with right-censored raw observations within their respective reliable intervals; and the lightweight backbone that makes the design-attribution ablations of Sections~4.4/4.7 affordable (Section~\ref{sec:mlp-ablation-affordability-en}).

The price for these structural affordances is point accuracy. On the in-distribution uncensored CDF protocol the SVGP is the stronger point predictor (RMSE \SI{4.193}{mm} vs.\ \SI{4.265}{mm}); the one axis on which the MLP leads is uncertainty coverage (1$\sigma$/2$\sigma$ \(0.887/0.989\) vs.\ \(0.716/0.945\)). Under leave-one-nozzle-family-out transfer the SVGP's point-error advantage widens and it degrades more gracefully on the worst fold (Chapter~5, Section~\ref{sec:svgp-lono-comparison-en}). Crucially, once the off-design Nozzle0 fold is excluded the coverage gap also closes, so the MLP's coverage advantage is an in-distribution effect, not a general property.

This pattern is precisely the anticipated manifestation of the separation of inductive biases discussed in Section~\ref{sec:kernel-vs-nn-ood-en}: the SVGP kernel reverts to the prior outside the training support, whereas the staged MLP carries the structure of the training distribution---including the beneficial \(\mu\)-anchor---to the held-out folds. The staged surrogate should therefore be read as a regressor-agnostic instantiation of the censoring-aware learning protocol: the same protocol can host a kernel mean function and would likely gain out-of-distribution accuracy from it; the MLP was chosen for the structural extensions it supports and the ablation affordability it brings, not for an absolute architectural superiority.

These structural commitments also dictate how the frozen screening backbone is adapted at deployment. Because the trunk carries the training distribution, a new injector family can be fitted with a small additive residual head rather than a full retrain: on the uncensored CDF table this recovers the accuracy that independent per-family heads lose (RMSE \SI{4.631}{mm} vs.\ \SI{5.010}{mm}, Chapter~5) while retaining a structural fallback to the shared model for unseen families, and under LONO transfer it cuts in-family RMSE by \(\approx31\%\) (\(7.44\rightarrow\SI{5.16}{mm}\)) and repairs the mean bias. This is the operational payoff of a parametric backbone over the stronger SVGP regressor: cheap, warm-started family conditioning. Its limit is equally informative for positioning---delta-only adaptation of the frozen trunk removes the bias but cannot recover the genuinely off-design Nozzle0 family (few-shot RMSE plateaus far above the \(\approx\SI{5}{mm}\) in-family band, Chapter~5), marking the boundary at which the screening layer needs new trunk capacity rather than a new head. The deployed positioning is therefore a shared censoring-aware trunk plus a cheap per-family residual adapter for in-design nozzles, with off-design families flagged as out-of-scope rather than silently adapted.

\subsection{Time-Varying Nature of Empirical Scaling Exponents and Its Implications for Feature Engineering}

The time-windowed log-log regression in Chapter~4 (Figure~\ref{fig:time-windowed-exponents}) and the feature engineering LONO ablations (Section~\ref{sec:stage1-lono-ablation}) collectively unveil a physically significant finding: within the observable window of short pilot injections, the proportionality exponent in classical empirical penetration laws is not a global constant, but a monotonically time-varying quantity.

Specifically, the \(\Delta P\) exponent smoothly descends from \(\approx0.58\)--\(0.68\) during the early injection phase (\(t\approx0.3\)--\(0.4\,\si{ms}\)) to \(\approx0.25\) at \(t\approx\SI{1.0}{ms}\), which is the Hiroyasu--Arai post-breakup asymptotic value; the exponent for the ambient density proxy \(P_\mathrm{ch}\) also drifts from \(-0.25\) towards a smaller absolute value within the same window. This pattern aligns with the stage-wise transition of spray physical mechanisms: the early injection is dominated by orifice flow and pre-breakup momentum, with the exponent biased towards the Bernoulli value of \(\approx0.5\); as droplet breakup and entrainment progressively dominate, the system asymptotes to the H--A post-breakup self-similar solution.

The second finding originates from the regression under fixed \(\Delta P\): \(P_\mathrm{ch}\) still exhibits a non-zero independent coefficient (\(\approx-0.21\) to \(-0.27\)), indicating that injection pressure and back pressure exert independent effects on penetration beyond what can be described by their difference. This comports with the physical picture of orifice throttling, cavitation, and internal nozzle flow: \(P_\mathrm{inj}\) and \(P_\mathrm{ch}\) independently affect the internal flow state of the nozzle, and are not solely coupled to the external spray via \(\Delta P\).

The above two findings concurrently demonstrate that any global constant exponent assumption---including the \(\Delta P^{1/4}\) of H--A and the empirically selected \(\Delta P^{0.5}\) of this thesis---is a mean-field approximation, valid only within a specific temporal and conditional domain. For short pilot injections, the training mass window is dominated by the transitional momentum phase (the effective observation interval for most trajectories is concentrated between \(0.3\) and \(\SI{1.0}{ms}\)), rather than being governed by the post-breakup asymptotic phase. This is precisely the empirical motivation for selecting \(\Delta P^{0.5}\) instead of \(\Delta P^{0.25}\) as the magnitude scaling group in this thesis, and it concurrently elucidates why the LONO ablation achieved consistent gains when \(\log P_\mathrm{inj}\) and \(\log P_\mathrm{ch}\) were input as independent residuals: the network needs to learn a time-varying, condition-dependent residual structure superimposed on the fixed \(\Delta P\), and this is precisely the degree of freedom that a single global exponent fails to afford.

It is imperative to note that this finding does not negate the physical validity of the Hiroyasu--Arai relation as a post-breakup asymptotic law. The contribution of this thesis here is to document that: within the specific observable time window of short pilot injections, the H--A asymptote has not yet been fully established, transitional momentum physics dominates, and this conclusion carries quantifiable practical significance for the feature design of the surrogate model.

\subsection{Future Validation Pathways for the Probabilistic Wall-Impingement Screening Prototype}

The wall-impingement screening prototype delineated in Section~4.7 realizes the probabilistic proxy advocated in the literature review, but the frame-by-frame impingement probability score \(P_{\mathrm{hit}}(t)\) has not yet been benchmarked against physical spray-wall observations.
This gap constitutes a well-defined subsequent endeavor rather than an open-ended limitation, as the requisite reference data already exists within the experimental dataset of the industrial collaborator.

For industrial deployment, five criteria should be satisfied before the prototype is used as anything stronger than a rank-oriented pre-screen.
First, the \(q_1\)-based late continuation should be validated against wide-field single-plume Mie recordings by artificially truncating the field of view and comparing the extrapolated trajectory with the full visible measurement.
Second, engine-representative optical wall data should establish whether the geometric interception score detects the first observed wall-contact frame and ranks cumulative spray exposure correctly.
Third, a CFD-annotated benchmark should quantify screening recall, false-negative rate, and computational savings for the proposed triage workflow.
Fourth, uncertainty summaries should be reported at condition or campaign level with independent confidence intervals rather than relying only on correlated point-wise samples.
Fifth, the fixed half-cone angle should be replaced or bounded by a learned, time-varying cone-angle model once the cone-angle proxy extracted in Chapter~3 has been validated.

The most direct validation target is high-speed optical experiments under representative piston geometries, namely, frame-by-frame imaging of the injection process under engine-representative boundary conditions.
Such experiments can directly observe the expansion of the spray in the vicinity of the piston crown and compare it with \(P_{\mathrm{hit}}(t)\):
The first frame in which spray contact is observed provides an empirical boundary for impingement onset, and the cumulative spray exposure within the injection window can be juxtaposed with the integrated score \(E = \int P_{\mathrm{hit}}\,\mathrm{d}t\).
This comparison will scrutinize the axial penetration surrogate under dynamic geometric conditions that the training dataset cannot provide.

Another complementary pathway is CFD numerical simulation.
High-fidelity spray calculations can resolve liquid-phase distribution, wall liquid film mass, and local droplet concentration, whereas the surrogate defined by Mie imaging merely approximates these physical quantities with a rotated Gaussian envelope.
The positioning of this surrogate is not to supplant CFD, but to serve as a low-cost upstream screening layer: operating conditions where the impingement probability exceeds the threshold proceed to full computation, while those safely below the threshold are filtered out.
Validating the screening recall rate, false negative rate, and computational savings of this stratified workflow necessitates a set of CFD-annotated reference operating conditions; the current dataset does not yet possess these.
Only after such a benchmark can conservative geometric coverage be translated into a defensible industrial safety margin.

Both validation pathways will resolve a structural open question in the current prototype: whether a fixed half-cone angle \(\alpha\) is acceptable, or whether it is necessary to construct a time-varying transverse broadening model utilizing the cone-angle proxy extracted in Chapter~3.
Only after acquiring frame-by-frame matched reference data can the sensitivity to \(\alpha\) be reliably assessed.
