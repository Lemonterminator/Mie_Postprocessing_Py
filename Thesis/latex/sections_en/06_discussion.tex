\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Role of the Stage-3 surrogate as a screening model}

The synthetic dataset is generated by the reduced-order \(q_1\) fit described in Section~4.1: the raw CDF trajectories are first cleaned and time-aligned, then fitted to the \(q_1\) form, which provides smooth extrapolated targets over the prescribed time window.

Stage~1 extracts a stable representative mean trajectory for each operating condition from the full synthetic dataset and validates the $A$-scaling hypothesis.
Because each condition is represented by only one trajectory, however, Stage~1 alone discards plume-to-plume and shot-to-shot variation and is therefore not suitable as the final screening model.

Stage~2 extends the supervision to the full synthetic dataset and uses two output heads to learn both the conditional mean and the conditional spread over the extrapolation window.
This also explains why its MAE is slightly higher than that of Stage~1: Stage~2 solves a harder supervision problem, and one that is closer to the final screening task.

Stage~3 combines the strengths of the first two stages.
At early times, where raw observations are dense, it learns directly from measured CDF data.
At later times, where observations become sparse, it follows the right-censoring rationale established in Chapter~2 and aligns with the conditional mean and variance supplied by the Stage~2 teacher through a Gaussian KL loss.
Because both teacher and student output unimodal Gaussian distributions, the KL divergence between them has a closed form and can be computed directly from their conditional means and variances.
No Monte Carlo sampling is required, and no additional sampling noise is introduced into the training process.
This hybrid supervision explains both the early-time fidelity and the more conservative late-time behaviour of Stage~3.

\subsection{Validated Findings and Scope Limitations}

Within the declared scope, four claims are supported by the results.
First, the optical workflow can extract repeatable penetration targets from multihole Mie videos at archive scale.
Second, the reduced-order fitting stage provides both trajectory smoothing and target construction for censored observations.
Third, the staged surrogate progresses from representative mean prediction to uncertainty-aware full-dataset prediction and finally to raw-CDF-refined screening performance.
Fourth, the final uncertainty intervals are conservative enough to support cautious early-stage engineering ranking rather than only point prediction.

The scope of these claims is explicitly limited.
The extracted spray boundary is interpreted as a Mie-defined liquid-envelope observable under a specific optical protocol, not as full in-cylinder liquid truth \cite{ecn_liquid_penetration,ecn_liquid_penetration_accessory}.
The model is designed mainly for interpolation within the trained operating domain, not for unconstrained extrapolation.
It is also not intended to replace CFD, engine testing, or validated spray--wall impingement physics.
Its narrower role is to provide an auditable early-stage engineering screening layer that reduces the design space before higher-fidelity validation is launched.

The target definition itself is also limiting.
The reduced-order fit converges toward a quarter-power-dominated continuation, and the neural surrogate is regularised to remain monotone increasing.
These shape priors help enforce plausible trajectory growth and repair short-horizon extrapolation near the end of the observation window, but they do not model the image-formation physics of Mie scattering.
This thesis further assumes that, in the room-temperature, non-reacting Mie experiments, late-time image fading is caused mainly by droplet breakup, spatial dilution, reduced local droplet number density, scattering intensity falling below the detection threshold, and the side effects of the brightness-multiple mask used for background-noise suppression, rather than by rapid fuel evaporation within the millisecond observation window.
Thus, even when the plume front gradually becomes invisible before reaching the edge of the observed window, this work interprets that behaviour as a loss of visibility of the liquid geometric envelope, not as physical termination of droplet motion.
This interpretation is consistent with the physical intuition that diesel evaporation at room temperature is relatively slow, but it is not directly validated here by simultaneous droplet-size, mass-flux, or evaporation-rate measurements.
The resulting penetration should therefore be read as a continuation of a Mie-defined geometric envelope, not as direct evidence of a strong liquid-phase spray tip, local liquid mass, droplet size, phase state, or wall-film formation potential \cite{ecn_liquid_penetration,ecn_liquid_penetration_accessory}.

This extrapolation assumption still lacks direct control validation.
The originally planned validation path was to use wide-field single-plume Mie videos from the industrial partner, recorded with similar nozzle types, similar operating conditions, and a field of view roughly twice as wide as the present multihole archive, so that the full penetration trajectory could be observed up to the plateau region.
One could then artificially truncate the field of view, fit the \(q_1\) model only to the pre-truncation time series, and compare the extrapolated curve point by point against the complete measured trajectory that remains visible after the truncation point.
That experiment would directly test the core assumption of the KD chain in this thesis: that the parametric form \(\sigma\!\left((t-t_0)/s\right)\cdot k_{1/4}\,t^{1/4}\) remains a credible continuation beyond the field-of-view boundary, rather than a bias dominated by shape priors.
Because the partner was unable to provide such single-plume data, this validation could not be completed within the present study.
The reliable time range of penetration values extrapolated under the \(q_1\) assumption therefore remains an open empirical question.

These boundaries matter for downstream wall screening.
Spray--wall interaction is not uniformly harmful: if most of the liquid phase has evaporated before reaching the wall, the remaining wall interaction may be less damaging and, in some regimes, may even assist entrainment and mixing.
The primary concern is direct droplet-wall impact and persistent wall-film formation \cite{MorieiraMotaPanao2010,liu2024marine_impingement,peraza2022swi,ahmad2025fueldilution}.
The present model should therefore be understood as a conservative screening surrogate for possible near-wall reach of a Mie-defined spray envelope, not as a phase-resolved predictor of harmful liquid-wall impingement.
The relationship between liquid-envelope geometry, temperature, evaporation, local liquid mass flux, and wall-film formation is not modelled or validated in this thesis.
A higher wall-interaction score only indicates a higher geometric proximity risk; it should not be equated directly with harmful liquid impingement or wall-wetting mass.

The downstream engine-geometry coupling is also simplified.
In the current prototype, piston motion only translates the wall mask through slider--crank kinematics; time-varying chamber pressure, pressure-wave dynamics, and large-scale gas motion do not feed back into the penetration prediction.
This simplification is inherited from the OSCC measurements, where each spray event is recorded in a rigid chamber at fixed back-pressure.
The surrogate therefore learns penetration under quasi-static chamber conditions, not under fully coupled in-cylinder transients.
Because diesel-spray penetration is sensitive to ambient-gas density, this decoupling should be understood as a pragmatic modelling choice rather than as evidence that pressure and flow effects are negligible \cite{NaberSiebers1996}.
Within the intended use case, however, the approximation remains acceptable as a first-order engineering screen: the target scenario is a short pilot-injection window near top dead centre, on a millisecond time scale, and the current toy piston setup moves by less than \SI{1}{mm} over the reported \SI{5}{ms} horizon.
The immediate need is geometric ranking, not crank-angle-resolved in-cylinder truth prediction.
As engine speed increases, injection timing moves farther from top dead centre, or transient pressure and gas velocity materially change the ambient state seen by the spray, the present wall-interaction score should be read only as a decoupled screening proxy.

A related limitation is the treatment of spray cone angle.
Although the image-processing stage extracts a cone-angle proxy, this quantity is not analysed systematically across campaigns and is not promoted to a learned target in the final surrogate.
In the downstream wall-interaction model, the half-cone angle \(\alpha\) is therefore a fixed input rather than a validated time-varying observable.
Prior spray studies show that spreading angle can vary with transient development, ambient-gas density, injection conditions, and nozzle geometry \cite{NaberSiebers1996,JungManinSkeenPickett2015}.
The current geometry screen therefore learns uncertainty only along the axial penetration direction; the transverse envelope governed by \(\alpha\) is assumed rather than predicted.

The results also imply several specific boundaries:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The surrogate is trained only within the operating domain jointly supported by the experimental and synthetic data.
        It should not be assigned the same credibility under genuinely out-of-distribution pressure, nozzle geometry, ambient temperature, or injection strategy.

  \item The leave-one-nozzle-family evaluation shows that the staged MLP still has lower RMSE than the two physical baselines on unseen nozzle families, indicating a degree of cross-family robustness in the mean prediction.
        However, the hardest held-out family remains under-covered, and the Nozzle~3 image evidence links the error to detached leading puffs, main-jet catch-up and overtake, hole-to-hole asymmetry, and step-like penetration.
        This behaviour is consistent with wake-driven following-spray acceleration and catch-up/overtake motion reported in split- and multiple-injection literature: a preceding liquid packet can modify the penetration of a following packet through wake effects, axial gas momentum, and preconditioning of the local mixing field.
        Because this thesis does not include simultaneous gas-velocity measurements or packet-identity tracking, the Nozzle~3 case can only be interpreted as image evidence consistent with that mechanism; it cannot by itself prove the complete fluid-dynamic origin.
        The main cross-family risk is therefore not a collapse of average penetration prediction, but insufficient uncertainty coverage for nozzle-family-specific early transients.

  \item The external-cleaning evaluation reports four repeated anchor-off runs, with a seed-to-seed RMSE standard deviation of \SI{0.120}{mm}.
        The repeated-run mean is more reliable than a single point estimate, but it is not a full statistical certification because bootstrap confidence bands are not reported.

  \item The original held-out RMSE and MAE are computed from 519{,}570 pointwise samples drawn from 21{,}470 trajectories, but errors within the same trajectory are temporally correlated.
        The effective number of independent samples is therefore substantially smaller than the point count, and any confidence statement based on pointwise sample size should be interpreted with that correlation in mind.

  \item The staged surrogate is compared, under the same external-cleaning protocol, with calibrated Hiroyasu--Arai, delayed Naber--Siebers, a sparse heteroscedastic Gaussian-process baseline, and its own Stage~3 ablations.
        These comparisons cover physical correlations, non-parametric probabilistic regression, and the main differences in supervision strategy.
        However, lightweight tabular regressors such as random forests, gradient boosting, or quantile boosting are not systematically included.
        This thesis therefore does not claim that the neural architecture has an optimal marginal benefit over all deterministic machine-learning alternatives.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future Validation Pathway for the Probabilistic Screening Prototype}

The screening prototype described in Section~4.7 operationalises the probabilistic proxy introduced in the literature review, but the resulting frame-by-frame collision probability scores \(P_{\mathrm{hit}}(t)\) have not yet been compared with physical spray--wall observations.
This gap is a well-defined future-work item rather than an open-ended limitation, because the required reference data exist within the industrial partner's experimental portfolio.

The most direct validation target is high-speed optical imaging of spray injection in a representative piston geometry under engine-representative boundary conditions.
Such experiments can directly observe spray spreading near the piston crown and compare it with \(P_{\mathrm{hit}}(t)\): the first frame with observed spray contact provides an empirical bound on collision onset, while cumulative spray exposure over the injection window can be compared with the integrated score \(E = \int P_{\mathrm{hit}}\,\mathrm{d}t\).
This comparison would test the axial-penetration surrogate under dynamic-geometry conditions that the training dataset cannot provide.

A complementary validation path uses CFD.
High-fidelity spray computations can resolve liquid-phase distribution, wall-film mass, and local droplet concentration, whereas the Mie-defined surrogate approximates these quantities with a rotated Gaussian envelope.
The surrogate is not intended to replace CFD, but to serve as a low-cost upstream screening layer: operating points above a collision-probability threshold advance to full computation, while points safely below the threshold are filtered out.
Validating the recall rate, false-negative rate, and computational savings of this tiered workflow requires a set of CFD-labelled reference cases that the current dataset does not contain.

Both validation paths would address a structural open question in the current prototype: whether the fixed half-cone angle \(\alpha\) is acceptable, or whether a time-varying lateral-spread model driven by the cone-angle proxy extracted in Chapter~3 is needed.
The sensitivity to \(\alpha\) can only be assessed reliably once frame-matched reference data are available.
