\section{Experimental Data and Image-Processing Workflow}

\subsection{Workflow overview}

Figure~\ref{fig:surrogate-data-flow} shows the overall data flow of the surrogate-model workflow used in this thesis. The pipeline is organised into four lanes. Raw inputs (high-speed Mie videos, calibration configuration, and test metadata) pass through CUDA-accelerated image processing to produce 1D features and boundary points; from these, both binary-based (BW-based) and CDF-based penetration traces and other geometric metrics are derived. These observables are extended into synthetic trajectories by parameter-free curve fitting and used to drive three neural training stages: Stage~1 (MSE), Stage~2 (NLL), and Stage~3 (Raw + KD). The final trained model is deployed in the wall-impingement screening application, combining piston geometry and physical parameters to produce collision probabilities and visualisation animations. Table~\ref{tab:workflow} further summarises the implemented workflow stages and the role of each stage in textual form. This chapter focuses on the first two lanes of Figure~\ref{fig:surrogate-data-flow}: how the OSCC video data are calibrated, preprocessed, registered, and converted into plume-resolved geometric observables. Target construction, surrogate training, and probabilistic screening are deferred to the following chapter.

The method-level motivation is that optical experiments provide image-based evidence, but the result of event-wise image post-processing is still essentially a tabulated dataset of discrete observations.
If left at that level, these observations cannot directly serve as a condition-parametric predictive model for fast design iteration.
For that reason, this chapter treats image processing not as a cosmetic preprocessing step, but as the auditable layer that turns high-speed Mie recordings into consistently defined plume-resolved temporal observables.

These tasks are carried out on data from the W\"artsil\"a Optical Spray Combustion Chamber (OSCC).
The raw material consists of terabyte-scale, room-temperature, non-reacting, top-view Mie-scattering recordings
collected with a fixed camera arrangement across multiple operating conditions and nozzle geometries.
The method first calibrates the nozzle geometry manually, then uses a CUDA-accelerated preprocessing, polar-registration,
and plume-strip extraction chain to generate plume-resolved observables,
and constructs penetration targets through reduced-order fitting before passing them to a three-stage MLP surrogate described in Chapter~4.

Each operating-condition subfolder typically contains five repeated recordings,

\subsection{Manual calibration of nozzle geometry}\label{subsec:nozzle-calibration}

The manual calibration workflow in \textbf{the interactive calibration interface} consists of three user-facing stages.

First, the operator opens a representative frame in the main annotation interface, adjusts the display settings, and prepares the nozzle-centred view that will be used for the batch anchor.

Second, the dedicated calibration dialog is launched, where the user clicks nozzle-rim points on the selected frame.

Third, the fitted centre and radius are returned to the main interface, where the recovered geometry can be inspected together with the plume guides and ring overlay before the configuration is saved for later batch processing.

The point-selection dialog enforces at least three clicks and computes the circle through \textbf{the algebraic circle fitting function} once the required number of points has been entered. The fitted circle provides the nozzle centre, calibration radius, and angular offset for the full recording.

Calibrating once and reusing the same centre for all frames avoids frame-to-frame jitter in the downstream plume-angle analysis.

\subsection{Batch video processing and plume extraction}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{../images/Multi-Hole Top-View Mie Scattering Video Processing Pipeline.png}
\caption{Overview of the multi-hole top-view Mie-scattering processing pipeline, from raw recordings through calibration, CUDA-accelerated preprocessing, plume registration, and penetration extraction.}
\label{fig:pipeline-overview}

The file processor in \textbf{the multi-hole geometric construction script} is the operational centre of the image-analysis workflow. For each \texttt{.cine} recording, \textbf{the video-processing loop} loads the video to the active array backend, reuses an annular ring mask for the whole folder, calls \textbf{the multi-hole preprocessing routine} and \textbf{the multi-hole postprocessing routine}, extracts plume-wise penetration curves, computes boundary-based geometric metrics, and finally exports synchronised CSV and metadata files. The numerically heavy part of this chain stays backend-local: when CuPy is available, convolution, angular binning, remapping, thresholding, connected-component cleanup, and morphology all remain on the CUDA path until the final tabular export. Throughout this section, bolded descriptive names are used in place of specific script, function, and variable names to keep the exposition implementation-agnostic; the correspondence is tabulated in Appendix~\ref{app:implementation_mapping}.

This implementation choice was motivated by the practical limits of the earlier in-house route, which relied on frame-by-frame MATLAB processing and lacked effective batch parallelism: processing approximately 50~GB of video could take roughly a day and remain prone to crashes. Moving the heavy image operations into a bulk CUDA-oriented pipeline is therefore presented as a methodological rather than a cosmetic software decision.

\subsubsection{Batch scheduling, memory reuse, and resume contract}

The CUDA pipeline is an end-to-end archive-processing scheduler built around three engineering safeguards.
Video loading uses a depth-1 producer--consumer prefetch: while recording~\(N\) is being processed on the GPU, a background worker reads recording~\(N+1\) from disk, so that GPU compute and disk I/O overlap rather than serialise.
Host-to-device transfers and CuPy array construction are deliberately kept in the main thread, because CuPy's CUDA execution context is thread-affine; this ensures that kernel dispatch, synchronisation, and memory-pool state are all managed by a single thread without locking overhead.
Output writes are handled by a persistent three-worker I/O pool: metrics CSVs, metadata JSONs, boundary archives, and optional preview video files are submitted as futures immediately after the GPU stage completes, so that the next recording's GPU processing overlaps with the previous one's disk writes.

Memory management is matched to the archive access pattern.
After each recording, intermediate arrays are released and a garbage-collection pass returns CuPy arrays to the default memory pool, but block-level pool deallocation is deliberately deferred to subfolder boundaries.
Because consecutive recordings within the same subfolder typically share the same frame shape, retaining pool blocks allows the next file to reuse an already-allocated GPU buffer directly, avoiding repeated round-trips to the CUDA driver; hard pool release at subfolder boundaries limits fragmentation across long archive runs.

The pipeline also maintains a recoverable output contract.
A per-run checkpoint file, \codepath{processing\_checkpoint.json}, is written with an explicit filesystem flush after each recording; it tracks per-file fields \texttt{status}, \texttt{file}, \texttt{outputs}, \texttt{error}, and \texttt{updated\_at}.
Completion requires both a metrics CSV and a metadata JSON to be present; when boundary export is enabled, the corresponding boundary archive must also exist, and a partial result triggers reprocessing rather than being silently accepted.
This contract allows a failed multi-hour batch run to resume from the last successful file without repeating work.

The same checkpoint logic normalises metadata across heterogeneous experiment years.
Frame rate is read preferentially from the Phantom CINE header field \texttt{FrameRate}, with \texttt{FrameRate16} and \texttt{fPbRate} as successive fallbacks; injection duration is resolved from a test-matrix JSON lookup keyed on the CINE filename, with a within-group constant as a further fallback; injection pressure, plume count, and umbrella angle come from nozzle or test-matrix JSON files, reverting to conservative in-script defaults only when absent.
This metadata hierarchy matters for the mixed Nozzle0 (2022) and Nozzle1--8 (2024) archives, whose recording-header conventions differ.
The clipping bounds described in Section~3.2 set a physical time window of approximately 3--4\,ms, covering the pilot injection and its early relaxation tail targeted in this thesis.

\subsubsection{Calibration hand-off and batch anchoring}

The batch stage starts from the calibration produced in Section~\ref{subsec:nozzle-calibration}. In the algebraic circle fitting function, the clicked nozzle-rim points \((x_i,y_i)\) are fitted with the algebraic circle model
\[
x_i^2+y_i^2 + D x_i + E y_i + F = 0,
\]
solved in least-squares form by \texttt{np.linalg.lstsq}. The recovered parameters are
\[
c_x=-\frac{D}{2}, \qquad c_y=-\frac{E}{2}, \qquad r=\sqrt{c_x^2+c_y^2-F},
\]
and the same routine also estimates an average angular offset from the clicked rim points relative to an ideal equally spaced set. The GUI writes these values to \textbf{the calibration configuration file}, after which the batch pipeline can process a full folder without manual re-centering. The minimal \texttt{config.json} schema is \{\texttt{plumes}, \texttt{centre\_x}, \texttt{centre\_y}, \texttt{inner\_radius}, \texttt{outer\_radius}\}; the two radii define the annular processing mask, the local orifice origin, and the downstream strip geometry. The pixel-to-millimetre scale is not obtained from a full camera-intrinsic calibration, but inferred from the known physical outer-ring scale:
\[
s_{\mathrm{mm/px}}=\frac{90.0}{r_{\mathrm{outer,px}}},
\]
where \(r_{\mathrm{outer,px}}\) is the \texttt{outer\_radius} stored in the configuration file. This one-time calibration is deliberately user-facing rather than fully automatic: the operator can zoom, choose a representative frame, and anchor the full batch with a physically meaningful nozzle centre \cite{Pratt1987CircleFit,Taubin1991CircleFit}.

\subsubsection{Bulk and robust preprocessing}

The first algorithmic contribution inside the batch pipeline is a foreground construction stage designed for noisy Mie videos rather than for idealised binary images. In \textbf{the foreground extraction filter}, the raw frame \(V_t(x,y)\) is mapped to
\[
L_t(x,y)=\log(V_t(x,y)+\varepsilon),
\qquad
L_{\mathrm{bkg}}(x,y)=\operatorname{median}_{t<t_{\mathrm{SOI}}} L_t(x,y),
\]
followed by the log-ratio reconstruction
\[
R_t(x,y)=\exp\!\left(L_t(x,y)-L_{\mathrm{bkg}}(x,y)\right)-1
=\frac{V_t(x,y)-B(x,y)}{B(x,y)}.
\]
The implementation then multiplies \(R_t\) by an intensity gate \(M_t(x,y)=\mathbf{1}[V_t(x,y)>\alpha B(x,y)]\), subtracts a pre-injection baseline, applies a soft threshold
\[
F_t(x,y)=\max\!\left(M_t(x,y)R_t(x,y)-R_0(x,y)-\tau,\,0\right),
\]
where \(\varepsilon\) is a small constant to prevent log-zero, \(t_{\mathrm{SOI}}\) is the start-of-injection time, \(B(x,y) = \exp(L_{\mathrm{bkg}}(x,y)) - \varepsilon\) is the linear-scale background, \(\alpha\) is a contrast multiplier for the intensity gate, \(R_0(x,y)\) is the residual pre-injection baseline, and \(\tau\) is a soft threshold to suppress background noise. The implementation then rescales the result by robust quantiles before any geometric processing. This sequence is important for Mie videos because the raw intensity is not a direct measure of liquid volume fraction, and because the practical objective is stable geometry extraction under non-uniform illumination, glare, and background texture rather than exact optical inversion \cite{BohrenHuffman2004,Linne2013SprayImaging,GonzalezWoodsDIP}.

The need for this robust preprocessing can already be seen at the visualization level, but display enhancement itself does not enter the quantitative chain. Figure~\ref{fig:gui-gain-halo} compares the same spray frame under two display gains while the remaining GUI parameters are held fixed. At the lower gain, the plume cores remain comparatively compact. At the higher gain, a bright halo spreads around the spray core and contaminates a much larger region of the image. The example is not evidence that one display gain is more physically truthful than another. Instead, it shows that using gamma-corrected, display-gain-adjusted, or locally contrast-enhanced frames for boundary extraction would make the boundary move with the display mapping \cite{Cromey2010TwistedPixels}. These views are therefore used only for human inspection. The quantitative computation uses a robust background estimate from pre-injection statistics and then shifts the representation toward edge-dominant Sobel features, so that the geometric response is governed by fixed operators and local boundary structure rather than display brightness \cite{BohrenHuffman2004,Linne2013SprayImaging,GonzalezWoodsDIP}.

\subsubsection{Rank-1 separable Sobel high-pass on the CUDA backend}

The second contribution is the explicit edge-enhancing high-pass stage applied directly to the full video cube. In \textbf{the GPU-accelerated Sobel filter}, Sobel kernels \(K_x\) and \(K_y\) are generated once and applied with \textbf{the 2D convolution routine}:
\[
S_t(x,y)=\sqrt{\left(K_x * F_t\right)^2+\left(K_y * F_t\right)^2},
\]
where \(K_x\) and \(K_y\) are the horizontal and vertical Sobel convolution kernels, \(*\) denotes the 2-D convolution operator, \(F_t\) is the preprocessed foreground image, and \(S_t\) is the resulting gradient magnitude.

A subtler efficiency comes from the rank-1 structure of the Sobel kernel itself. In \textbf{the kernel construction function}, each directional kernel is assembled as an explicit outer product,
\[
K_x = \mathbf{g} \otimes \mathbf{d}_g, \qquad K_y = \mathbf{d}_g \otimes \mathbf{g},
\]
where \(\mathbf{g}\) is a discrete Gaussian envelope and \(\mathbf{d}_g\) is its derivative. An outer-product matrix has rank exactly one, so the 2-D convolution is equivalent to two sequential 1-D passes along the spatial axes. \textbf{The rank-1 separability test} verifies this automatically and switches to the separable path when it holds, reducing the per-pixel multiply-add cost from \(O(k^2)\) to \(O(2k)\) for a \(k\times k\) kernel. Because the Sobel kernel is rank-1 by construction, both \(K_x\) and \(K_y\) are always applied as a pair of 1-D convolutions across the full video cube.

The output is then robust-scaled again and masked by the ring geometry, converting a low-contrast greyscale plume into an edge-dominant representation while keeping the entire operation on the CuPy backend. The contribution is not the Sobel operator itself, which is classical, but its integration into a bulk video workflow that avoids repeated host--device copies and prepares every plume for the same downstream geometric treatment \cite{GonzalezWoodsDIP,Szeliski2010Vision}.

The high-pass strips are no longer binarized with a triangle threshold, because that rule was sensitive to the near-zero peak, the long-tailed intensity distribution, and the fraction of zeros introduced by masking. The current implementation first applies percentile-based min--max normalization to each plume's \((t,y,x)\) high-pass strip volume, mapping the \(q_{\min}\) and \(q_{\max}\) percentiles to 0 and 1 and clipping values outside this range. It then applies a single fixed threshold \(T_{\mathrm{BW}}=0.01\) on the normalized strip:
\[
B_p(t,y,x)=\mathbf{1}\!\left[\tilde{H}_p(t,y,x)>T_{\mathrm{BW}}\right].
\]
This makes the percentile normalization absorb video-to-video brightness and local-contrast differences, while the threshold only expresses whether the normalized edge response is strong enough. The threshold marked in Figure~\ref{fig:threshold-mask-repair-summary}(a) is this fixed support threshold on the normalized intensity scale.

When the binary support needs further repair, \textbf{the binary plume-video repair function} applies a time-volume-first, frame-second sequence. For each plume's \((t,y,x)\) binary volume, the routine first applies closing with a \(3\times3\times3\) structuring element, fills holes, and retains the largest 3-D connected component; it then keeps the largest 2-D connected component in each individual frame. The first step uses temporal adjacency to bridge short breaks, while the second ensures that each frame is free of detached fragments. Figure~\ref{fig:threshold-mask-repair-summary}(d) visualizes the effect of this repair path on a single-frame support mask; it should be read as a robustness step for binary geometric metrics, not as a physical recovery of the raw Mie intensity field.

\subsubsection{Angular density as polar resampling}

After preprocessing, the pipeline does not search for plume directions by contour tracing in Cartesian coordinates. Instead, \textbf{the polar re-parameterization routine} performs a polar re-parameterization around the calibrated nozzle center. For every pixel,
\[
\theta(x,y)=\operatorname{atan2}(y-c_y,\;x-c_x)\bmod 360^\circ,
\]
and the per-frame angular signal for bin \(k\) is accumulated as
\[
A_t(k)=\sum_{(x,y)\in \mathcal{B}_k} I_t(x,y), \qquad
D_t(k)=\frac{A_t(k)}{\#\mathcal{B}_k},
\]
where \(\mathcal{B}_k\) is the set of pixels whose angle falls into bin \(k\), \(I_t(x,y)\) is the processed foreground intensity, \(A_t(k)\) is the accumulated angular signal, \(D_t(k)\) is the mean angular density, and \(\#\mathcal{B}_k\) is the number of pixels in the bin. In the CuPy implementation, the binning and weighted sums are performed directly on GPU arrays by \textbf{the GPU histogram binning operation} and \textbf{the GPU weighted sum operation}. Conceptually, this is a radial collapse of the 2D image into a 1D angular occupancy signal, which is a much more stable object for multihole alignment than raw edge fragments in the original frame \cite{Szeliski2010Vision}.

\subsubsection{FFT-based self-adaptive rotation alignment}

The plume guide angles are then estimated from the periodicity of the angular signal rather than from a fixed offset. Let
\[
\bar{A}(k)=\sum_t A_t(k)
\]
be the time-summed angular profile, and let \(P\) be the known number of plumes. The function \textbf{the FFT-based phase alignment function} computes the real FFT of \(\bar{A}\), reads the phase of the plume harmonic \(P\), and converts it to an angular offset
\[
\hat{\phi}_0
=
-\frac{\arg\!\left(\mathcal{F}\{\bar{A}\}[P]\right)}{P}\frac{180^\circ}{\pi}.
\]
This is what makes the alignment self-adaptive: the code uses the observed harmonic phase of the current dataset instead of assuming a manually typed reference angle for every recording. \textbf{the multi-hole geometric construction script} then constructs plume directions
\[
\phi_p=\frac{360^\circ p}{P}-\hat{\phi}_0, \qquad p=0,\dots,P-1,
\]
and combines them with a triangle-threshold occupancy mask and short-gap filling to reject empty angular sectors. The FFT itself is standard; the contribution here is using the known injector multiplicity to turn the angular-density signal into an automatic plume registration rule \cite{CooleyTukey1965FFT,Szeliski2010Vision}.

The same occupied-angle mask also suggests a simple cone-angle proxy. Treat the bin-wise mask as periodic on \([0,360^\circ)\), merge any \texttt{True} runs that cross the \(0^\circ/360^\circ\) boundary, and convert each contiguous run length from bins to degrees. The resulting angular widths quantify the support occupied by each plume over the full recording. We report both the total occupied angle and the mean run width; the latter serves as a coarse cone-angle proxy because it summarises the average angular spread of a plume without requiring a separate edge fit in every frame.

\subsubsection{Inverse affine mapping and plume-strip extraction}

Once the target angles are known, each plume is remapped into a common nozzle-left strip coordinate system. Each plume angle \(\phi_p\) defines a rotation-plus-translation that places the nozzle at the left edge and mid-height of the output strip; the remap is computed via the inverse relation implemented in \textbf{the inverse affine map generator}:
\[
\begin{bmatrix}
x_{\mathrm{src}}\\y_{\mathrm{src}}
\end{bmatrix}
=
\mathbf{A}^{-1}
\!\left(
\begin{bmatrix}
u\\v
\end{bmatrix}
-\mathbf{b}
\right),
\qquad
\mathbf{A}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\phi_p & \sin\phi_p\\
-\sin\phi_p & \cos\phi_p
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{t}-\mathbf{A}\mathbf{c},
\]
where \(\mathbf{c}=(c_x,c_y)^\top\) is the nozzle centre and \(\mathbf{t}\) is the strip-origin translation. For each plume, \textbf{the X/Y-coordinate inverse spatial maps} are precomputed once by \textbf{the forward affine transform builder}, and \textbf{the GPU video remapping routine} samples the full video cube with nearest, bilinear, bicubic, or Lanczos kernels. These maps store source-image sampling coordinates rather than spray intensities: the X-map pixel value gives the source \(x\) coordinate sampled for that output-strip pixel, and the Y-map stores the corresponding \(y\). The multihole pipeline applies a fixed strip size \textbf{the standardised strip dimensions} and a triangular plume mask after remapping. Precomputing the inverse maps once per plume angle makes the per-frame cost independent of rotation; every downstream metric is then measured in the same local strip coordinate system \cite{Szeliski2010Vision}.

\subsubsection{Robust CDF penetration from aligned plume strips}

The penetration estimator is deliberately based on cumulative intensity support instead of on the single brightest pixel or the outermost isolated blob. For each plume strip \(I_p(t,y,x)\), \textbf{the plume-wise CDF penetration estimator} first collapses the cross-stream direction,
\[
H_p(t,x)=\sum_y I_p(t,y,x),
\]
subtracts a left-edge baseline from each frame, clips negative values, and generates a support mask by automatic thresholding, largest-component cleanup, and binary opening. Within that support, \textbf{the axial CDF front locator} forms the axial cumulative distribution
\[
C_p(t,x)=\frac{\sum_{\xi \le x} \tilde{H}_p(t,\xi)}{\sum_{\xi} \tilde{H}_p(t,\xi)+\varepsilon},
\]
where \(\tilde{H}_p(t,\xi)\) is the baseline-subtracted and cleaned cross-stream sum, \(\varepsilon\) prevents division by zero, and \(C_p(t,x)\) is the resulting axial cumulative distribution. The penetration front \(\hat{x}_p(t)\) is then defined as the first location satisfying
\[
\hat{x}_p(t)=\min \{x : C_p(t,x)\ge q\},
\]
where the quantile threshold is fixed at \(q=0.995\) in the batch archive used throughout this thesis, i.e., \texttt{upper\_quantile\_cdf = 1 - 5e-3} in \codepath{mie\_multi\_hole.py}. The reusable library function keeps a lower default value for interactive testing; all quantitative results in this thesis use the \(q=0.995\) value written by the batch script into the archive. The inner radius is then subtracted to express distance from the nozzle annulus, followed by explicit trajectory cleanup. This cleanup does not enforce monotonicity by lifting all backward values with a cumulative maximum. Instead, the first non-physical backward jump triggers tail truncation:
\[
\Delta \hat{x}_p(j)=\hat{x}_p(j+1)-\hat{x}_p(j),\qquad
j_p^\star=\min\{j:\Delta \hat{x}_p(j)<0\},
\]
\[
\hat{x}_p(t)\leftarrow \mathrm{NaN}\quad \text{for}\quad t\ge j_p^\star .
\]
In the implementation, truncation begins near the first negative-difference frame, which can sacrifice later weak reappearances of signal; this is a conservative choice to avoid interpreting isolated noise spikes or boundary reflections as true penetration retreat. After tail truncation, the script builds a valid-frame mask and removes isolated short segments with \texttt{remove\_short\_true\_runs(..., min\_len=5)}. This CDF front is one of the main practical contributions of the workflow: it is much less sensitive to broken leading-edge pixels than a naive max-index definition, while still producing a penetration quantity aligned with standard diesel-spray usage \cite{Dent1971Penetration,HiroyasuArai1990,NaberSiebers1996}.

\subsubsection{Boundary metrics and geometric proxies}

Binarized plume strips are also passed to \textbf{the secondary feature extractor}, which computes several secondary observables. The projected area is
\[
A_p(t)=\sum_{x,y} B_p(t,y,x).
\]
Axial binarized penetration is obtained from the rightmost column whose occupancy exceeds a pixel threshold, while polar penetration is defined from the upper and lower boundary point clouds as
\[
R_p(t)=\max\!\left(
\max_i \sqrt{x_{u,i}^2+y_{u,i}^2},
\max_j \sqrt{x_{l,j}^2+y_{l,j}^2}
\right).
\]
The code also stores a volume-like geometric proxy,
\[
V_p(t)=x_{\mathrm{scale}} \frac{\pi}{4}\sum_x \left(w_u(t,x)+w_l(t,x)\right)^2,
\]
where \(w_u\) and \(w_l\) are the upper and lower half-widths and \(x_{\mathrm{scale}}=1/\cos((180^\circ-\theta_{\mathrm{umb}})/2)\) compensates for umbrella-angle tilt when needed. This quantity is not a three-dimensional reconstruction; it is a geometric proxy obtained by interpreting the binary projected cross-section as an axisymmetric envelope. The script also exports upper and lower variants,
\[
V_{p,\max}(t)=\pi x_{\mathrm{scale}}\sum_x \max(w_u,w_l)^2,\qquad
V_{p,\min}(t)=\pi x_{\mathrm{scale}}\sum_x \min(w_u,w_l)^2,
\]
which use the larger and smaller of the two half-widths as the local effective radius. These bounds record the possible range of the proxy when the upper and lower boundaries are asymmetric. Cone-angle proxies are computed in two ways: by averaging local boundary angles
\[
\alpha_{\mathrm{avg}}(t)=\overline{\arctan(y_u/x_u)}-\overline{\arctan(y_l/x_l)},
\]
and by fitting fixed-intercept boundary lines and taking
\[
\alpha_{\mathrm{lg}}(t)=\arctan(m_u)-\arctan(m_l).
\]
Near-nozzle opening and closing times are finally inferred from a small detection window close to the injector. These metrics matter because they turn each aligned strip into a compact geometric state vector; however, the downstream modeling in this thesis remains focused on penetration, which is the best-supported target in the available analyses \cite{Linne2013SprayImaging,LefebvreMcDonell2017}.

\subsection{Boundary extraction without pixel-level ground truth}

This thesis does not formulate spray-boundary extraction as a pixel-level supervised segmentation task, and therefore does not use IoU or F1 score, which require manually annotated ground-truth masks, as the primary evaluation criteria. For high-speed Mie images, droplet scattering usually creates direct local contrast between a bright foreground and a dark background; compared with the background refractive-index texture and large-scale grey-level variation often encountered in Schlieren images, this imaging characteristic is better matched to gradient enhancement and morphological cleanup for obtaining an operational boundary. The spray boundary itself is also affected by atomisation, breakup, and grey-level transition, so it does not provide a unique pixel-level ground truth. Building an IoU or F1 benchmark would require pixel-wise manual annotation over a large time sequence, and those annotations would still contain subjective boundary judgements. The reliability of the extracted boundary is therefore assessed primarily through the Mie-scattering imaging mechanism, overlay inspection, temporal continuity, and consistency among multiple geometric proxy metrics. This evaluation choice matches the objective of the thesis: stable extraction of penetration, cone angle, and boundary-envelope observables, rather than training or comparing a generic semantic segmenter.
Quantitative evidence supporting the reliability of the extracted observables --- including cross-metric consistency between CDF and binary penetration estimates, temporal-continuity checks, and leave-one-nozzle-family diagnostics --- is presented in Chapter~5.
