\section{Literature Review and Technical Basis}

The purpose of this chapter is not to preemptively repeat the subsequent implementation details, but to clarify the origins of these methodological choices.
Centering on Mie geometric observables, short-pulse penetration behaviors, multi-plume registration, right-censored targets, uncertainty-aware surrogate models, and probabilistic wall-impingement risk screening, this thesis outlines the support provided by existing research while highlighting the gaps that have not been comprehensively covered by any single literature thread.
Therefore, this chapter serves as a transitional bridge:
before delving into the specific workflows, it elucidates the logical chain between existing knowledge and the engineering design decisions in this thesis.
Readers with a primary background in fluid mechanics and mechanical design, who wish to first familiarize themselves with the terminology of computer vision, robust optimization, GPU parallel computing, and neural network training involved in this thesis, are referred to Appendix~\ref{app:ml-primer-en}.

One structural consequence runs through the entire workflow and is worth signalling upfront.
The top-view multi-plume configuration was chosen because it simultaneously captures all $N$ spray plumes in a single record, preserving the plume-to-plume and shot-to-shot variation that data-driven modelling requires.
However, this same configuration makes the field-of-view boundary a hard ceiling on all observable trajectory endpoints: the effective horizontal observation depth ($\approx$80--85\,mm after masking near the nozzle) is smaller than the nozzle-to-wall distance in the target engine geometry (W20DF bore 200\,mm, nozzle-to-wall radius $\approx$100\,mm), meaning every trajectory in the dataset is structurally right-censored.
Treating censored endpoints as ordinary regression labels would systematically pull the surrogate's predicted penetration toward the observation boundary rather than toward the true physical terminus---the model would learn to interpret ``leaving the field of view'' as ``physical saturation.''
The downstream consequence would be unreliable late-stage penetration predictions and therefore unreliable probabilistic impingement risk rankings, which are the primary engineering output of this thesis.
This chain---experimental configuration $\to$ structural censoring $\to$ biased labels $\to$ corrupted risk ranking---is the common thread linking the data construction, training, and evaluation decisions described in the following subsections.

\subsection{Principles of Mie Scattering Imaging and Geometric Observables}

Mie scattering is widely used for dense liquid-phase spray visualization, a primary reason being its relatively concise experimental implementation;
compared to many alternative diagnostic techniques, Mie imaging is easier to integrate with high-speed photography platforms, thereby achieving high temporal resolution \cite{Linne2013SprayImaging}.
However, a relatively easy imaging implementation does not imply that quantitative interpretation is equally straightforward.
Mie imaging records the scattering intensity field, a signal that cannot be directly equated to fuel mass or liquid volume fraction.
Its scattering response depends on droplet size distribution, number density, refractive index, wavelength, scattering angle relative to the illumination direction, and path length through the spray volume; in dense regions, multiple scattering and shadowing effects further alter the image intensity distribution \cite{BohrenHuffman2004,Linne2013SprayImaging}.
Therefore, attempting to directly invert the complete spray physical state from pixel intensities is inherently an ill-posed problem.

Different optical diagnostic methods possess distinct response mechanisms to spray information.
DBI (Diffuse Background Illumination) measures path-integrated extinction by applying the Beer--Lambert law to a spatially uniform diffuse transmitted-light field, yielding a measurement proportional to the liquid volume fraction integrated along the line of sight, and is typically suitable for extracting liquid-phase contours \cite{Pickett2011DBISAEref};
Schlieren responds to the first spatial derivative of the refractive index field, while Shadowgraph responds to its second derivative (the Laplacian); both can image liquid-spray boundaries through the refractive index discontinuity at the liquid–gas interface, but their signals are simultaneously modulated by gas-phase density gradients and vapour-phase distributions in evaporating conditions, making it harder to isolate the pure liquid-phase boundary compared to droplet scattering methods;
LIF characterizes vapor distribution or equivalence ratio through fuel or tracer fluorescence (liquid-phase LIF and exciplex techniques can also separate liquid and vapor contributions \cite{Suzuki1990}, but both require fluorescent additives or dedicated tracer compounds that impose chemical compatibility constraints, which effectively precludes their use with commercial diesel in facilities such as the OSCC);
PIV focuses on velocity fields and turbulent structures;
PDPA provides single-point droplet size and velocity information; reconstructing a two-dimensional spray envelope requires spatial scanning, making it unsuitable for instantaneous full-field multi-plume characterisation.
While all these methods have their respective values, their alignment with the target observables differs for the non-evaporating, non-reacting liquid-phase diesel sprays focused on in this thesis:
LIF, PIV, and PDPA lean toward component, flow field, or single-point size-velocity measurements, which struggle to directly support the high-temporal-resolution liquid-phase envelope tracking required here;
Although Schlieren and Shadowgraph can provide spray shape information, their specificity for liquid-phase boundaries is not as explicit as that of droplet scattering methods;
DBI and Mie are much closer to the tasks of extracting liquid-phase contours, penetration, and cone angles that this thesis concerns \cite{Linne2013SprayImaging}.

This positioning is consistent with existing research.
Early two-dimensional Mie scattering work conducted inside optical diesel engines treated the jet tip penetration and cone angle as practical outputs for image analysis, rather than pursuing complete liquid-phase state reconstruction \cite{MuenchLeipertz1992};
Even in recent imaging studies with higher instrument levels and synchronization precision, liquid-phase spray comparisons still revolve around penetration and expansion angle metrics \cite{JungManinSkeenPickett2015}.
This implies that studies based on Mie videos should be judged primarily by the quality, repeatability, and explicit boundaries of the observables they extract.

The data used in this thesis comes from completed top-view multi-hole nozzle Mie scattering experiments by Wärtsilä OSCC.
Compared to common backlit, side-view, single-hole isolated setups, this experiment uses side-window illumination and places the injector on the back of the chamber, allowing a single imaging event to simultaneously cover all spray plumes.
This arrangement is more suitable for preserving the plume-to-plume and shot-to-shot variations required for subsequent modeling in this thesis.
Based on the aforementioned methodological attributes, this thesis does not interpret pixel intensities as thermodynamic ground truths for direct inversion, but rather treats them as geometric cues for the liquid-phase boundaries;
The image processing objectives are explicitly restricted to extracting operational observables such as penetration, cone angle, projected area, and boundary positions, as such geometric descriptions are more defensible than claims of precise liquid-phase distributions \cite{Linne2013SprayImaging,LefebvreMcDonell2017}.
Among these, penetration is the core input for subsequent trajectory fitting, surrogate modeling, and wall-impingement risk screening \cite{Linne2013SprayImaging,LefebvreMcDonell2017,Dent1971Penetration,HiroyasuArai1990,NaberSiebers1996}.
Under this positioning, the slowly varying halos near the nozzle caused by multiple scattering are not interpreted as local liquid-phase concentrations, but are treated as background components to be suppressed during post-processing.

Mie imaging also carries a characteristic physical limitation of multiple scattering:
In the dense liquid core region near the nozzle, the high droplet number density produces intense forward-scattered and wide-angle scattered light that redistributes intensity spatially, blurring the sharp liquid-phase boundary and producing a diffuse halo rather than a clear edge \cite{BohrenHuffman2004,Linne2013SprayImaging,Berrocal2007MultipleScattering}.
Berrocal et al.\ quantified the optical depth threshold beyond which multiple scattering becomes the dominant image-formation mechanism in dense diesel sprays, demonstrating that liquid-core regions typically operate well into this multiply scattered regime.
This thesis treats this halo as a slowly varying background component rather than a local liquid-phase concentration. The corresponding suppression methods (gradient-domain filtering) and geometric corrections under line-of-sight integration are discussed in the image processing workflow in Chapter~3.

Equally important is that quantitative processing should strive to preserve the linear response of the camera's raw gray values, and should not use display-oriented gamma correction, frame-by-frame display gain, or local contrast enhancement as measurement inputs \cite{Cromey2010TwistedPixels}.
Such nonlinear or adaptive grayscale transformations alter local gradient proportions, relative threshold relationships, and the cumulative distribution support ranges used for leading-edge extraction, making boundary positions no longer determined solely by optical records and fixed post-processing rules.
Therefore, the enhancement steps used later in this thesis are not intended to make the images more visually appealing, but to extract geometric boundaries from the raw Mie records under fixed, reproducible rules.

It must be further emphasized that the process from Mie intensity fields to geometric observables depends on specific measurement rules.
The reported ``penetration'' is therefore not a universal ground truth, but the result of a specific measurement rule acting on the optical signal.
The Engine Combustion Network (ECN) benchmark studies provided systematic evidence for this methodological dependency:
inter-facility comparisons of nominally identical injectors (Spray~A and Spray~D) revealed scatter in liquid-phase penetration arising from a combination of genuine ambient-condition differences, distinct optical setups (DBI versus Mie), and post-processing threshold definitions \cite{ecn_liquid_penetration,ecn_liquid_penetration_accessory,MeijerECN2012}.
Separately, Magnotti and Genzale showed that even within a single dataset, contrasting metrics such as maximum liquid penetration, path-integrated liquid volume, and projected spray area can lead to substantially different model validation conclusions \cite{MagnottiGenzale2013}, explicitly establishing the optical liquid-phase boundary as a quantity defined by a specific measurement protocol rather than an absolute representation of the true liquid-phase distribution.
The experimental conditions in this thesis differ from the high-temperature, high-pressure evaporating conditions of ECN Spray~A, and also from the baseline settings of Spray~D:
This thesis uses top-view data of multi-hole marine nozzles under room-temperature pressurized environments, involving no fuel evaporation.
However, the methodological principles of ECN still apply:
Penetration and boundary values depend on post-processing rules, and this dependency must be incorporated into the interpretation of the results.

\subsection{Empirical Models of Transient Nozzle Penetration Physics and Their Limitations}

Traditional empirical spray models have long revolved around penetration, because penetration can compress complex spray evolution into a few physically meaningful low-order quantities.
In the Hiroyasu--Arai framework, the tip penetration is typically expressed using a multi-stage empirical formulation: it grows approximately linearly with time before breakup, and transitions to a square-root scaling after breakup. Its simplified form can be written as
\[
S_{\mathrm{tip}}(t)\propto
\begin{cases}
t, & t<t_b,\\
t^{1/2}, & t\ge t_b,
\end{cases}
\]
where \(t_b\) is the breakup time, and the proportionality coefficients are determined jointly by the pressure difference, nozzle diameter, and fluid properties \cite{HiroyasuArai1990}.
If this proportionality is rewritten as a fittable function practically adopted by the baseline in this thesis, the Hiroyasu--Arai baseline can be expressed as
\[
S_{\mathrm{HA}}(t)=
\begin{cases}
k_v \left( \dfrac{2\Delta P}{\rho_l} \right)^{1/2}t,
& t<t_b,\\[6pt]
k_p \left( \dfrac{\Delta P}{\rho_a} \right)^{1/4}(d_n t)^{1/2},
& t\ge t_b,
\end{cases}
\qquad
t_b=k_b\,\frac{\rho_l d_n}{(\rho_a\Delta P)^{1/2}} .
\]
Here \(d_n\) is the nozzle hole diameter, \(\Delta P=P_{\mathrm{inj}}-P_{\mathrm{ch}}\) is the injection pressure difference, and \(\rho_l\) and \(\rho_a\) are the fuel and ambient gas densities, respectively.
In the literature, \(k_v\), \(k_p\), and \(k_b\) can take empirical constants; in the baseline comparisons of the results chapter in this thesis, these three dimensionless coefficients are recalibrated on the training splits, providing a fair baseline specifically tailored to the current dataset for this empirical equation.
The physical meaning of this function is clear: before breakup, it is controlled by the nozzle exit velocity; after breakup, it is jointly controlled by momentum flux, ambient gas density, and nozzle hole scale.

The Naber--Siebers correlation retains a similar momentum scaling but places more emphasis on the impact of ambient gas density and spray dispersion angle on penetration \cite{NaberSiebers1996}.
When implemented as a baseline in this thesis, to avoid introducing angle priors that cannot be reliably observed across all samples, a reduced-order form with time delay is adopted:
\[
S_{\mathrm{NS}}(t)
=
k_{\mathrm{NS}}
\left(\frac{\Delta P}{\rho_a}\right)^{1/4}
d_n^{1/2}
\left[\max(t-\tau,0)\right]^{1/2},
\]
where \(k_{\mathrm{NS}}\) is the fitted gain and \(\tau\) is the effective start delay.
If a full angle correction is used, the expansion angle \(\alpha\) can alter the same momentum scale through an approximation factor \((\tan(\alpha/2))^{-1/2}\);
however, the Naber--Siebers baseline reported in this thesis does not use training set cone angles or oracle cone angles, but only fits \(k_{\mathrm{NS}}\) and \(\tau\), thus maintaining the same input availability as other models.

The third type of reference is not a closed-form spray physics equation, but a Gaussian process (GP) non-parametric regression baseline \cite{RasmussenWilliams2006}.
It treats penetration or scaled penetration as a stochastic function driven by input features,
\[
f(\mathbf{x})\sim\mathcal{GP}\!\left(m(\mathbf{x}),\,K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}')\right),
\qquad
S(\mathbf{x})=A\,f(\mathbf{x}),
\]
where \(A=(\Delta P/\rho_a)^{1/4}d_n^{1/2}\) is the same Hiroyasu--Arai post-breakup amplitude mentioned above, and \(\mathbf{x}\) includes standardized features such as time, spray inclination angle, number of nozzle holes, injection duration, and control back pressure.
The adjustable objects of a GP are not a few physical coefficients, but kernel function hyperparameters: amplitude, length scales, noise level, and the individual length scales for each input dimension under the Automatic Relevance Determination (ARD) setting.
The sparse heteroscedastic GP baseline in this thesis further provides \(\mu_S(t)\) and \(\sigma_S(t)\) jointly using a mean GP and a log-residual-variance GP, and is extended to large sample tables via sparse variational approximation \cite{HensmanFusiLawrence2013}, making it a strong statistical baseline rather than a traditional empirical correlation.
The commonality of these three baselines is that they do not directly read image pixels, but rather read the penetration observations and test matrix features exported by the same image processing chain; the difference lies in that Hiroyasu--Arai and Naber--Siebers restrict the penetration to explicit physical function families, while the GP learns a more flexible conditional function in the feature space through kernel functions.
The advantage of these three baselines is their ability to provide interpretable or statistically controlled references for the overall penetration history.
Their limitations, however, mainly emerge during short injection events.
Research by Zhou et al. on injection start transients shows that when sac pressurization and needle valve opening processes are non-negligible, the early penetration behavior is more complex than classical two-stage laws;
During the start of injection (SOI) phase, penetration initially approximates a \(t^{3/2}\) scaling, then transitions to \(t^{3/4}\), and only later gradually approaches a \(t^{1/2}\) behavior \cite{ZhouLiYi2021}.
For this thesis, this is not merely general background information, but a direct constraint:
The diesel pre-injection duration is short enough that the early transients occupy a significant portion of the observable signal.
Therefore, any single and rigid empirical penetration law struggles to comprehensively describe the entire trajectory.

Penetration studies after the end of injection further illustrate that what changes across stages is not just the power-law exponent, but also the relevant time variable itself.
Zhou et al.\ showed that after EOI the tip penetration enters a deceleration branch in which the time coordinate shifts from the ASOI clock $t$ to a delayed clock $(t-t_i)$, with exponents transitioning from $t^{1/2}$ to $(t-t_i)^{1/2}$ and $(t-t_i)^{1/4}$ \cite{ZhouLiLaiWei2019}.
The full four-branch piecewise expression is reproduced in Chapter~4 where it is used as a baseline reference.
The physical implication is that the effective time origin itself carries physical meaning: the power-law exponent alone does not characterise the penetration regime, because the clock from which time is measured also changes at each transition.
In short pre-injections, the finite observation window may simultaneously contain both SOI-referenced and EOI-referenced clock segments, and the switching moments are difficult to reliably identify from noisy Mie-extracted trajectories.

The failure of the quasi-steady picture is not determined solely by fluid mechanics; the upstream nozzle hardware itself introduces additional dynamics.
In common-rail injection systems, the opening and closing of the needle valve are mechanical processes of finite duration;
before the needle valve is fully lifted, the effective flow area varies with time, continually modulating the instantaneous injection rate and the near-nozzle flow field \cite{StumppRicco1996,HiroyasuArai1990}.
Internal nozzle flow under high pressure and potential cavitation further alter the jet structure and atomization state, mechanisms which classical penetration correlations do not attempt to characterize \cite{liu2020sprayreview}.
For short-pulse pre-injections, the opening and closing transients themselves may occupy a considerable proportion of the injection event, rendering the quasi-steady assumption valid only fleetingly in the middle segment at best.
This leads to the modeling judgment in this thesis: rigid empirical power laws remain valuable physical baselines but do not constitute the complete target form for the dataset in this thesis.
Constrained by physical scaling and auditable preprocessing, data-driven models can absorb the synthetic effects of multiple overlapping physical mechanisms on the observed trajectories without needing to pre-identify and parameterize various transient segments.

\subsection{Image-to-Data Workflow for Mie Videos: Processing, Metric Extraction, and Plume Registration}

\subsubsection{Parallel Computing and Mie-Optics-Based Image Processing and Feature Extraction}

This thesis deals not with a small number of static spray images, but with a terabyte-scale archive of 12-bit grayscale Mie videos.
Each raw video is naturally represented as a 3D video tensor \(V\in\mathbb{R}^{F\times H\times W}\);
after rotation, cropping, masking, and plume-by-plume extraction, this batch of information is reorganized into a 4D aligned tensor per plume \(I\in\mathbb{R}^{P\times F\times H\times W}\).
To stably convert the raw videos into comparable plume-by-plume observables, the processing chain must sequentially complete manual calibration, log-domain background subtraction and foreground construction, gradient enhancement, polar coordinate aggregation, rotational registration, inverse remapping, and geometric measurement.
Most of these operations, including background subtraction, local filtering, gradient computation, angular binning, thresholding, morphological cleanup, and cumulative distribution computation, need to be repeatedly executed over a large number of independent pixels, angular bins, and frames.
Therefore, these operations not only have a clear image processing rationale but are also naturally suited for tensorized CUDA parallel implementations organized under the Single Instruction Multiple Thread (SIMT) execution model of GPU hardware \cite{NickollsBuckGarlandSkadron2008,GonzalezWoodsDIP,Szeliski2010Vision}.

The starting point of the processing chain is manual nozzle calibration, because downstream angular analysis defaults to fixing the nozzle center in image coordinates.
This task is essentially noisy circle fitting, for which least-squares circle estimation is a standard tool \cite{Pratt1987CircleFit,Taubin1991CircleFit}.
In this thesis, choosing manual calibration does not mean abandoning automation, but is based on the experimental condition that the high-speed camera positions are fixed and the same setup is heavily reused:
once a common origin is determined, it can be inherited by the entire batch of videos.
For this type of data, minor drifts in the nozzle center propagate systematically into subsequent angular binning, harmonic phase, and plume-by-plume geometric measurements.
In practice, automated circle detection methods like RANSAC or Matlab's \texttt{imfindcircles} work on ideal images, but are prone to interference from glare, unclosed edges, and local occlusion in the current spray images.
Therefore, trading a single manual verification for a consistent geometric reference frame across a batch of data is a more secure engineering tradeoff.

Building upon this, log-domain background subtraction and foreground construction draw on the basic idea of homomorphic signal processing:
when the image formation process contains multiplicative components, taking the logarithm transforms multiplicative disturbances into additive ones, thereby improving the stability of subsequent background subtraction and filtering \cite{OppenheimSchaferStockham1968,GonzalezWoodsDIP}.
This choice also carries direct numerical significance in this thesis.
Under an approximately multiplicative model \(I(x,y,t)\approx L(x,y)\cdot S(x,y,t)\), taking the logarithm converts the illumination field \(L\) into an additive term removable by subtraction of a background log-image.
In practice, additive photon shot noise and read noise mean the model is only approximately multiplicative; the $\epsilon$-regularised form \(\log(I+\epsilon)-\log(B+\epsilon)\) handles the noise floor and ensures numerical stability in low-intensity regions at the cost of a slight departure from exact homomorphic equivalence.
This operation can be understood as a constrained flat-field correction:
it prioritizes attenuating the common multiplicative background unrelated to the spray event, rather than altering the single-frame display appearance.
Directly performing pixel division on spray images easily produces unstable ratios in low-brightness regions;
direct subtraction struggles to adequately eliminate multiplicative illumination changes.
The purpose of entering the log domain is not to interpret Mie intensities as concentrations, but to simultaneously suppress multiplicative illumination and gain drift, avoiding the foundation of subsequent boundary extraction on numerically fragile raw brightness ratios.

\subsubsection{Metric Definitions and Boundary Extraction in Spray Imaging}

Following foreground construction, Sobel-like gradient magnitude operators, percentile scaling, automated thresholding, and morphological operations are sequentially applied to enhance and extract the spray boundaries while suppressing diffuse low-frequency background structures \cite{GonzalezWoodsDIP,Szeliski2010Vision,ZackRogersLatt1977}.
The goal of this step is not to recover absolute brightness ground truths, but to stably delineate the boundary geometry.
For Mie videos, effective liquid-phase boundaries typically first manifest as local contrast changes rather than a globally consistent absolute gray value.
Thus, transforming the raw intensities into a gradient-dominated representation aligns better with the observation goals of this thesis than defining the foreground directly in the brightness domain.
The difference between a Sobel gradient and gamma correction or display gain is that the former is a fixed spatial differential operator serving boundary geometry, while the latter are display or adaptive enhancements that alter the grayscale metric relationships.
This judgment is consistent with boundary extraction experience in existing spray imaging research.
Johnson et al. pointed out that traditional threshold-based cone angle estimation from back-scattered diesel images is highly sensitive to the chosen intensity threshold, proposing contour-based methods to reduce this subjectivity \cite{JohnsonNaberLee2012}.
Existing studies thereby indicate that spray boundary and cone angle estimations cannot be defined independently of the specific imaging modality, post-processing rules, and research objectives.
Accordingly, this thesis treats boundary metrics as operational geometric descriptors rather than direct measurements of a precise liquid-phase envelope;
the penetration front is established on the cumulative distribution support of the spray plume, supplemented by multiple boundary proxy metrics, avoiding reliance on a single breakup pixel at the very front or a single global fixed threshold.

\subsubsection{Multi-Plume Registration and Symmetry Utilization}

The multi-hole nozzle further distinguishes the computational tasks from generic single-jet segmentation.
There is no single isolated jet to directly track in the images; rather, multiple spray plumes unfold around a common nozzle center, possessing a known number of holes and an approximately repeating angular structure.
More importantly, a multi-hole spray is not a mechanical replication of a single-hole spray \(N\) times:
internal sac flows, inter-hole interactions, and plume-to-plume disturbances alter the final morphology, differences which single-hole models struggle to encompass a priori \cite{JinKimKakamiNishidaOgataLuo2020,MopuriGrahnSedarskyHyvonen2024}.
Furthermore, single-hole configurations studied with DBI or Schlieren typically require the remaining holes to be physically plugged, which fundamentally alters the internal sac flow symmetry and may change cavitation characteristics and SOI/EOI momentum dynamics relative to the production multi-hole geometry; data from such configurations is therefore not directly representative of production nozzle spray behaviour.
Therefore, a key task in this thesis is not only detecting which pixels belong to the spray, but also stably registering the repeating plumes across frames, records, and operating conditions.
To this end, the workflow transitions from Cartesian image coordinates to nozzle-centered polar coordinates, folding the 2D repeating structure into a 1D occupancy signal along the angular axis \cite{Szeliski2010Vision}.
Under this representation, the number of holes defines the natural angular frequency, and the phase of the corresponding primary harmonic provides an estimate of the rotational offset (modulo the inter-plume angular spacing); this polar--Fourier phase-correlation approach follows established image registration principles \cite{KuglinHines1975PhaseCorrelation,DeCastroMorandi1987RotationFFT}.
The crux of this design lies not in using the FFT itself, but in explicitly utilizing the nozzle symmetry:
after polar expansion, the multiple spray boundaries previously scattered across the 2D image are compressed into a periodic signal on the angular axis, and the primary harmonic corresponding to the hole number naturally carries the rotational offset information.
Subsequent inverse remapping transforms the aligned results into a dense, plume-by-plume strip representation, preventing the holes produced by naive forward mapping and providing a unified coordinate system for batch feature extraction per plume.

Elevating these operators from mere image processing steps to methodological choices in this thesis hinges on the scale at which they operate.
When this processing chain is applied to a terabyte-scale 12-bit video archive, serial execution frame-by-frame would make cumulative costs dominate the entire workflow, rendering subsequent calibration adjustments, target definition rewrites, and model stage switches unbearable.
GPUs are particularly suited for dense array operations, template-based neighborhood computations, and repetitive map-reduce pattern workloads, as the same arithmetic pattern can be executed in parallel over vast numbers of data elements \cite{NickollsBuckGarlandSkadron2008}; the operators used in this thesis are themselves standard image processing primitives \cite{GonzalezWoodsDIP,Szeliski2010Vision}.
Consequently, the tensorized CUDA implementation is a methodological decision: it transforms the same processing chain into an iterative research instrument that can be repeatedly re-run alongside calibration methods, target definitions, and model stages, rather than a one-time extraction script.

\subsection{Surrogate Model Learning Workflow: Target Construction, Phased Training, and Probabilistic Evaluation}

\subsubsection{Why Deterministic, Uncertainty-Aware, and Teacher-Guided Learning Stages Are All Necessary}

In recent years, data-driven surrogate models have started being used for the rapid prediction of macroscopic spray characteristics.
Existing research demonstrates that when inputs are compressed into operating parameters, nozzle geometries, and a few macroscopic descriptors, artificial neural networks can learn the mapping between penetration, cone angles, other spray geometries, and operating conditions without explicitly providing closed-form empirical laws.
Based on high-speed Mie scattering experimental data in a constant-volume spray chamber, Cheng et al. utilized artificial neural networks to predict key geometric parameters of multi-hole nozzle sprays;
their data inherently included penetration, cone angle, spray tip velocity, area, as well as plume-to-plume and shot-to-shot variations, thus it can be viewed as an experimental ANN surrogate model closely aligned with the problem in this thesis \cite{ChengAhmadGrahn2023}.
Zhang et al. subsequently constructed a GA-BP neural network using experimental diesel spray penetration data and reported that its prediction accuracy surpassed traditional empirical formulas \cite{ZhangWuXu2024}.
In scenarios closer to marine injector design screening, Vaz et al. further used neural networks to directly predict spray penetration and cone angle from operating and geometric parameters, supporting rapid design space exploration for marine injectors \cite{VazKarathanasis2026}.

The aforementioned studies collectively demonstrate that compact regressors, represented by multi-layer feedforward neural networks, are already capable of assuming the role of surrogate models for spray macroscopic quantities.
Particularly crucial for this thesis is that what the models truly need to learn is not the raw video volumes or high-dimensional spatiotemporal textures, but the low-dimensional engineering features and temporal parameterizations obtained after image processing.
Therefore, once the inputs are structured into operating parameters, time, and physically constrained observables or their scaled representations, the problem is much closer to low-dimensional nonlinear regression rather than an end-to-end video understanding task that must rely on recurrent networks, long short-term memory, or large-scale attention mechanisms.
Based on this judgment, the Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) is adopted as a reasonable baseline architecture in this thesis:
it retains universal function approximation capabilities \cite{Hornik1989UAT}, while providing a secure compromise among training cost, implementation complexity, and engineering auditability \cite{BishopPRML2006}.

However, there remain key gaps between existing data-driven spray research and this thesis.
Most existing studies treat the training targets as fully observable spray macroscopic quantities and focus primarily on the point prediction accuracy of penetration or cone angle;
even when using experimental data, it is generally assumed that the late-stage trajectories are not systematically truncated, or at least this truncation is not explicitly modeled as right-censoring.
Furthermore, such studies rarely treat input-dependent uncertainty as a primary output target, nor do they often discuss how to avoid systematic underestimation when late-stage observations are incomplete.
The constraints in this thesis are stricter:
the visible trajectories of short-pulse pre-injections are dominated by SOI and EOI transients, a restricted field of view renders a significant portion of trajectories as right-censored samples, and plume-to-plume as well as shot-to-shot dispersions are themselves engineering-relevant information that cannot be simply averaged away.
Thus, this thesis does not aim to re-prove that neural networks can predict spray macroscopic quantities, but rather introduces three targeted components atop existing pathways:
censoring-aware target construction, heteroscedastic uncertainty quantification, and a distillation-based refinement stage.

\subsubsection{Right-Censoring and Stable Target Construction}

Among the three, censoring-aware target construction is unavoidable due to the geometric constraints of the experimental data used in this thesis.
The choice of data is dictated not merely by historical evolution, but jointly by the required observables and hardware constraints.
Under cold, non-evaporating conditions, Mie scattering is sensitive to forward and side scattering of droplets and provides high contrast at liquid-phase boundaries, thus matching best with the penetration and cone angle observables extracted in this thesis; this has been discussed in the optical diagnostic methods comparison above.
More decisively, hardware-level constraints exist: both Schlieren and DBI require a transmissive optical path through front and rear quartz windows, restricting the configuration to a single-hole geometry (with other holes physically plugged). Even if a full window diameter of 180\,mm is achievable, simultaneous observation of multiple plumes is structurally excluded.
The top-view Mie configuration adopted in this thesis enables all $N$ spray plumes ($N=9$, 10, or 11, depending on the nozzle) to be visible simultaneously in a single record;
combined with five repetitions per operating condition, each experiment provides 45, 50, or 55 penetration time series per condition---this is the sample scale necessary for data-driven modeling of plume-to-plume variations.
This configuration subsequently brings a field-of-view tradeoff that must be explicitly acknowledged: an observation window diameter of 180\,mm (radius 90\,mm) leaves an effective horizontal observation penetration of roughly 80--85\,mm after excluding the masked region near the nozzle;
even if an umbrella angle correction is applied to the acquired projected penetration to recover the 3D axial path length (typical correction factor ~1.05--1.2), the resulting trajectory lengths are still less than the radial distance to the cylinder wall for the smallest W20DF (bore 200\,mm, radius 100\,mm) among Wärtsilä's in-service dual-fuel diesel pre-injection products.
Therefore, the right-censoring of all trajectories in this thesis is not caused by a mismatch of experimental configuration on a specific engine, but is a structural limitation of top-view diagnostics for real-scale engine cylinder wall observations.
This assertion applies to the scenario where the spray reaches the cylinder wall/piston crown; impacts within the piston bowl are outside this scope.

The impact of a finite field of view is not merely cropping the image scope; it alters the statistical properties of the labels.
When the true late-stage penetration crosses the visible window, the recorded trajectory does not merely contain additional noise, but is right-censored by the observation boundary.
This structure is well-discussed in statistics: limited or censored dependent variable literature specifically focuses on problems where observations serve as restricted representations of underlying true quantities \cite{Tobin1958,BuckleyJames1979}.
Although this literature mostly stems from econometrics with a different context from spray imaging, the core structure is identical:
the dependent variable of interest can only be observed below a certain threshold, and information beyond the threshold is compressed or lost.
In this thesis, this threshold is the camera's field-of-view boundary.

Figure~\ref{fig:right-censoring-example-en} provides a representative example from the current workflow.
Some plumes have already crossed the upper boundary of the observation window, leaving only blurred annular edge traces near the boundary;
other plumes remain entirely within the visible region.
If interpreted purely based on surface image readings, the "terminal penetration" of the former is actually determined by the camera's field of view, not by the spray's physical processes.

This distinction directly impacts surrogate model training.
If the visible endpoints of censored trajectories are treated as ordinary regression labels, the target values will be systematically depressed in late-stage regions that should have continued growing.
The model might consequently be trained to interpret "leaving the field of view" as "physical saturation."
In a dataset where censored and uncensored trajectories are mixed, this label construction produces unstable late-stage gradients and artificially flattens the penetration curves.
The problem here is not inadequate model expressiveness, but biased supervision signals.

From a statistical modeling perspective, the Tobit model is the classical starting point for handling censored dependent variables \cite{Tobin1958}.
If the goal of this thesis were merely to predict a censored scalar at a fixed time, Tobit-style regression would naturally be a baseline worth prioritizing.
However, this thesis deals not with a single scalar, but with short-duration penetration trajectories jointly shaped by SOI, EOI, and nozzle transients.
For late-stage samples that have crossed the field-of-view boundary, the Tobit likelihood primarily provides a one-sided constraint that "the true value is higher than the censoring boundary,"
but cannot provide shape information on "how the trajectory should continue to grow after crossing the boundary."
When late-stage uncensored samples are already scarce and conservative extrapolation is inevitable, such gradients more easily help the model learn "whether it crossed the boundary,"
but are insufficient to stably reconstruct "to what extent it extends after crossing."
Given the data conditions of this thesis, this information structure is unsuitable as the primary basis for late-stage trajectory reconstruction.

The broader survival regression literature offers additional tools for censored outcomes: Accelerated Failure Time (AFT) models parameterise the censored outcome under distributional assumptions more flexible than the Gaussian Tobit; inverse probability of censoring weighting (IPCW) approaches re-weight complete cases to account for the censoring mechanism; and Gaussian process regression with censored likelihoods has been developed for exactly this combination of GP modelling and right-censored targets \cite{Groot2012CensoredGP}.
However, all of these handle censored scalar outcomes at a fixed time, not censored trajectory shapes.
The key challenge here is that ``how the trajectory continues after crossing the boundary'' requires shape information that one-sided likelihood constraints cannot provide; the teacher-guided stage described below addresses this gap specifically by learning the trajectory continuation from manually extrapolated synthetic data.

The motivation for uncertainty-aware extensions is that not every operating point or extracted trajectory holds the same reliability.
Heteroscedastic Gaussian negative log-likelihood is a standard approach, allowing the network to simultaneously predict the mean and an input-dependent variance \cite{KendallGal2017}.
In regression form, it corresponds to the conditional model
\[
y=\mu(x,t)+\varepsilon(x,t),\qquad \varepsilon(x,t)\sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2(x,t)),
\]
where the Gaussian distribution should be understood as a practical statistical assumption rather than a claim that spray physics truly obeys a Gaussian distribution at every instant \cite{BishopPRML2006,KendallGal2017}.
What this thesis truly needs is a way to express the synthetic residual of unresolved factors and separate this residual from the conditional mean trajectory;
otherwise, the model would be forced to squeeze all inter-plume and cycle-to-cycle variations into a single mean function.

However, relying solely on heteroscedastic regression does not completely eliminate the systematic underestimation caused by right-censoring.
If raw partially observed trajectories are directly used for supervision, even if the network can output variance, its late-stage mean might still be pulled down by the field-of-view boundary.
Therefore, this thesis further adopts a teacher-guided distillation-style refinement:
First, a heteroscedastic teacher model is trained on the aforementioned "manually fitted and conservatively extrapolated" synthetic trajectories, causing it to learn a censoring-aware late-stage continuation;
then, this teacher model is used to constrain a student model oriented toward raw CDF observations.
In this framework, reliable early- and mid-stage raw observations retain their direct supervisory role, while late-stage sparse or already censored intervals rely more on the soft targets provided by the teacher.
In other words, distillation in this thesis is not a model compression technique in the general sense, but a bias correction mechanism that re-projects the censoring-aware synthetic data back into the raw observation domain.

Gaussian outputs also offer a practical advantage here:
when both the teacher and student output univariate Gaussian distributions, the Kullback--Leibler divergence between them has an analytical closed-form solution:
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(\mathcal{N}(\mu_1,\sigma_1^2)\,\|\,\mathcal{N}(\mu_2,\sigma_2^2)\right)
=\log\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1}+\frac{\sigma_1^2+(\mu_1-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2}-\frac{1}{2},
\]
meaning the distillation loss can be calculated precisely without Monte Carlo sampling, and without introducing extra gradient variance to the training process \cite{BishopPRML2006,KendallGal2017}.
The direction of the KL divergence itself remains a design choice requiring explanation.
This thesis adopts the forward KL direction from teacher to student, \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathrm{teacher}}\|p_{\mathrm{student}})\), primarily because it is algebraically consistent with the Stage~1--2 NLL objective: minimising NLL for a Gaussian model is equivalent to minimising \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathrm{data}}\|p_{\mathrm{model}})\), and applying the same forward direction in Stage~3 maintains this training-objective consistency.
The qualitative mass-covering versus mode-seeking distinction between forward and reverse KL is a general argument from variational inference that reduces, for Gaussian teacher--student pairs specifically, to quantitative differences in the $\sigma$ terms of the closed-form expression above.
In practice, empirical evaluation showed that pure forward KL can create a sigma-arbitrage incentive in which the student lowers the KL loss by inflating its predicted variance $\sigma_2$ rather than improving the mean, leading to under-prediction of late-stage mean trajectories near the field-of-view boundary; the production Stage~3 loss therefore uses a hybrid form combining mean-MSE and log-variance MSE terms, described in Chapter~4.
Kang and Kang (2024) showed specifically for regression tasks that teacher-generated soft targets substantially reduce student errors in under-labelled regions \cite{KangKang2024KDRegression}, which provides direct empirical support for the distillation design adopted here.
Note that Tarvainen and Valpola's mean-teacher mechanism operates via an exponential moving average of the student's own weights \cite{TarvainenValpola2017}, whereas this thesis uses a separately pretrained and frozen teacher model; the shared principle is teacher-guided soft supervision, not the specific EMA update rule.
The broader knowledge distillation literature (Hinton et al.\ 2015 for classification settings \cite{HintonVinyalsDean2015}) established that teacher-guided training can improve student performance in data-sparse regimes; the present regression extension for censored trajectory labels follows the same structural principle.

The resulting learning basis binds right-censoring-aware data construction, heteroscedastic regression for input-dependent uncertainty, and distillation-style bias correction based on a manually fitted and extrapolated teacher into a unified framework.

\subsubsection{Shape Constraints and Physics-Informed Neural Networks}

Beyond right-censoring and uncertainty, the surrogate model must also satisfy fundamental penetration shape constraints.
Purely data-driven smooth MLPs generally yield reasonable interpolations in densely sampled regions;
but in locally sparse or lightly extrapolated areas of the operating space, the model may exhibit non-physical oscillations, localized regressions, or sharp gradients.
Such locally weakly supported regions, for instance, specific combinations of nozzle hole diameters, injection pressures, and durations, do exist in the test matrix of this thesis.
Therefore, relying solely on data losses is insufficient to guarantee that trajectory shapes remain physically credible at all times.

Encoding physical priors into neural network training objectives is a representative paradigm in the Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) literature \cite{Raissi2019PINNs}.
The core mechanism of Raissi et al.'s work lies in utilizing Automatic Differentiation to compute partial derivatives of network outputs with respect to inputs,
and adding these derivative-related terms as soft penalties to the loss function.
In this way, partial differential equations or empirical physical laws can serve as constraints, guiding the network to stay within a reasonable function space even in data-sparse regions.

This thesis does not attempt to use neural networks to solve full Navier--Stokes equations.
Rather, it borrows the constraint concept of PINNs:
first- and second-order temporal derivatives of the penetration mean are obtained via automatic differentiation, and corresponding soft penalties are added to the loss.
The first-order derivative constraint is used to suppress unphysical negative velocities, while the second-order derivative constraint encourages concavity throughout the injection and post-injection deceleration phases, meaning the penetration growth rate decays with time.
This also explains why smooth activation functions like Swish/SiLU mentioned earlier are necessary:
only when the second-order derivatives can be stably and smoothly propagated is a curvature-based physical shape constraint suitable for gradient descent optimization.

\subsubsection{Evaluating Probabilistic Predictions: Proper Scoring Rules and Model Calibration}

Having the network output both mean and variance does not automatically imply that its uncertainty is reliable.
The evaluation criteria for probabilistic predictions differ from deterministic regression:
Mean Squared Error or Mean Absolute Error primarily evaluates point predictions, whereas the predictive distribution must be tested for both accuracy and spread simultaneously.
Therefore, evaluating probabilistic models requires the use of Strictly Proper Scoring Rules.
Gneiting and Raftery's work showed that a proper scoring rule attains its expected optimum only when the predicted distribution aligns with the true conditional distribution \cite{GneitingRaftery2007CRPS}.
The Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS) is a commonly used metric of this kind:
it simultaneously penalizes mean bias and overly broad predictive distributions, forcing the model to be accurate while maintaining appropriate sharpness.

Moreover, modern deep networks can exhibit overconfidence even if their point prediction accuracy is high:
the nominal probability of a prediction interval does not necessarily equal the empirical frequency of true observations falling within that interval.
Guo et al. systematically diagnosed this phenomenon and emphasized the necessity of post hoc probability calibration and empirical coverage checks \cite{Guo2017Calibration}.
In the context of this thesis, a simply trained heteroscedastic network might yield overly small variances under certain conditions,
causing the actual observations to fall outside the prediction intervals more frequently than the nominal level indicates.
Therefore, a surrogate model aimed at early design screening must verify uncertainty calibration on a hold-out set;
for out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization, empirical coverage (e.g., $1\sigma$ and $2\sigma$ coverage) should also be checked via spatial cross-validation, such as holding out complete hardware families.
This constitutes the basis for adopting CRPS, reliability diagrams, and Leave-One-Nozzle-Family-Out (OOD) diagnostics in the subsequent results evaluation.

\subsubsection{Differences Between Kernel Methods Baselines and Neural Surrogates Outside the Distribution}
\label{sec:kernel-vs-nn-ood-en}

Choosing between kernel method baselines, such as Sparse Variational Gaussian Process (SVGP), and neural surrogates is not merely a matter of capacity or implementation cost; the two types of models inherently carry different inductive biases, and these differences only become apparent when test inputs deviate from the training support.
A Gaussian process employing an ARD Mat\'ern kernel and inducing point variational posteriors is essentially a Bayesian model in function space: the kernel function explicitly encodes a smoothness prior, and the predictive mean will smoothly revert to the prior mean as features move away from the training inducing points, while the predictive variance increases in the same direction \cite{RasmussenWilliams2006,HensmanFusiLawrence2013}.
Thus, the out-of-distribution behavior is conservative by default---not because the model is meticulously tuned for it, but because wherever the data cannot constrain it, the prior naturally dominates.
Neural surrogates have no equivalent fallback mechanism: a trained network is a deterministic nonlinear extrapolator, whose values outside the distribution are governed jointly by the learned function geometry and the shape or anchor priors added during training.
From the perspective of Bayesian Deep Learning, Wilson and Izmailov point out that this is a general disparity rather than an architectural quirk: kernel methods naturally achieve mild generalization via their priors, whereas neural networks require explicit calibration to approximate similar behaviors \cite{WilsonIzmailov2020BDL}.
Large-scale empirical studies by Ovadia et al. confirmed the practical consequences under distribution shifts: deep models, including heteroscedastic forms, often exhibit overconfidence or inherit the biases of the training distribution when the test distribution shifts, whereas probabilistic baselines lose accuracy much more gracefully \cite{Ovadia2019Trust}.
For this thesis, these references serve merely as general background rather than core arguments, as the contribution of this thesis lies in right-censoring-aware learning protocols and screening interfaces, not in the choice of regressors itself.
The significance is methodological: the Leave-One-Nozzle-Family-Out evaluation in Chapter 5 should not be read as an architectural showdown with only one winner.
When the held-out nozzle family indeed falls outside the training support, the kernel method baseline will be more conservative a priori, while the phased MLP will more aggressively fit the structure of the training distribution and carry this structure to the hold-out folds.
Therefore, the empirical results in Chapter 5 are a consistent manifestation of this "prior versus likelihood" separation, not an accident requiring post hoc explanation, and the thesis presents them in this spirit.

\subsection{Spray Wall Impingement Mechanisms and Probabilistic Impingement Risk Screening}

Penetration control of diesel pre-injection sprays is primarily an issue of combustion organization, not just a problem of impingement avoidance.
In dual-fuel diesel-gas engines, the geometric distribution of the pre-injection spray---including penetration, cone angle, and the extent of liquid-phase spread---directly determines the spatial position and size of the ignition kernel,
and thereby affects whether the flame can efficiently propagate into the premixed main fuel gas charge \cite{Karim2015DualFuel,WeiGeng2016DualFuelReview,Sahoo2009DualFuelReview}.
If the pre-injection penetration is too short, the ignition kernel position deviates from the appropriate zone or its volume is insufficient, which may lead to inadequate main fuel flame propagation, elevated unburned hydrocarbons (e.g., methane slip), and a corresponding drop in overall combustion efficiency;
If the pre-injection penetration is too long, even if definitive impingement has not yet occurred, it may alter the relative position of the ignition kernel to the gas charge, leading to incomplete combustion of the main fuel and increasing the likelihood of liquid-phase wall impingement \cite{WeiGeng2016DualFuelReview,Sahoo2009DualFuelReview}.
In other words, pre-injection penetration is foremost a key control variable for main fuel mixing, ignition kernel placement, and combustion efficiency;
on top of this, it is also the core geometric input for wall-impingement risk screening.
Predictions of pre-injection penetration and its uncertainty in this thesis thus primarily serve the evaluation of pre-injection control and combustion organization, while concurrently supporting wall risk ranking.

In large-bore medium-speed dual-fuel engines such as the Wärtsilä W20DF (bore 200\,mm), this combustion organisation challenge takes a specific form.
At pilot injection timing, the premixed methane--air charge is largely homogeneous throughout the cylinder volume rather than stratified; the pilot spray therefore does not ignite into a locally enriched mixture but must establish an ignition kernel that subsequently propagates through a uniform lean charge.
Penetration in this context primarily controls where in the homogeneous charge the kernel forms: if penetration is too short, the kernel may be confined close to the nozzle tip and fail to grow rapidly enough to consume the bulk cylinder charge within the available crank-angle window.
In large-bore 4-stroke engines, methane slip is closely associated with the gas exchange phase---unburned methane escapes through the exhaust valve during the valve-overlap period rather than being retained in quench zones as in smaller automotive engines.
Karmann et al.\ demonstrated that pilot spray penetration and breakup characteristics in a large-bore dual-fuel engine (MAN 35/44DF, medium-speed 4-stroke) differ substantially from automotive-scale predictions, confirming that penetration models calibrated on small-nozzle data cannot be directly applied at marine-engine scale \cite{KarmannDualFuelOptical2023}.
Zhang et al.\ showed that a 2\,$^{\circ}$CA advance in pilot injection timing measurably changes combustion efficiency and methane slip for a marine natural gas/diesel dual-fuel engine, illustrating how sensitive the combustion organisation is to pilot spray geometric control \cite{ZhangPilotTiming2024}.

When this spatial control fails, the contact between liquid spray and engine walls becomes a consequence that must be addressed.
If liquid fuel reaches the piston crown or cylinder wall before evaporating or igniting, a fuel film may form on the wall, leading to wall wetting, localized mixing deterioration, elevated unburned hydrocarbon emissions, and lube oil dilution \cite{peraza2022swi,ahmad2025fueldilution,MorieiraMotaPanao2010}.
This issue cannot be understood purely from the micro-physical perspective of single-droplet impacts.
Moreira et al. note in their review that while single droplet impact studies have indeed accumulated a wealth of fundamental knowledge,
directly extrapolating it to continuous spray impingement is unsafe, because the collective behavior of a spray plume is further modulated by adjacent droplet interactions, residual wall films, and secondary atomization \cite{MorieiraMotaPanao2010}.
Thus, moving from a single droplet to a plume is not a simple smooth interpolation, but involves real-scale and mechanistic gaps.
At the marine large-bore scale, the problem becomes further complicated:
spray development scales cannot be obtained by simple proportional enlargement, and optical studies on large marine nozzles also indicate that wall constraints significantly alter ignition delays and natural luminous flame intensities \cite{liu2024marine_impingement}.

In engineering, the most direct screening rule is relatively clear:
compare the liquid tip penetration $S_\mathrm{tip}(t)$ with the distance from the nozzle to the wall $L_\mathrm{wall}$; when \(S_\mathrm{tip}(t) \geq L_\mathrm{wall}\), it is flagged as an impingement \cite{Baumgarten2006MixFormation}.
In Baumgarten's systematic treatise on mixture formation in internal combustion engines, penetration correlations are treated as baseline tools for simplifying spray trajectories, and the comparison of penetration with wall distance serves as the first-order impingement criterion \cite{Baumgarten2006MixFormation}.
It is worth noting that subsequent research has not discarded this geometric core but has appended more information to it.
Targeting high-pressure gasoline direct injection, Park et al. reconstructed the radial fuel mass distribution from spray images,
and combined it with injection timing to estimate the mass fraction of fuel impinging on the piston and cylinder wall;
their trend conclusion---that impingement quantity decreases as injection pressure rises and atomization improves---is not surprising in itself; what is more valuable is its methodological implication:
image analysis can provide quantitative impingement estimations without running full CFD \cite{Park2018WallImpingement}.
Under the ECN framework, Peraza et al. used a single-hole nozzle and a temperature-controlled flat wall system to measure spray impingement, characterizing how the penetration-wall distance relationship varies with ambient density and temperature \cite{peraza2022swi}.
These works collectively demonstrate that:
no matter how measurement details evolve, penetration remains the most critical geometric input in impingement screening.

However, for short-pulse pre-injections, treating a deterministic threshold as the primary screening tool is too fragile.
Penetration itself carries uncertainties stemming from needle valve transients, cycle-to-cycle momentum fluctuations, and residual optical measurement noise; the SOI/EOI-dominated stages also make steady-state correlations less reliable over large segments of the observation window.
If a quantity that should be treated as a distribution is compressed into a single value, and then subjected to a binary pass/fail judgment, information useful for ranking candidate operating conditions is concealed.
The approach taken in this thesis represents penetration as a conditional Gaussian distribution \(S_\mathrm{tip}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mu(x,t),\,\sigma^2(x,t))\), and projects this distribution onto a 2D engine geometry plane with the nozzle center as the origin.
Under the premise that lateral expansion is controlled by the spray half-cone angle and approximated by a normal distribution, the probability density of the spray front can be written as a rotated 2D Gaussian.
With this simplified geometry, the wall-impingement risk score can be expressed as the integral of the probability mass falling within the geometric mask regions of the wall and piston.
This output should be understood as a probabilistic screening proxy metric intended for early design ranking, rather than a fully validated production-grade impingement prediction.

The intended engineering application context for this screening output encompasses several complementary use cases:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Nozzle geometry pre-screening:} ranking candidate nozzle families (varying hole diameter, count, and umbrella angle) by impingement probability before committing to prototype manufacture;
  \item \textbf{Injection timing sweep:} evaluating how the risk score varies with pilot start-of-injection timing for a given nozzle, without requiring a separate test campaign for each timing point;
  \item \textbf{Ambient condition sensitivity:} estimating how changes in back pressure or charge density (e.g.\ from alternative fuel blends or different engine load points) shift the impingement risk margin for an existing nozzle design;
  \item \textbf{Operational margin quantification:} expressing the safety margin between the $P_{90}$ penetration envelope and the wall geometry to support pre-injection strategy decisions and fleet-level parameter monitoring.
\end{itemize}
In each case the surrogate replaces or reduces the number of physical spray-chamber experiments or full CFD runs required at the pre-screening stage, providing a fast probabilistic ranking that directs resource-intensive methods toward the most promising candidates.
