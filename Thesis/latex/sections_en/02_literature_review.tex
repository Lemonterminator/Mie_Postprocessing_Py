\section{Literature Review and Technical Rationale}

This chapter treats the literature review as a technical rationale rather than an early repetition of the implementation details. It asks how existing work supports the technical choices made in this thesis: Mie-based geometric observables, short-pulse penetration behaviour, multihole plume registration, right-censored targets, uncertainty-aware surrogate modelling, and probabilistic impingement screening. It also identifies the parts of the problem that are not covered by any single strand of the literature. The purpose is to make the chain from prior knowledge to engineering design decisions explicit before the workflow itself is described.

Readers whose primary background is in fluid mechanics and mechanical design may find a short primer on the four technical domains used in this thesis --- computer vision, robust optimisation, GPU parallel computing, and neural-network training --- useful before reading this chapter. That primer is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:ml-primer}.

\subsection{Mie diagnostics and penetration-oriented observables}

Mie scattering is widely employed as an optical technique for visualising dense liquid sprays,
as it combines the strengths of high temporal resolution and a comparatively simple experimental setup \cite{Linne2013SprayImaging}.
The recorded intensity field, however, is not a direct measurement of fuel mass or liquid volume fraction.
The pixel-level scattering response depends on droplet size, refractive index, wavelength, and viewing geometry;
dense regions add multiple scattering and shadowing on top \cite{BohrenHuffman2004,Linne2013SprayImaging}.
Reconstructing a full physical spray state directly from pixel intensity is therefore an ill-posed inverse problem
and has been treated as impractical for decades.

Optical diagnostic methods employ distinct response mechanisms to capture the structural information of sprays.
Diffused Backlight Imaging (DBI) characterises path-integrated optical thickness via back-illumination extinction,
making it particularly suitable for extracting liquid-phase contours;
Shadowgraphy and Schlieren primarily respond to refractive index or density gradients,
making them more effective for observing gaseous sprays, boundary instabilities, and breakup processes;
Laser-Induced Fluorescence (LIF) characterises vapour distribution or equivalence ratios through fuel or tracer fluorescence;
Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) focuses on velocity fields and turbulent structures;
and Phase Doppler Particle Analysis (PDPA) provides local droplet size and velocity information.

While each of these methods offers distinct advantages,
their applicability to the specific conditions mandated in this work---non-evaporating, non-reacting, room-temperature liquid diesel sprays---is limited.
LIF, PIV, and PDPA primarily focus on chemical composition, flow fields, or point-wise droplet measurements,
and thus cannot directly support the high-temporal-resolution liquid envelope tracking required for this study.
Although Schlieren and Shadowgraphy can provide spray boundaries,
their sensitivity to the liquid-phase boundary is less pronounced than that of droplet-scattering-based methods.
Consequently, DBI and Mie scattering are most closely aligned with the tasks of extracting liquid contours, penetration, and cone angles \cite{Linne2013SprayImaging}.

This thesis therefore adapts long-established experimental spray analysis:
for Mie scattering experiments, since there is no reliable one-to-one mapping from pixel intensity to a physical spray quantity,
the camera intensity is treated as a carrier of geometric cues, not as a stand-in for a thermodynamic field.
Operational observables --- penetration, cone angle, projected area, boundary position ---
are defensible in a way that claims about exact liquid distribution are not \cite{Linne2013SprayImaging,LefebvreMcDonell2017}.
The reason penetration and cone angle have stayed as standard descriptors in diesel spray research is the same:
they are interpretable, and their measurements stay reasonably stable across controlled experimental campaigns
\cite{Dent1971Penetration,HiroyasuArai1990,NaberSiebers1996}.

The same emphasis remains consistent in prior optical spray studies.
Early two-dimensional Mie-scattering work inside optical diesel engines already reported jet-tip penetration and cone-angle information
as the practical outputs of image analysis, rather than attempting a full liquid-state reconstruction \cite{MuenchLeipertz1992}.
Even more recent imaging studies, with improved instrumentation and tighter synchronisation,
still organise liquid-spray comparison around penetration and spreading-angle measurements \cite{JungManinSkeenPickett2015}.
The implication is that a thesis built on Mie videos should be judged first by the quality, reproducibility,
and stated limits of its extracted observables.

The data used in this work were recorded in existing top-view multi-hole nozzle Mie-scattering experiments conducted at the Wärtsilä OSCC.
In contrast to common back-illumination or side-view single-hole isolation configurations,
this experimental setup utilizes lateral window illumination and positions the injector at the rear of the chamber,
thereby allowing all spray plumes to be captured simultaneously within a single field of view.
This configuration is particularly advantageous for preserving the plume-to-plume and shot-to-shot variations essential for the subsequent modelling in this thesis.

Within this framing, penetration in particular serves as the core input for the subsequent trajectory fitting, surrogate modelling, and wall-impingement risk screening
\cite{Linne2013SprayImaging,LefebvreMcDonell2017,Dent1971Penetration,HiroyasuArai1990,NaberSiebers1996},
while the slowly varying halo near the nozzle caused by multiple scattering or ambient reflection is not read as local liquid concentration but as a background component to be suppressed in post-processing.

Mie imaging also brings a characteristic physical limitation from multiple scattering:
in the dense liquid-core region near the nozzle, repeated scattering and internal shadowing elevate the local intensity but contribute almost no sharp directional structure, producing a diffuse halo instead of a clean plume boundary \cite{BohrenHuffman2004,Linne2013SprayImaging}.
This thesis treats the halo as a slowly varying background component rather than local liquid concentration; the corresponding suppression strategy (gradient-domain filtering) and the geometric correction associated with the line-of-sight simplification are described as part of the image-processing workflow in Chapter~3.

Equally important, quantitative processing should preserve the camera's original linear greyscale response as far as possible.
Display-oriented gamma correction, frame-wise display gain, and local contrast enhancement should not be treated as measurement inputs \cite{Cromey2010TwistedPixels}.
Such nonlinear or adaptive grey-level transforms change local gradient ratios, threshold relationships, and the cumulative-distribution support used for front extraction.
The enhancement steps used later in this thesis are therefore fixed, reproducible measurement operations for extracting geometric boundaries from the raw Mie records, not visual adjustments intended to make frames easier to inspect.

A further point worth stressing is that the path from the Mie intensity field to geometric observables depends on the specific measurement rule.
Magnotti and Genzale show that different liquid-penetration metrics lead to materially different conclusions in diesel-spray model validation,
because each metric responds to a different facet of the underlying liquid distribution \cite{MagnottiGenzale2013}.
The reported ``penetration'' is therefore not a universal truth but the result of a particular measurement rule applied to an optical signal.
The Engine Combustion Network (ECN) provides systematic evidence of this method dependence:
ECN tests of nominally identical injectors (Spray~A and Spray~D) at multiple institutions reveal observable differences in liquid penetration across facilities and post-processing definitions,
and explicitly frame the optical liquid boundary as a quantity defined by a specific measurement protocol rather than an absolute representation of the true liquid distribution
\cite{ecn_liquid_penetration,ecn_liquid_penetration_accessory,MeijerECN2012}.
The conditions in this thesis differ from ECN Spray~A's high-temperature, high-pressure evaporative setting and from the Spray~D baseline:
the present data are top-view recordings of multi-hole marine nozzles in a room-temperature pressurised environment without fuel evaporation.
The methodological principle, however, still applies:
penetration and boundary numbers depend on post-processing rules, and that dependence must enter the interpretation of the results.

\subsection{Empirical models of short-pulse spray-penetration physics and their limits}

Classical spray-penetration modelling has long centred on penetration because it compresses the complex spray evolution into a small number of physically meaningful low-order quantities.
In the widely used Hiroyasu--Arai picture, the tip penetration takes a stage-wise form:
one branch before breakup, another after. In simplified proportional form,
\[
S_{\mathrm{tip}}(t)\propto
\begin{cases}
t, & t<t_b,\\
t^{1/2}, & t\ge t_b,
\end{cases}
\]
where \(t_b\) is the breakup time and the prefactors depend on pressure difference, nozzle diameter, and fluid properties \cite{HiroyasuArai1990}.
Written in the fitted form used by the thesis baselines, the Hiroyasu--Arai model is
\[
S_{\mathrm{HA}}(t)=
\begin{cases}
k_v \left( \dfrac{2\Delta P}{\rho_l} \right)^{1/2}t,
& t<t_b,\\[6pt]
k_p \left( \dfrac{\Delta P}{\rho_a} \right)^{1/4}(d_n t)^{1/2},
& t\ge t_b,
\end{cases}
\qquad
t_b=k_b\,\frac{\rho_l d_n}{(\rho_a\Delta P)^{1/2}} .
\]
Here \(d_n\) is the nozzle-hole diameter, \(\Delta P=P_{\mathrm{inj}}-P_{\mathrm{ch}}\) is the injection pressure difference, and \(\rho_l\) and \(\rho_a\) are the fuel and ambient-gas densities.
In the literature, \(k_v\), \(k_p\), and \(k_b\) may be treated as empirical constants; in the baseline comparison of this thesis, these three dimensionless coefficients are recalibrated on the training split to give the empirical law a fair, dataset-specific baseline.
The physical interpretation is direct: the pre-breakup branch is controlled by exit velocity, while the post-breakup branch is controlled by momentum flux, ambient density, and nozzle scale.

The Naber--Siebers correlation preserves a similar momentum scaling but gives explicit importance to ambient-gas density and spray spreading angle \cite{NaberSiebers1996}.
As implemented for the baseline in this thesis, it is reduced to a delayed form so that it does not require an angle observable that is not reliable for every sample:
\[
S_{\mathrm{NS}}(t)
=
k_{\mathrm{NS}}
\left(\frac{\Delta P}{\rho_a}\right)^{1/4}
d_n^{1/2}
\left[\max(t-\tau,0)\right]^{1/2},
\]
where \(k_{\mathrm{NS}}\) is a fitted gain and \(\tau\) is an effective onset delay.
If the full angle correction is used, the spreading angle \(\alpha\) can modify the same momentum scale through an approximate \((\tan(\alpha/2))^{-1/2}\) factor.
The reported Naber--Siebers row, however, uses neither training-set cone angle nor oracle cone angle; it fits only \(k_{\mathrm{NS}}\) and \(\tau\), preserving the same information availability as the other baselines.

The third reference model is not a closed-form spray-physics law but a Gaussian process (GP) non-parametric regression baseline \cite{RasmussenWilliams2006}.
It treats penetration, or scaled penetration, as a random function of the input features,
\[
f(\mathbf{x})\sim\mathcal{GP}\!\left(m(\mathbf{x}),\,K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}')\right),
\qquad
S(\mathbf{x})=A\,f(\mathbf{x}),
\]
where \(A=(\Delta P/\rho_a)^{1/4}d_n^{1/2}\) is the same Hiroyasu--Arai post-breakup amplitude and \(\mathbf{x}\) contains standardised features for time, plume tilt, plume count, injection duration, and control back-pressure.
The tunable objects in the GP are not a small set of physical coefficients but kernel hyperparameters: output scale, length scales, noise level, and, under automatic relevance determination (ARD), one length scale per input dimension.
The sparse heteroscedastic GP baseline used here further combines a mean GP with a log-residual-variance GP to output both \(\mu_S(t)\) and \(\sigma_S(t)\), and uses sparse variational approximation to scale to the large tabular dataset \cite{HensmanFusiLawrence2013}, making it a strong statistical baseline rather than a traditional empirical correlation.
All three baselines consume the same penetration observations and test-matrix features exported by the image-processing chain rather than raw pixels; they differ in how tightly the penetration function is constrained, from the physical forms of Hiroyasu--Arai and Naber--Siebers to the kernel-defined flexibility of the GP.
The appeal of these baselines is practical: together, they provide interpretable or statistically controlled reference points for the gross penetration history.
Their limitation becomes visible when the injection event is short.
The more recent start-of-injection transient analysis by Zhou et al.\
shows that early-time behaviour can be substantially richer than the classical two-stage law when sac pressurisation and needle-opening transients matter.
In their study, the start-of-injection (SOI) stages were found to scale approximately as \(t^{3/2}\) at first, then \(t^{3/4}\),
and only later settle toward the familiar \(t^{1/2}\) behaviour \cite{ZhouLiYi2021}.
The implication for this thesis is direct:
for pilot diesel injections, the injection duration and the first-breakup time scale are short enough that early transients occupy a non-negligible portion of the observed signal,
so any single rigid empirical law is, by construction, an inadequate descriptor of the full trace.

End-of-injection studies push the same point further by showing that the relevant time variable itself changes across regimes.
In Zhou et al.'s extended formulation for tip and tail penetration after end of injection (EOI),
the pre-transition branches are still indexed by the ASOI clock \(t\),
but the decelerating branches carry terms in \((t-t_i)^{1/2}\) and \((t-t_i)^{1/4}\),
with \(t_i\) the injection duration \cite{ZhouLiLaiWei2019}.
Their combined expression for the liquid length scale \(S_L(t)\) reads
\[
S_L(t)=
\begin{cases}
K_v \left( \dfrac{2\Delta P}{\rho_f} \right)^{1/2} t,
& 0 < t \le t_b,\\[6pt]
K_p \left( \dfrac{\Delta P}{\rho_a} \right)^{1/4} d_n^{1/2} t^{1/2},
& t_b < t \le t_i,\\[6pt]
\begin{aligned}[t]
K_p \left( \dfrac{\Delta P}{\rho_a} \right)^{1/4} d_n^{1/2} t^{1/2}\\
\quad - k d_n^{1/2}(t-t_i)^{1/2}(\tan\theta)^{-1/2}\rho_a^{-1/4},
\end{aligned}
& t_i < t \le 2t_i,\\[6pt]
\begin{aligned}[t]
2^{1/2} K_p \left( \dfrac{\Delta P}{\rho_a} \right)^{1/4} d_n^{1/2} t_i^{1/4}(t-t_i)^{1/4}\\
\quad - k d_n^{1/2}(t-t_i)^{1/2}(\tan\theta)^{-1/2}\rho_a^{-1/4},
\end{aligned}
& 2t_i < t.
\end{cases}
\]
In modelling terms, one piece of the penetration law lives on the clock counted from start of injection,
while another piece lives on the clock counted from end of injection
--- or, more precisely, from the moment the decelerating entrainment-wave state starts to dominate at the tip.
That distinction is useful here because it makes clear that the power-law exponent alone is not the full story:
the effective time origin carries physical meaning of its own.
It also makes the case for a softer engineering parameterisation,
because in short pilot injections the observation window can contain both clocks at once and the precise switching instant
is not directly readable from a noisy Mie-derived trace.


The collapse of the quasi-steady picture is not caused by fluid dynamics alone.
The upstream injector hardware adds its own dynamics.
In a common-rail injection system, needle-valve opening and closing are finite-time mechanical transients;
until the needle is fully lifted, the effective nozzle flow area varies with time
and directly modulates the instantaneous injection rate and near-nozzle flow field \cite{StumppRicco1996,HiroyasuArai1990}.
At high injection pressures, internal cavitation inside the nozzle can further alter the jet structure and atomisation characteristics
in ways that classical penetration correlations make no attempt to capture \cite{liu2020sprayreview}.
Both effects are especially pronounced for short pilot injections,
where opening and closing transients can occupy a substantial fraction of the total injection event and the quasi-steady assumption survives,
at best, in a narrow middle window.
The modelling implication is that rigid power-law correlations are valuable baselines but incomplete targets for this dataset.
Data-driven models, when bounded by physical scaling and auditable preprocessing,
do not require the transient regimes to be identified and parameterised in advance,
and they can absorb the combined effect of several overlapping physical mechanisms without assembling a separate piecewise rule for each one.

\subsection{Mie-video image-to-data workflow: processing, metric extraction, and plume registration}

\subsubsection{Parallel computation and Mie-optics-based image processing and feature extraction}

This thesis confronts not a handful of static spray frames but a terabyte-scale archive of 12-bit greyscale Mie videos.
Each recording lives naturally as a video cube \(V\in\mathbb{R}^{F\times H\times W}\);
after rotation, cropping, masking, and per-plume extraction, the same information is reorganised into plume-aligned tensors
\(I\in\mathbb{R}^{P\times F\times H\times W}\).
Turning that raw video stably into comparable per-plume observables requires a chain of operations:
manual calibration, log-domain background removal and foreground construction, gradient-based enhancement,
polar aggregation, rotational alignment, inverse remapping, and binary-shape measurement.
Most of those operations --- background subtraction, local filtering, gradient evaluation, angular binning, thresholding,
morphological cleanup, and cumulative-distribution calculations --- are evaluated again and again over many independent
pixels, angular bins, or frames, and therefore organise naturally as bulk tensor operations under
Single Instruction, Multiple Threads (SIMT) and Single Instruction, Multiple Data (SIMD) execution in a CUDA pipeline
\cite{NickollsBuckGarlandSkadron2008,GonzalezWoodsDIP,Szeliski2010Vision}.

The chain begins with manual nozzle calibration because the downstream angular analysis assumes that the injector centre is fixed in image coordinates.
Since the experimental hardware always presents the nozzle as a co-centred circular feature,
calibration is a noisy circle-fitting problem, and least-squares circle estimation is a standard solution for this class of problem
\cite{Pratt1987CircleFit,Taubin1991CircleFit}.
Choosing manual calibration here is not a rejection of automation but an engineering judgement made under the present experimental conditions:
the high-speed camera is rigidly mounted and the same arrangement is reused across many recordings,
so once a common origin has been fixed it is inherited by the entire batch.
Small drifts of the nozzle centre would otherwise propagate systematically into angular binning, harmonic phase, and per-plume geometric measurements.
In practice, automatic detectors such as RANSAC or Matlab's \texttt{imfindcircles} work on idealised images
but become fragile under glare, broken contour edges, and partial occlusion typical of these spray recordings;
trading a single human verification for a consistent geometric reference frame across the whole batch is the safer engineering compromise.

The log-ratio preprocessing stage borrows directly from homomorphic signal processing.
When image formation contains multiplicative components --- illumination, gain drift, sensor response ---
taking a logarithm turns those multiplications into additions,
after which background subtraction and filtering behave more stably than they would on raw intensity
\cite{OppenheimSchaferStockham1968,GonzalezWoodsDIP}.
The choice has a direct numerical motivation in the present data as well:
if the observed intensity is approximated as \(I(x,y,t)=L(x,y)S(x,y,t)+n\), with \(L\) representing slowly varying illumination, window reflection, or camera-gain structure, then \(\log(I+\epsilon)-\log(B+\epsilon)\) acts as a limited flat-field correction.
It preferentially suppresses event-independent multiplicative background structure rather than changing the appearance of a single displayed frame.
a pixel-wise division on raw intensity produces unstable ratios in low-brightness regions,
while plain subtraction does not adequately remove multiplicative illumination variation.
Working in the log domain is not meant to interpret Mie intensity as concentration; it simultaneously suppresses multiplicative illumination and gain drift
and avoids basing later boundary extraction on numerically fragile raw intensity ratios.

\subsubsection{Metric definition and boundary extraction in spray imaging}

After foreground construction, Sobel-type gradient magnitudes, percentile scaling, automatic thresholding, and morphological cleanup are applied in sequence to enhance plume boundaries while suppressing diffuse low-frequency background structure
\cite{GonzalezWoodsDIP,Szeliski2010Vision,ZackRogersLatt1977}.
The aim is not to recover an absolute intensity field, but to describe boundary geometry in a stable way.
In Mie videos, the effective liquid boundary is usually expressed first as a local contrast change, not as a globally consistent grey-level value.
A gradient-dominated representation therefore matches the observation target better than a brightness-domain foreground.
The distinction from gamma correction or display gain is important: a Sobel gradient is a fixed spatial-differentiation operator serving boundary geometry, whereas display or adaptive enhancement changes grey-scale relationships.
This choice is consistent with earlier spray-imaging work:
Johnson et al.\ showed that conventional threshold-based cone-angle estimation on back-scattered diesel images is highly sensitive to the selected intensity threshold, and proposed contour-based methods to reduce this subjectivity \cite{JohnsonNaberLee2012}.
The present work therefore treats boundary measurements as operational geometric descriptors rather than direct measurements of a precise liquid envelope.
The penetration front is built from the support of per-plume cumulative distributions and is complemented by several boundary proxies, so that the result does not depend on one fragile leading pixel or one globally fixed threshold.

\subsubsection{Multihole plume registration and symmetry exploitation}

Multihole nozzles distinguish this task from generic single-jet segmentation.
There is no isolated jet to track; instead, several plumes unfold around a common nozzle centre, with a known hole count and an approximately repeated angular structure.
More importantly, a multihole spray is not simply \(N\) mechanical copies of a single-hole jet:
internal sac flow, hole-to-hole interactions, and inter-plume disturbances can change the final morphology in ways that single-hole models cannot anticipate
\cite{JinKimKakamiNishidaOgataLuo2020,MopuriGrahnSedarskyHyvonen2024}.
The task is therefore not only to detect spray pixels, but also to register repeated plumes consistently across frames, recordings, and operating conditions.

The workflow accordingly moves from Cartesian image coordinates to nozzle-centred polar coordinates, folding the repeated 2D directional structure into a one-dimensional occupancy signal on the angular axis \cite{Szeliski2010Vision}.
In this representation, the hole count defines a natural angular frequency, and the phase of the corresponding dominant harmonic gives the rotational offset directly \cite{CooleyTukey1965FFT}.
The point of this design is not the FFT itself, but the explicit use of nozzle symmetry:
after polar unfolding, multiple plume boundaries that were scattered across the image plane become a periodic angular signal whose dominant harmonic carries rotational information by construction.
The subsequent inverse remapping turns the aligned result into a dense per-plume strip representation, avoids the holes left by naive forward mapping, and provides a common coordinate system for batch-wise per-plume feature extraction.

The scale of the data turns these operators from convenient image-processing steps into a methodological choice.
When the chain is run over terabyte-scale 12-bit video archives, a serial frame-by-frame implementation would make calibration updates, target-definition changes, and model-stage substitutions prohibitively expensive.
GPUs are well matched to dense-array operations, stencil-like neighbourhood calculations, and repeated map--reduce patterns because the same arithmetic pattern can be applied to many data elements in parallel \cite{NickollsBuckGarlandSkadron2008}.
Since the operators used here are standard image-processing primitives \cite{GonzalezWoodsDIP,Szeliski2010Vision}, the CUDA implementation turns the processing chain into an instrument that can be rerun as the research choices evolve, rather than a one-pass extraction script.

\subsection{Surrogate-learning workflow: target construction, staged training, and probabilistic evaluation}

\subsubsection{Why deterministic, uncertainty-aware, and teacher-guided learning stages are all useful}

Recent data-driven spray studies show that neural networks can act as fast surrogate models for macroscopic spray quantities once the inputs are reduced to operating conditions, nozzle geometry, and a small set of geometric descriptors.
Cheng et al.\ used high-speed Mie-scattering data from a constant-volume spray chamber to predict key geometric quantities of multihole sprays with artificial neural networks; their data include penetration, cone angle, spray-tip velocity, area, and plume-to-plume and shot-to-shot variation, making the setting closely related to the present one \cite{ChengAhmadGrahn2023}.
Zhang et al.\ built a GA-BP neural network on experimental diesel-spray penetration data and reported better accuracy than traditional empirical formulae \cite{ZhangWuXu2024}.
In a setting closer to marine-injector design screening, Vaz et al.\ used neural networks to predict spray penetration and cone angle directly from operating and geometric parameters for rapid design-space exploration \cite{VazKarathanasis2026}.

Together, these studies show that compact feed-forward regressors can serve as spray-macroquantity surrogates.
For this thesis, the important point is that the model does not need to learn directly from raw video volumes or high-dimensional spatio-temporal textures.
After image processing, the learning problem is closer to low-dimensional nonlinear regression over operating conditions, time, and physically scaled observables.
A multilayer perceptron is therefore a reasonable baseline architecture: it retains general function-approximation capacity \cite{Hornik1989UAT} while keeping training cost, implementation complexity, and engineering auditability under control \cite{BishopPRML2006}.

The gap addressed in this thesis is more specific.
Most existing spray-surrogate studies treat the target as a fully observed macroscopic quantity and focus mainly on point-prediction accuracy for penetration or cone angle.
They rarely model late-time field-of-view loss explicitly as right-censoring, and they do not usually make input-dependent uncertainty a primary output.
The present dataset is more restrictive:
short pilot-injection traces are dominated by SOI and EOI transients, the finite field of view turns a substantial part of the trajectories into right-censored samples, and plume-to-plume and shot-to-shot variation is engineering information rather than a nuisance to average away.
The purpose here is therefore not to show again that neural networks can predict spray macroscopic quantities, but to add three components required by this dataset:
censoring-aware target construction, heteroscedastic uncertainty quantification, and distillation-based refinement.

\subsubsection{Right-censoring and stable target construction}

Among these three components, censoring-aware target construction is unavoidable because it follows directly from the geometry of the experiment.
The data choice is jointly determined by the required observables and by hardware constraints.
Under the cold, non-evaporating conditions used here, Mie scattering is sensitive to forward and side scattering from droplets and gives strong contrast at the liquid-phase boundary, making it well matched to the penetration and cone-angle observables discussed above.
The more decisive constraint is optical access:
Schlieren and DBI require a through-path arrangement with front and back quartz windows, which limits the configuration to a single-hole geometry with the remaining holes physically blocked.
Even if a full-window diameter of 180\,mm is available, simultaneous multihole observation is excluded by that geometry.
The top-view Mie configuration, in contrast, makes all $N$ plumes ($N=9$, 10, or 11, depending on the nozzle) visible in one recording.
With five repetitions per operating condition, this yields 45, 50, or 55 penetration time series per condition, which is the sample volume needed to model plume-to-plume variation.

This configuration carries a field-of-view trade-off that must be stated explicitly.
The observation window has a diameter of 180\,mm (radius 90\,mm), and after the region obscured near the nozzle is removed, the effective horizontal observation range is roughly 80--85\,mm.
Even after applying an umbrella-angle correction to recover the 3D along-axis path length from projected penetration (typically a factor of about 1.05--1.2), the available trajectory remains shorter than the cylinder-wall radial distance of the smallest in-service Wärtsilä dual-fuel pilot-diesel product, the W20DF (200\,mm bore, 100\,mm radius).
The right-censoring in this work is therefore not an artefact of a particular chamber-to-engine mismatch, but a structural limitation of top-view diagnostics at engine-relevant scales.
This statement refers to spray reaching the cylinder wall or piston crown; impingement on the piston-bowl inner wall lies outside its scope.

A finite field of view does more than crop the image: it changes the statistics of the label.
When true late-time penetration crosses the visible window, the recorded trajectory is not simply noisy; it is right-censored by the observation boundary.
The same structure is familiar from the literature on limited or censored dependent variables, where the observation is a bounded proxy for a latent quantity of interest \cite{Tobin1958,BuckleyJames1979}.
Although that literature comes mainly from econometrics, the statistical pattern is the same here:
the variable of interest is observable only below a threshold, and information above that threshold is compressed or lost.
In this thesis, the threshold is the camera field-of-view boundary.

Figure~\ref{fig:right-censoring-example} shows a representative example from the current workflow.
Some plumes have already crossed the upper observation boundary and survive only as blurred ring-like edge traces near the limit, while others remain fully inside the visible domain.
Read at face value, the apparent terminal penetration of the censored plumes is set by the camera, not by the spray physics.

This distinction has direct consequences for surrogate-model training.
If the visible endpoint of a censored trajectory is treated as an ordinary regression label, the late-time target is biased downward exactly where penetration should still be growing.
The model is then encouraged to interpret ``leaving the field of view'' as ``physical saturation''.
In a dataset that mixes censored and uncensored trajectories, this target construction creates unstable late-time gradients and artificially flattens the penetration curve.
The problem is not a lack of model capacity; it is biased supervision.

From a statistical modelling perspective, the Tobit model is the classical starting point for censored dependent variables \cite{Tobin1958}.
If the task were only to predict one censored scalar at a fixed time, a Tobit-style regression would be a natural baseline.
Here, however, the target is a short-time penetration trajectory shaped by SOI, EOI, and injector transients.
For late samples that have already crossed the field-of-view boundary, a Tobit likelihood mainly supplies the one-sided information that the true value is above the censoring limit.
It does not say how the trajectory should continue after crossing that limit.
When uncensored late-time data are already sparse and conservative extrapolation is unavoidable, that information structure is insufficient as the main basis for trajectory reconstruction.

The uncertainty-aware extension is motivated by the fact that not every operating point or extracted trajectory deserves the same confidence.
Heteroscedastic Gaussian negative log likelihood lets the network predict both a mean and an input-dependent variance \cite{KendallGal2017}.
In regression form, this corresponds to
\[
y=\mu(x,t)+\varepsilon(x,t),\qquad \varepsilon(x,t)\sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2(x,t)),
\]
where the Gaussian assumption should be read as a pragmatic statistical model, not as a claim that the spray physics are literally Gaussian at every instant \cite{BishopPRML2006,KendallGal2017}.
The purpose is to separate the conditional mean trajectory from unresolved residual variation caused by plume-to-plume differences, cycle-to-cycle fluctuations, and residual optical-processing noise.

Heteroscedastic regression alone, however, does not remove the downward bias caused by right-censoring.
If partially observed raw trajectories are used directly as supervision, the late-time mean can still be pulled down by the field-of-view boundary even when the network also outputs variance.
This motivates the teacher-guided distillation stage:
first, a heteroscedastic teacher is trained on manually fitted and conservatively extrapolated synthetic trajectories so that it learns a censoring-aware late-time continuation;
then, a student model trained against the raw CDF observations is constrained by the teacher.
Reliable early- and mid-time raw observations remain directly supervised, while sparse or censored late-time regions are guided more strongly by the teacher's soft targets.
Distillation is therefore not used here merely as a compression trick; it is a way to project censoring-aware synthetic information back into the raw-observation domain.

The Gaussian output form is also useful computationally.
When both teacher and student produce univariate Gaussian distributions, the Kullback--Leibler divergence between them has a closed form:
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(\mathcal{N}(\mu_1,\sigma_1^2)\,\|\,\mathcal{N}(\mu_2,\sigma_2^2)\right)
=\log\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1}+\frac{\sigma_1^2+(\mu_1-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2}-\frac{1}{2},
\]
so the distillation loss can be evaluated exactly, without Monte Carlo sampling and without adding gradient variance to training
\cite{BishopPRML2006,KendallGal2017}.
The direction of the KL divergence is still a design choice.
This thesis uses the teacher-to-student, or forward, KL direction \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathrm{teacher}}\|p_{\mathrm{student}})\), because it is consistent with the NLL-style objectives used in the earlier stages and encourages the student to cover the teacher distribution in sparse late-time regions.
Reverse KL is more mode-seeking and is less sensitive to thin tails and rare extrapolated trajectories.
Preliminary checks indicated only small prediction differences between the two directions within the operating range of interest, so forward KL should be understood as a deliberate but not separately proven conservative choice.
Recent work on regression distillation and incomplete supervision likewise shows that teacher soft targets can reduce student error where direct labels are missing or unreliable
\cite{HintonVinyalsDean2015,TarvainenValpola2017,KangKang2024KDRegression}.

The resulting learning rationale ties together censoring-aware target construction, heteroscedastic regression for input-dependent uncertainty, and teacher-guided bias correction.

\subsubsection{Shape constraints and physics-informed neural networks}

Beyond censoring and uncertainty, the surrogate model must also respect basic shape constraints implied by penetration physics.
A purely data-driven smooth MLP can interpolate well in densely sampled regions, but in locally sparse or mildly extrapolative parts of the operating space it may produce oscillations, local reversals, or sharp gradients that have no physical support.
The experimental matrix in this thesis contains such locally sparse regions, for example in particular combinations of nozzle diameter, injection pressure, and injection duration.
Data loss alone is therefore not enough to guarantee a physically credible trajectory shape.

Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) provide a representative route for injecting physical prior knowledge into neural-network training \cite{Raissi2019PINNs}.
The core mechanism introduced by Raissi et al.\ is to use automatic differentiation to compute derivatives of the network output with respect to its inputs, and to add derivative-based terms to the loss function as soft penalties.
This allows differential equations or empirical physical laws to act as constraints, guiding the network toward a more plausible function space where data are sparse.

This thesis does not solve the full Navier--Stokes equations with a neural network.
It borrows the constraint mechanism:
automatic differentiation is used to obtain the first and second time derivatives of the predicted penetration mean, and corresponding soft penalties are added to the training objective.
The first-derivative term suppresses unphysical negative tip velocity, while the second-derivative term encourages an overall concave trajectory during injection and post-injection deceleration, meaning that the penetration growth rate decays with time.
This is why smooth activations such as Swish/SiLU matter:
curvature-based constraints are only stable under gradient descent if second derivatives propagate smoothly through the network.

\subsubsection{Evaluation of probabilistic predictions: proper scoring rules and model calibration}

Allowing a network to output both a mean and a variance does not by itself make the uncertainty reliable.
Probabilistic forecasts are evaluated differently from deterministic regressions:
metrics such as MSE or MAE mainly assess point predictions, while a predictive distribution must be judged by both its accuracy and its spread.
This requires strictly proper scoring rules.
Gneiting and Raftery showed that a proper scoring rule reaches its expected optimum only when the predictive distribution matches the true conditional distribution \cite{GneitingRaftery2007CRPS}.
The Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS) is one widely used example:
it penalises both bias in the predicted mean and excessive dispersion in the predictive distribution, encouraging predictions that are accurate without being unnecessarily diffuse.

High-accuracy deep networks can still be overconfident, meaning that their reported probabilities or confidence intervals do not match empirical frequencies.
Guo et al.\ diagnosed this problem systematically and emphasized the need for post-hoc probabilistic calibration and empirical coverage checks \cite{Guo2017Calibration}.
In the present setting, a naively trained heteroscedastic network may output variances that are too narrow under some operating conditions, so that observations fall outside nominal prediction intervals too often.
For an early-design-screening surrogate, uncertainty calibration must therefore be checked on hold-out data.
For out-of-distribution behaviour, it should also be tested through spatial cross-validation, such as leaving out an entire nozzle family, and by comparing empirical $1\sigma$ and $2\sigma$ coverage with their nominal levels.
This motivates the use of CRPS, reliability diagrams, and leave-one-nozzle-family diagnostics in the results chapter.

\subsection{Spray--wall impingement mechanisms and probabilistic collision-risk screening}

Pilot-spray penetration control is first a combustion-organisation problem, not only a wall-avoidance problem.
In dual-fuel diesel--gas engines, the geometric distribution of the pilot diesel spray
--- including penetration, cone angle, and the liquid-phase envelope ---
directly determines the spatial location and size of the ignition kernel,
and thereby affects whether the flame can propagate efficiently into the premixed main-fuel gas charge
\cite{Karim2015DualFuel,WeiGeng2016DualFuelReview,Sahoo2009DualFuelReview}.
If the pilot penetration is too short, the ignition kernel may form in an unfavourable region or occupy too small a volume,
which can weaken main-fuel flame propagation, increase unburned hydrocarbons such as methane slip,
and reduce the overall combustion efficiency.
If the pilot penetration is too long, even before clear wall contact occurs,
it can also shift the relative placement of the ignition kernel and the gaseous charge,
causing inefficient main-fuel burning and increasing the likelihood of liquid-wall contact \cite{WeiGeng2016DualFuelReview,Sahoo2009DualFuelReview}.
Pilot penetration is therefore first a key control variable for main-fuel mixing, ignition-kernel placement, and combustion efficiency;
on that basis, it is also the central geometric input for wall-risk screening.
The penetration and uncertainty predictions developed in this thesis primarily support pilot-injection control and combustion-organisation assessment, while also supporting wall-risk ranking.

When that spatial control fails, liquid-phase spray contact with engine surfaces becomes one of the downstream consequences that must be managed.
When liquid fuel reaches the piston crown or cylinder wall before evaporation or ignition has had time to consume it,
a fuel film deposits on the surface, and the downstream consequences
--- wall wetting, locally deteriorated mixing, elevated unburned-hydrocarbon emissions, and lubricant dilution
--- are well documented across both passenger-car and heavy-duty contexts \cite{peraza2022swi,ahmad2025fueldilution,MorieiraMotaPanao2010}.
The problem does not stay purely microscopic.
Moreira \textit{et al.} note that although single-droplet impact studies provide a substantial knowledge base, direct extrapolation to continuous spray impingement is limited:
a spray plume behaves collectively, and that collective behaviour is shaped by neighbour-droplet interactions, residual wall film,
and secondary atomisation phenomena that do not appear in isolated droplet experiments \cite{MorieiraMotaPanao2010}.
The translation from droplet to plume is therefore a gap, not a smooth interpolation.
The picture changes again at marine scale.
The spatial scale of spray development in large-bore marine diesel engines is not a scaled-up version of an automotive case,
and optical studies with full-size marine injectors confirm that wall confinement materially shifts both ignition delay and natural-luminosity
flame intensity relative to a free-jet configuration \cite{liu2024marine_impingement}.

The simplest screening rule used in engineering practice has barely changed in two decades:
compare the liquid-phase spray-tip penetration $S_\mathrm{tip}(t)$ against the nozzle-to-wall distance $L_\mathrm{wall}$,
and flag impingement once $S_\mathrm{tip}(t) \geq L_\mathrm{wall}$ \cite{Baumgarten2006MixFormation}.
Baumgarten's reference treatment of mixture formation in internal combustion engines codifies this practice,
naming penetration correlations as the baseline trajectory descriptor and the penetration-to-wall-distance comparison
as the default first-order impingement criterion \cite{Baumgarten2006MixFormation}.
Two refinements of this idea are worth singling out.
Park \textit{et al.}\ pushed the criterion further for high-pressure gasoline direct injection by reconstructing the radial fuel-mass distribution
from spray images and combining it with injection-timing data to quantify the fuel-mass fraction actually striking the piston and cylinder wall;
the trend they extract --- impingement falling as injection pressure rises and atomization sharpens --- is itself unsurprising,
but the methodological contribution is more interesting:
it shows that image-based analysis can deliver a quantitative impingement estimate without the computational cost of a full CFD run \cite{Park2018WallImpingement}.
Within the ECN framework, Peraza \textit{et al.}\ measured spray impingement on a controlled-temperature flat wall
using a single-hole injector and characterised how the penetration-to-wall-distance relationship deforms with ambient density and temperature
\cite{peraza2022swi}.
Read together, the two studies confirm a single point:
penetration depth, however it is measured, remains the central geometric input for first-order impingement screening.

For short-pulse pilot injections, that deterministic threshold is too brittle as the primary screening tool.
Penetration here carries non-negligible uncertainty from needle-valve transients,
cycle-to-cycle fluctuations in delivered momentum, and residual optical-measurement noise,
and the SOI/EOI-dominated regime makes steady-state correlations imprecise over a large fraction of the observable window.
A binary pass/fail rule, applied on top of a quantity that is itself a distribution rather than a number,
hides information that is useful for ranking candidate conditions.
The framework proposed in this thesis therefore represents penetration as a conditional Gaussian distribution
$S_\mathrm{tip}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mu(x,t),\,\sigma^2(x,t))$,
and projects that distribution onto a two-dimensional engine-geometry plane centred on the nozzle.
With the lateral spread modelled as a normal distribution governed by the spray half-cone angle,
the spray probability density function takes the form of a rotated two-dimensional Gaussian.
Under that simplified geometry, the collision-risk score is the probability-mass integral of this distribution over the cylinder-wall and piston-crown masks.
The output should therefore be read as a probabilistic screening proxy, not as a validated production-grade impingement prediction.
