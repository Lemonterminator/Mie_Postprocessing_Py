\section{Censoring-Aware Penetration Surrogate Modeling and Probabilistic Screening}

This chapter begins with the plume-resolved observations generated by the image processing workflow, transforming these discrete, partially right-censored measurements into a trainable target, an uncertainty-aware surrogate model, and a simplified geometric screening proxy. The separation between "video-to-observation" and "observation-to-predictor" is intentional, allowing the assumptions and limitations of each layer to be examined independently.

Short pilot injections exacerbate the tension between measured trajectories and trainable targets. Classical empirical penetration laws remain physically valuable, but their applicability narrows when the observation window is dominated by the start-of-injection (SOI) and end-of-injection (EOI) transients. The right-censoring problem identified in Chapter 2 is treated here as a target-construction constraint that propagates through the fitting, heteroscedastic uncertainty, and distillation stages.

\paragraph{Terminological Conventions.}
Four operational labels recur throughout this chapter:
\begin{description}
  \item[\emph{SGQR}.] The three-parameter sigmoid-gated quarter-root single-branch fit defined in Section~\ref{sec:SGQR-target-construction-en}.
  \item[\emph{$A$}.] The single amplitude divisor used for target scaling and physical reconstruction, \(\hat S=S/A\), \(\mu_S=A\hat\mu\), and \(\sigma_S=A\hat\sigma\); its exponents are justified in Section~\ref{sec:feature-engineering-en}.
  \item[\emph{Datasets \#1/\#2/\#3}.] Respectively, the raw cleaned CDF observations, the censor-truncated evaluation points and P50/SGQR oracle, and the post-fit-shielded SGQR trajectory archive; their distinct roles are specified in Section~\ref{sec:three-derived-populations-en}.
  \item[\emph{LONO}.] Leave-one-nozzle-family-out retraining and evaluation; unlike the group-aware in-domain split, it probes generalization to a nozzle family absent from training.
\end{description}
Spatial right-censoring refers specifically to a trajectory reaching its nozzle-specific FOV cap. Density-drop signal loss is a distinct loss of observable Mie support before the cap, although either trigger can truncate Dataset~\#2. \emph{Post-fit shielding} denotes the SGQR plausibility and robust-outlier gate; ``filtering'' remains a generic verb that should always be qualified, while ``screening'' is reserved for the downstream geometric proxy. The \emph{Stage~2 teacher} is the frozen heteroscedastic MLP used to supervise Stage~3; SGQR curves are fitting targets, not themselves a teacher model.

\subsection{Problem Reframing and Observation Conventions}
\label{sec:surrogate-problem-framing-en}

The modeling task in this chapter is intentionally reframed as a low-dimensional probabilistic regression problem rather than a raw image prediction problem. Chapter 3 has already converted the top-view Mie records into plume-resolved temporal observations. Therefore, the surrogate model receives operating conditions, geometric descriptors, and time, and predicts the conditional penetration distribution,
\[
x=\{t,\theta_{\mathrm{tilt}},N_{\mathrm{plume}},t_{\mathrm{inj}},P_{\mathrm{cb}},P_{\mathrm{inj}},P_{\mathrm{ch}},d_n,\rho_a\}
\;\longmapsto\;
\{\mu_S(t),\sigma_S(t)\}.
\]
The scalar target \(S(t)\) is measured in millimeters, and \(\sigma_S(t)\) is interpreted as a heteroscedastic conditional spread rather than a single global residual band. \(\sigma_S(t)\) does not include epistemic uncertainty. Multi-seed retraining probes only the sensitivity of aggregate metrics to initialization and optimization stochasticity; it does not quantify pointwise epistemic uncertainty arising from limited data coverage.

Among the penetration descriptors exported by the image processing workflow, CDF penetration is used as the primary training and evaluation observation. Binary axial penetration \(S_{\mathrm{BW,x}}\) and polar/boundary penetration surrogate \(R_{\mathrm{BW}}\) remain important consistency checks and geometric diagnostics, but they inherit thresholding, morphological, and boundary interpretation choices from the binary support images. The CDF front, in contrast, tracks the axial cumulative support of the cleaned plume strips, making it less sensitive to isolated leading pixels and aligning more directly with the time-series target required by the surrogate. Therefore, unless explicitly marked otherwise, all training and evaluation protocols below use the CDF penetration convention.

This reframing also clarifies the role of uncertainty. The objective is not merely to fit a smooth mean curve through every measured point; the downstream screening task requires a distribution over plausible penetrations at each time point. Consequently, this chapter processes the archive in three complementary ways: as a collection of raw time-indexed measurements, as a statistically truncated point-level evaluation set, and as an archive of smoothed fitted trajectories that can serve as a smooth teacher. The remainder of this chapter will define these data populations, construct the fitting targets, and then compare neural network-based and kernel-based method backbones on the same scaled regression problem.

\subsection{Right-Censoring in Penetration Targets and Three Derived Data Populations}
\label{sec:cdf-populations-en}

\subsubsection{Quantifying How Many Trajectories Are Truncated by the Field of View}
\label{sec:cdf-spatial-censoring-audit}

\newcommand{\scrNCensored}{9630}%

Before constructing fitting targets, we quantify the severity of the CDF penetration right-censoring problem directly from the image processing output. This audit defines censoring spatially, rather than based on the subsequent \(\SI{5}{ms}\) surrogate model horizon. For each nozzle family \(n\), all positive raw CDF penetration values derived from the multi-hole Mie workflow are pooled, and a field-of-view (FOV) cap \(C_n\) is estimated as the \(q=0.999\) quantile. A plume trajectory's raw or cleaned CDF trace is flagged as spatially right-censored if it reaches \(C_n-\epsilon_n\), where \(\epsilon_n=\max(\SI{1}{mm},0.01C_n)\). This audit is implemented in the spatial right-censoring audit script, which compares the raw Mie output in the image processing directory with the fitting targets in the synthetic data export directory (codebase identifiers are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:implementation_mapping}).

The resulting scalar diagnostic is the spatial censoring rate,
\[
\mathrm{SCR}
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\mathbf{1}\!\left[
\max_t S_i^{\mathrm{raw/clean}}(t)
\ge C_{n(i)}-\epsilon_{n(i)}
\right],
\]
where \(n(i)\) is the nozzle family of trajectory \(i\).

The cleaned split contains \(\num{\scrNTotal}\) CDF penetration trajectories, of which \(\num{\scrNCensored}\) satisfy this spatial right-censoring criterion, yielding a spatial censoring rate of \(\scrRatePct\%\). The median nozzle-specific cap is \(\SI{92.52}{mm}\). Among the censored cleaned trajectories, the median time to first hit the cap is \(\SI{1.76}{ms}\), equivalent to \(44.4\%\) of the raw positive observation span. If a naive target construction method simply held the final cleaned CDF penetration value forward, its median gap relative to the accepted SGQR continuation at \(\SI{5}{ms}\) would be approximately \(\SI{\scrNaiveHoldGapMedianMm}{mm}\); if the cap itself were treated as a terminal saturation value, the corresponding median gap would still be \(\SI{43.96}{mm}\). These continuation gaps are not viewed as errors against a wide-field ground truth. They quantify the magnitude of target bias that would be introduced by reading cap-hitting trajectories as physical saturation observations.

\subsubsection{Three Populations Derived from the Cleaned CDF Archive}
\label{sec:three-derived-populations-en}

The cleaned CDF archive is not used as a single homogeneous dataset. Downstream code reads it through three structurally distinct populations, each with its own selection biases and role in the surrogate protocol.

\textbf{Dataset \#1: Raw Cleaned CDF Observations.}
This population consists of the per-frame \((t,S)\) observations that passed Chapter 3's CDF extraction, hydraulic delay alignment, jump truncation, and short-segment filtering. It is stored in long and wide formats in the cleaned CDF sequence exports. The cleaned archive contains approximately \(24{,}316\) post-fit-shielded trajectories and \(703{,}592\) individual frame observations, drawn from a raw inventory of \(30{,}341\) plume trajectories; the per-gate attrition counts on this common raw denominator are reported in Table~\ref{tab:data-attrition-generated}. Its advantage is point-wise fidelity: these rows are the closest objects in the pipeline to the measured penetration records, and they preserve the time-varying noise and inter-plume spread. Its limitation is the censoring quantified above: late-time bins are depleted by FOV hits and signal decay, so raw supervision is only trustworthy when coverage remains high. Thus, Stage~3 uses this population directly only in the reliable raw CDF regime.

\textbf{Dataset \#2: Statistically Truncated Points and P50/SGQR Oracle.}
This population is derived by aggregating the observations in Dataset \#1 per operating condition. A point-level table bins time across \(0\)--\(\SI{5}{ms}\) at \(\Delta t=\SI{0.1}{ms}\) intervals, then truncates each condition at the first time bin satisfying either a density-drop signal-loss criterion or an FOV-cap criterion: the smoothed trajectory count per bin drops below a fixed fraction of its intra-condition peak, or the per-bin \(p_{95}\) penetration reaches \(95\%\) of the estimated FOV cap. The resulting uncensored point set contains about \(506{,}634\) points spanning \(592\) operating conditions. An accompanying P50/SGQR oracle is constructed by taking the per-bin median penetration for each surviving condition and fitting a SGQR to this median trace; it covers \(495\) conditions and provides a condition-level reference curve. This dataset is not used for training. It serves as a lower-bias evaluation yardstick for uncensored CDF accuracy, P50 observation behavior, SGQR extrapolation diagnostics, leave-one-nozzle-out (LONO) evaluation, and the comparison between MLP and Sparse Variational Gaussian Process (SVGP).

\textbf{Dataset \#3: Per-Trajectory SGQR Fitted Archive.}
For every accepted plume trajectory, the reduced-order SGQR model described in Section~\ref{sec:SGQR-target-construction-en} stores the fitted parameters and associated fit diagnostics. This population provides smooth, differentiable, and extrapolatable targets over the surrogate model horizon. Stage~1 selects a representative fitted trajectory from this population for each operating condition group, and Stage~2 trains on all SGQR trajectories that pass post-fit shielding. Dataset~\#3 is therefore the training source for the clean-plume Stage~2 teacher, but it also carries an explicit selection bias: it favors optically clean, monotonic trajectories that are well represented by SGQR.

\subsection{Censoring-Aware SGQR Target Construction}
\label{sec:SGQR-target-construction-en}

\subsubsection{Trajectory Cleaning and Time Alignment}

The prerequisite for fitting and learning is that all penetration curves are aligned with the start of injection (SOI) as time zero: whether it is a polynomial empirical model or a neural network, its fitted parameters are highly sensitive to the time origin. If the start times of individual plumes are scattered across different frames, families of curves under the same operating conditions will exhibit systematic offsets, preventing the model from learning the true penetration dynamics.

The learning model is not directly trained on the raw exported trajectories. Instead, the cleaning and fitting routines first clean and align them. For each plume, the routine finds the earliest valid penetration segment, takes the first two positive samples after onset, fits a line to these early non-zero points, and uses the \(x\)-axis intercept of this line as the sub-frame onset delay estimate if the fitted slope is positive. If an area-based onset marker exists (projected area derived by summing foreground pixels of the binarized spray image per frame and converting via pixel-to-physical scale), this frame anchors the delay sweep; otherwise, it falls back to the first positive penetration frame. The resulting raw delay is then clipped to a small, median-centered window (across plumes) so that a single anomalous plume does not excessively shift the entire group.

This delay estimate is used in two coupled ways. The penetration array itself is shifted left by the nearest integer number of frames, while the aligned time vector retains the fractional residual through an exact offset subtraction. This pipeline performs plume onset registration, preserving sub-frame temporal information prior to the subsequent reduced-order fitting. After alignment, sudden unphysical jumps are removed, and low-quality fits are flagged via a set of rule-based masks. The residual offset between plumes post-alignment typically does not exceed three frames; hydraulic delay (the time difference between the electrical signal and spray appearance) is not formally statistically analyzed across operating conditions, but the temporal magnitude corresponding to this frame-level spread at the current frame rate is much smaller than the injection duration.

\subsubsection{Two-Stage Candidate Model}

Hiroyasu and Arai described diesel spray penetration as two regimes: pre-breakup growth proportional to \(t\), and post-breakup quasi-steady growth proportional to \(t^{1/2}\), with the breakup time \(t_b\) marking the transition \cite{HiroyasuArai1990}. In pilot diesel injection conditions, however, \(t_b\) falls very early in the observable window---empirically only a few camera frames---so the pre-breakup linear regime occupies too little of the signal to fit reliably. Zhou et al.\ further established that the SOI acceleration phase follows an uncertain exponent that is difficult to characterise from short, noisy Mie trajectories \cite{ZhouLiYi2021}; in practice, the fitting window begins effectively in the post-\(t_b\) regime. From this starting point, two functional primitives are chosen: \(\sqrt{t}\) to span the early post-breakup quasi-steady regime familiar from the H--A framework, and \(t^{1/4}\) to capture the empirically observed late-stage deceleration associated with post-injection dynamics \cite{ZhouLiLaiWei2019}. Rather than imposing an explicit delayed clock \((t-t_i)\)---which would require reliably identifying the end-of-injection moment from noisy per-plume records---a sigmoid gating weight \(w(t)\) interpolates continuously between the two branches, making the transition differentiable and independent of any external physical feature.

Each accepted trajectory is then fitted with a smooth two-stage model,
\[
S(t)=\left(1-w(t)\right)k_{\mathrm{sqrt}}\sqrt{t}
+w(t)k_{\mathrm{quarter}}t^{1/4},
\qquad
w(t)=\sigma\!\left(\frac{t-t_0}{s}\right),
\]
where \(k_{\mathrm{sqrt}}\), \(k_{\mathrm{quarter}}\), \(t_0\), and \(s\) are optimized in log-space. Specifically, the fitting problem is framed as a robust nonlinear least squares estimation,
\[
\min_{\theta}
\sum_i \rho_\delta\!\left(S(t_i;\theta)-y_i\right),
\qquad
\theta=
\left[\log k_{\mathrm{sqrt}},\log k_{\mathrm{quarter}},\log t_0,\log s\right],
\]
using the Huber penalty function
\[
\rho_\delta(r)=
\begin{cases}
\tfrac{1}{2}r^2, & |r|\le \delta,\\
\delta\left(|r|-\tfrac{1}{2}\delta\right), & |r|>\delta.
\end{cases}
\]
where \(r = S(t_i;\theta)-y_i\) is the fit residual, and \(\delta\) is the threshold where the penalty transitions from squared error to absolute error. This objective is solved via trust-region nonlinear least squares with \(\delta=1\), while the logarithmic parameterization preserves the positivity of the fit gains and transition scale \cite{Huber1964,NocedalWright2006}.

Therefore, this model should be read as a temporal parameterization rather than an explicit physical law. Its purposes are twofold: to provide credible extrapolation far from the EOI (downstream surrogate models require stable targets) and acceptable interpolation near the SOI (where relative errors are large and early apparent physics are hard to constrain from the image trajectory itself). The sigmoid gating is important for this role. Rather than imposing a hard breakpoint between two empirical branches, it creates a differentiable transition, which maintains the smoothness of the fitted curve and makes it more compatible with subsequent neural network stages. In other words, early transient physics are not forced into a strict closed-form law here; they are merely approximated well enough to allow the subsequent network to learn the remaining condition-dependent behavior from the full operating point inputs.
When compared to the extended EOI expressions in the spray literature, this interpretation becomes clearer. The post-EOI model of Zhou et al. distinguishes between a global injection clock \(t\) and a delayed clock \((t-t_i)\), because the late-stage penetration branch is activated only after the end-of-injection dynamics propagate downstream \cite{ZhouLiLaiWei2019}. The present pipeline handles this delay in two layers. First, the preprocessing stage estimates a plume-specific onset delay based on the early linear extrapolation described above, shifting the observed sequence to a common \(t\approx 0\) reference. Second, the reduced-order fit itself still does not incorporate an explicit delayed clock within the parameter branch. Instead, it uses
\[
w(t)=\sigma\!\left(\frac{t-t_0}{s}\right)
\]
to suppress the quarter-root branch prior to the learned transition time \(t_0\), allowing the \(t^{1/4}\) term to dominate only after this transition. Algebraically, this is not equivalent to replacing \(t^{1/4}\) with \((t-t_i)^{1/4}\). Rather, it is an engineering reparameterization: over a finite observation window, a delayed quarter-root branch can typically be adequately represented by a gated, unshifted quarter-root branch with adjusted amplitude and transition placement, especially when the early portion of that branch is strongly attenuated by \(1-w(t)\) or a near-zero \(w(t)\). In this sense, the physical clock of the delay is absorbed into the learned switching parameters \((t_0,s)\) and the branch amplitude, rather than being imposed as a fixed, known constant.

In summary, the engineering treatment above is reasonable for the goals of this thesis, but its applicability has clear boundaries. From an engineering perspective, compressing the cleaned discrete observations into a few parameters via curve fitting not only smooths out measurement noise but also reconstructs $y = f(t)$ at any arbitrary time bin density, permitting extrapolation within reasonable limits---something that cannot be done directly with discrete observations. Its justification lies in three points: the fit acts here as a smooth compressor for noisy trajectories, not as a final mechanistic spray law; it preserves the differentiability of the curve, facilitating subsequent learning; and it delegates the operating condition dependencies to the neural network modeling, eliminating the need to hard-code every physical effect into a closed-form analytical expression. However, this treatment should be understood as a reduced-order surrogate modeling strategy inspired by empirical penetration theory, rather than a proof that shifted-time physical effects have vanished. The ablation experiment below involving a quarter-root-only branch explicitly contrasts this.

\subsubsection{Quarter-Root Dominance and Selection of the SGQR Production Model}

The four-parameter candidate is useful mainly as a diagnostic model. In accepted fits, its square-root branch nearly always collapses, so retaining \(k_{\mathrm{sqrt}}\) adds an unstable degree of freedom without adding useful trajectory shape. At the fit transition time \(t_0\), the branch-weight ratio is measured as
\[
r = \frac{k_{\mathrm{sqrt}}\cdot t_0^{1/4}}{k_{1/4}}.
\]
Across \(14{,}563\) accepted plume fits in the polar binary penetration archive---the diagnostic archive on which the four-parameter candidate was characterised before the production target was switched to CDF penetration---the median ratio is \(r_{\mathrm{med}}=2.9\times10^{-9}\), with an interquartile range of \([9.0\times10^{-10},\;1.1\times10^{-8}]\). The collapse is structural rather than archive-specific: because the breakup time \(t_b\) falls within the first few observable frames (as argued above), the square-root branch is starved of support irrespective of the penetration definition, which is what motivates adopting the single-branch SGQR form for the CDF production fits below. In \(99.8\%\) of accepted fits, \(r<0.5\); in \(100\%\), \(r<1\). Expressed in absolute magnitude, the median quarter-root branch contributes \(\SI{69.2}{mm}\) at \(t_0\), while the median square-root branch contributes only \(2.0\times10^{-7}\,\si{mm}\). Successful four-parameter fits therefore degenerate into quarter-root dominated curves.

A simplified single-branch model, dubbed SGQR (for quarter-root, 1-branch), eliminates this risk by replacing the two-stage mixture with a single sigmoid-gated quarter-root branch,
\[
S_{\mathrm{SGQR}}(t) = \sigma\!\left(\frac{t-t_0}{s}\right)\,k_{1/4}\,t^{1/4},
\]
reducing the number of parameters from four to three: \([\log k_{1/4},\,\log t_0,\,\log s]\). An equivalent post-fit shielding pipeline was built in parallel for SGQR, using the same far-time plausibility window, parameter bounds, grouped robust outlier scores, and \(\mathrm{RMSE}<\SI{3}{mm}\) success criterion. The two model forms are therefore compared under symmetric post-fit shielding rules.

Out of \(23{,}495\) CDF penetration sequences in the nine-nozzle campaign, the four-parameter model achieved a \(61.3\%\) success rate, while SGQR achieved \(90.0\%\)---an absolute improvement of 28.6 percentage points, without a single nozzle regressing. On sequences accepted by both models, the median RMSE was essentially unchanged (\(\SI{1.23}{mm}\) for the four-parameter model and \(\SI{1.27}{mm}\) for SGQR). On the previously flagged subset, the median RMSE dropped from \(\SI{8.12}{mm}\) to \(\SI{1.08}{mm}\), a reduction of roughly \(7.5\times\). Removing \(k_{\mathrm{sqrt}}\) therefore preserves fit quality on well-behaved trajectories while preventing unstable far-time behavior on difficult ones.

Based on this ablation, SGQR was promoted to the production fitting target. Its curves serve as direct targets for Stages~1 and~2; Stage~3 receives their influence through the frozen Stage~2 teacher while also using reliable raw observations from Dataset~\#1. The four-parameter results are retained merely as a historical baseline for structural diagnostics and to motivate the model reduction step. Consequently, the practical purpose of this fit is not to claim a universal penetration law, but rather to compress noisy plume trajectories into smooth, differentiable, and trainable targets, while preserving the heteroscedastic variations within the dataset for the subsequent learning stages.

\subsubsection{SGQR Diagnostics: Scaling, Residuals, and Identifiability}

\textbf{Empirical Scaling Check.} After fitting each trajectory, three parameter values \((k_{1/4},\,t_0,\,s)\) are obtained. Because the fit constraints force the gating parameters \((t_0,s)\) to make the sigmoid product converge to near 1 within a finite time window, the end of the observable time period and the extrapolation segment satisfy \(S(t)\approx k_{1/4}\cdot t^{1/4}\); therefore, \(k_{1/4}\) is not an abstract fit amplitude, but the asymptotic linear scaling coefficient of the \(t^{1/4}\) penetration law, which can be directly compared with the proportionality coefficients of classical empirical formulas. Performing an ex-post log-log regression on it allows testing whether the fitting results follow the power-law expected by classical penetration theory with respect to the operating conditions. Regressing across \(24{,}316\) accepted fits via Ordinary Least Squares:
\[
  \log k_{1/4} = a\,\log\Delta P + b\,\log\rho_a + c\,\log d_n + \text{constant}
\]
yields exponents \(a\approx 0.52\), \(b\approx -0.23\), \(c\approx 0.26\), and \(R^2\approx 0.27\). These pooled regressions are descriptive diagnostics over the full accepted archive; they motivate, but do not themselves fit, the production amplitude exponent. The value used in \(A\) is fixed at \(0.5\), matching the Bernoulli exponent and exceeding the Hiroyasu--Arai exponent of \(0.25\). The single-seed five-fold Stage~1 LONO feature ablation in Section~\ref{sec:stage1-lono-ablation} provides out-of-sample support for this choice, but does not by itself rule out model-selection over-fitting.

Comparison against classical Hiroyasu--Arai theory reveals varying degrees of agreement. The density exponent \(b\approx-0.23\) almost perfectly matches the \(-1/4\) in the H--A post-breakup form \(S\propto\Delta P^{1/4}\rho_a^{-1/4}(d_n t)^{1/2}\), serving as a positive signal for the physical plausibility of the fitted parameter. The injection pressure exponent \(a\approx 0.52\), however, is markedly steeper than the H--A predicted \(1/4\); the exact reason for this deviation is uncertain---the condition coverage of the dataset, the SGQR functional form, and the possibility that the \(t^{1/4}\) branch absorbed contributions beyond the free-jet phase are all compatible with this result. The diameter exponent \(c\approx 0.26\) is much lower than the classical \(1/2\), most likely reflecting collinearity between nozzle diameter and other operating variables in the experimental matrix (independently confirmed in Section~\ref{sec:pointlevel-scaling-verification}). The regression \(R^2\) for the gating parameters \(t_0\) and \(s\) are only \(0.17\) and \(0.14\), respectively---which is expected: the sigmoid switching parameters are not directly predicted by classical theory; they mainly absorb residual onset delay variations after preprocessing alignment.

As a cross-check, direct log-log regressions on the raw discrete observations within fixed \(\SI{0.1}{ms}\) time bins yielded an \(R^2\) of roughly \(0.47\)--\(0.51\) in the mid-trajectory window (\(0.35\)--\(0.75\) ms), confirming that the three physical variables indeed explain a meaningful portion of the raw penetration variance in the well-sampled region. The lower \(R^2\approx0.27\) obtained from the compressed \(k_{1/4}\) parameter reflects the integral effect over noisy onset and post-injection regions rather than a model failure; the qualitative trends of the \(\Delta P\) exponent (\(0.44\)--\(0.65\), spanning values around the Bernoulli value \(0.5\) while remaining above the H--A value \(0.25\)) and the chamber pressure exponent (\(\approx-0.25\)) remain consistent across both analyses.

\textbf{Residual Structure and Heteroscedasticity.} Diagnostics of the temporal distribution of the fit residuals reveal the systematic behavior of the SGQR model across different trajectory stages, providing the physical rationale for the three-stage training and mixed supervision design. Reconstructing the residual \(\hat S(t)-y(t)\) at each observation frame for every cleaned trajectory and binning by \(\Delta t=\SI{0.1}{ms}\) yields the empirical envelope in Figure~\ref{fig:SGQR-residual-structure}, revealing two distinct regions of differing nature. In the central window \(t\in[0.3, 1.0]\,\si{ms}\), the median residual remains below \(\SI{0.4}{mm}\), and the robust standard deviation \(\sigma_{\mathrm{robust}}(t)=1.4826\,\mathrm{MAD}(t)\) reaches a minimum of \(\sim\SI{0.60}{mm}\) around \(t=\SI{1.0}{ms}\)---indicating that SGQR is essentially unbiased and tight in the well-sampled mid-trajectory region.

Systematic biases exist at both boundary ends: an early positive bias of \(\sim\SI{+1.7}{mm}\) at \(t=\SI{0.05}{ms}\) with \(\sigma_{\mathrm{robust}}\approx\SI{1.8}{mm}\), stemming from imprecise onset alignment and the steep transition of the sigmoid gate near \(t_0\); and a late negative bias of \(\sim\SI{-1.6}{mm}\) at \(t=\SI{1.55}{ms}\) with \(\sigma_{\mathrm{robust}}\approx\SI{0.7}{mm}\), arising from the right-censoring of the surviving plume population---where the frame count per bin drops drastically from \(\sim 65{,}000\) at \(t=\SI{0.15}{ms}\) to fewer than \(200\) frames by \(t=\SI{1.55}{ms}\). Thus, the envelope is U-shaped rather than monotonically growing. This dual-end bias is the core motivation for introducing mixed supervision in Stage 3: the SGQR fitting target exhibits structural errors in both the early pre-injection segment and the late observable tail, which ultimately need to be corrected via direct supervision signals rather than relying solely on the extension of the fitted curve. Moreover, the significant temporal variation of the residual scale indicates that a fixed-variance loss is inappropriate, necessitating the use of input-dependent \(\sigma\) in Stage 2's negative log-likelihood (NLL) loss.

\textbf{Parameter Identifiability.} The third diagnostic examines whether the three SGQR parameters can be independently determined by the data---if strong mutual compensation exists among parameters, the individual parameter values themselves will be hard to interpret, and regressions against operating conditions will be weak. To this end, using the trust-region Jacobian \(J\) returned by SciPy, the asymptotic covariance \(\Sigma\approx(J^TJ)^{-1}\hat\sigma^2\) is computed, yielding pairwise parameter correlations. The median results across all cleaned fits are: \(\overline{\mathrm{corr}}(\log k_{1/4},\log s)\approx0.76\), \(\overline{\mathrm{corr}}(\log k_{1/4},\log t_0)\approx0.56\), \(\overline{\mathrm{corr}}(\log t_0,\log s)\approx0.21\).

These correlations suggest that the three SGQR parameters are not independent physical knobs, but rather a coupled 3D smooth surface with considerable soft redundancy. This is the price paid for introducing the differentiable sigmoid gate (in contrast to the rigid stage breakpoints of Hiroyasu--Arai), and it also explains why the gating parameters showed weak scaling relationships against operating conditions in the earlier regression: the data constrains the surface as a whole, rather than constraining each axis individually. Regarding downstream design, the surrogate model (Section~\ref{sec:mlp-curriculum-en}) is trained to reproduce the entire evaluation curve \(\hat S(\cdot)\) rather than the parameter triplet itself, so strong parameter coupling does not propagate into the input-output mapping of the network.

\subsubsection{Post-Fit Shielding and the Selection Bias It Introduces}

A stricter post-fit shielding stage is also an important component of the engineering contribution. The robust outlier mask first evaluates the fitted curve at \(t_{\mathrm{far}}=\SI{5}{ms}\) to form a far-time penetration surrogate. The row is retained only if this far-time value remains within a broad physical plausibility window, and if the fit parameters pass basic sanity checks on \(t_0\), \(s\), point count, and fit cost. Robust grouped outlier scores are then computed using the median absolute deviation,
\[
z_{\mathrm{robust}}(x)=0.6745\,\frac{x-\operatorname{median}(x)}{\operatorname{MAD}(x)+\epsilon},
\qquad
\operatorname{MAD}(x)=\operatorname{median}\!\left(\lvert x-\operatorname{median}(x)\rvert\right),
\]
applied to \(t_0\), \(\log(1+\mathrm{RMSE})\), and \(\log(1+\mathrm{cost\_per\_point})\) within each file group. Rows with \(|z_{\mathrm{robust}}|>3\) on any of these diagnostics are flagged as outliers and excluded from the cleaned archive.

This post-fit shielding process introduces intentional engineering trade-offs, and its primary mechanism shifted alongside improvements in the upstream computer vision (CV) pipeline. In early processing, uneven illumination or inter-plume occlusion sometimes led the CV pipeline to mistake background noise for spray tips, generating physically impossible jumps that were subsequently truncated into severely shortened sequences by explicit upward-difference thresholds; the \(n \ge 10\) frame lower limit and \(t_0 < \SI{0.8}{ms}\) sanity gate were primarily designed to intercept these degenerate trajectories. Following CV enhancements, the vast majority of candidate sequences now pass the full post-fit shielding pipeline (per-nozzle survival rates are reported in Figure~\ref{fig:filter-survival-cdf-nozzle}). Flagged sequences are typically \emph{longer} trajectories whose \(t_0\), RMSE, or \(\mathrm{cost\_per\_point}\) exceed the three-sigma robust threshold within their group, rather than short noise-truncated sequences stemming from CV misidentification. As a result, the shielding logic now serves more as a fit-quality gate than a CV artifact cleaner.

Therefore, post-fit shielding introduces selection bias along two dimensions. The first is optical quality bias: the surviving dataset favors well-illuminated, unoccluded, high-contrast plumes and does not represent true geometric asymmetry or late-time signal decay. The second is injection profile regularity bias: SGQR is structurally limited to monotonically increasing smooth penetration curves, while trajectories with non-monotonic behaviors (arising from bi-modal injection or spray re-acceleration) carry higher intra-group RMSE and are flagged by the robust z-score outlier gate. The per-nozzle flag rate quantifies the resulting coverage gap: on the CDF source, the flag rate for nozzle 3 is 11.9\%, roughly double that of nozzles 4 and 6 (both 5.9\%), which is consistent with the spray re-acceleration events observed for nozzle 3 in the Stage 3 diagnostics. Thus, Stage 1 and Stage 2 jointly inherit both biases: their training sets lean toward optically ideal plumes and trajectories whose injection profiles are sufficiently regular to be adequately represented by the monotonic SGQR form.

Figure~\ref{fig:filter-survival-cdf-nozzle} illustrates the SGQR post-fit-shielding survival rates for CDF penetration per nozzle, summarizing the total sequence counts, cleaned sequence counts, and passing ratios. The purpose of this plot is not to argue that the gate is unbiased, but to clarify the footprint of that bias. Upon regenerating the report against the recovered PC archive, the nine-nozzle campaign spans a CDF penetration survival band of 88.1\%--94.1\%. The recovered nozzle 0 archive contributed \(\num{15475}\) candidate CDF penetration sequences, of which \(\num{14274}\) passed post-fit shielding (92.2\%), making it part of the reported coverage. Because no nozzle comes close to being completely rejected or completely retained, Stage~2 can be interpreted as a clean-plume teacher over the recovered archive, while still inheriting the explicit spatial and late-time selection biases documented here.

\subsection{Physics-Guided Feature Engineering}
\label{sec:feature-engineering-en}

Before the engineered feature architecture was finalized, an early nine-feature MLP trained using direct pressure and diameter inputs revealed a structural problem. Nozzle diameter exists at only six levels in the dataset, and these are not uniformly available across all pressure combinations---all six diameter levels appear exclusively within the family \((P_{\mathrm{inj}}=\SI{2000}{bar},\;P_{\mathrm{cb}}=4,\;P_{\mathrm{ch}}\in\{5,10,15\}\,\si{bar})\)---whereas injection pressure has only four levels. Figure~\ref{fig:sparse-support-topology} makes this discretely sampled grid explicit: most cells in the (chamber pressure, injection pressure, diameter) grid contain zero or one observation, and full six-diameter coverage exists only along a single pressure family.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{images/fig_sparse_support_topology.png}
\caption{Support topology of the training matrix. The left panel counts injection-pressure levels over the \((P_{\mathrm{ch}},P_{\mathrm{cb}})\) plane; the middle and right panels count nozzle-diameter levels over \((P_{\mathrm{ch}},P_{\mathrm{inj}})\) at \(P_{\mathrm{cb}}=\SI{1}{bar}\) and \(\SI{4}{bar}\). Most cells contain at most one diameter level, and all six levels occur only for \(P_{\mathrm{inj}}=\SI{2000}{bar}\), \(P_{\mathrm{cb}}=\SI{4}{bar}\), and \(P_{\mathrm{ch}}\in\{5,10,15\}\,\si{bar}\).}
\label{fig:sparse-support-topology}
\end{figure}

Because a smooth MLP treats these as continuous inputs, it generates derivative structures along the diameter and pressure axes that the data does not constrain. Figure~\ref{fig:sparse-diameter-interpolants} shows the result of dense diameter sweeps at \(t=\SI{0.85}{ms}\): between adjacent observation levels, the predicted mean curve undergoes five to six gradient sign reversals per slice, and autodiff gradients peak at \(\sim\!\num{4e4}\) over a support span of about \(\SI{0.05}{mm}\), equating to a curvature norm on the order of \(10^7\). The same diagnostic on injection pressure showed non-monotonic, unsupported extrapolation over sparsely supported slices. The root issue is structural---a smooth MLP is asked to learn continuous laws from what is effectively a discrete grid---so adding regularization can only suppress the instability at the cost of useful capacity. Therefore, the revised architecture addresses the problem at the source.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{images/fig_sparse_diameter_interpolants.png}
\caption{Dense diameter interpolation by the direct-feature MLP at \(t=\SI{0.85}{ms}\) for three operating conditions. The top row compares the predicted mean with the secant through the six observed diameter levels; the bottom compares the corresponding autodiff gradient with the secant slope. The unsupported sign reversals and gradient spikes show that continuous diameter dependence is not identified by the sparse support.}
\label{fig:sparse-diameter-interpolants}
\end{figure}

The revised surrogate model first replaces the weakly supported pressure and diameter inputs with a single scaling group. The Hiroyasu--Arai post-breakup branch \cite{HiroyasuArai1990} provides the dimensional starting point:
\[
S(t)\;\propto\;K_p\!\left(\frac{\Delta P}{\rho_a}\right)^{1/4}d_n^{1/2}\,t^{1/2},
\]
where the H--A exponent assigns \(\Delta P^{1/4}\) to the pressure ratio group. The SGQR scaling check above and the Dataset~\#2 verification below both show that the short-pilot training window is better collapsed by a stronger pressure exponent (validated out-of-sample by the LONO ablation in Section~\ref{sec:stage1-lono-ablation}), so the operational amplitude divisor is
\[
A\;=\;\Delta P^{0.5}\cdot\rho_a^{-0.25}\cdot d_n^{0.5},
\]
computed from the canonical feature table exported alongside the test matrix JSON file. This form should be read as an empirical short-window scaling choice, not a rejection of the Hiroyasu--Arai late-time asymptote. For nozzles 1 through 8, the chamber density is taken directly from the logged ambient gas density; for nozzle 0, where only ambient chamber pressure was logged, \(\rho_a\) is reconstructed using a linear law \(\rho_a=\SI{5}{kg/m^3}\times P_{\mathrm{ch}}/\SI{4.42}{bar}\) calibrated against the nozzle 1-8 data.

In the first design iteration, all four sparse inputs were fully merged into $A$, yielding a five-feature pure-scaling baseline
\[
[\,t_{\mathrm{norm}},\;\theta_{\mathrm{tilt},z},\;n_{\mathrm{plumes},z},\;t_{i,z},\;P_{\mathrm{cb},z}\,],
\]
where the subscript $z$ indicates that a feature has been standardized by subtracting the training-set mean and dividing by the training-set standard deviation (z-score normalization), $t_{\mathrm{norm}}$ denotes time rescaled by a fixed min-max mapping from the $[0,\,\SI{5}{ms}]$ window to $[0,1]$, and both sets of scaler parameters are estimated from the training split only and held fixed for validation and test to prevent leakage.
$A$ was used solely as a target divisor and did not enter the network. The training target was reparameterized as amplitude-scaled penetration $\hat{S}(t)=S(t)/A$; the network predicted $\hat{\mu}$ and $\log\hat{\sigma}^2$, and physical predictions were recovered during inference via $\mu_S=A\,\hat{\mu}$ and $\sigma_S=A\,\hat{\sigma}$. This baseline achieved a Stage 1 test MAE of \SI{8.05}{mm} and a Stage 2 test MAE of \SI{9.28}{mm}.

However, fully absorbing $P_{\mathrm{inj}}$ and $P_{\mathrm{ch}}$ into $A$ eliminated their independent explanatory power beyond what is captured by the fixed $\Delta P^{0.5}$ exponent. Regressions also estimated non-zero chamber pressure coefficients at fixed $\Delta P$ (roughly \(-0.21\) to \(-0.27\) in the mid-injection window), meaning $P_{\mathrm{inj}}$ and $P_{\mathrm{ch}}$ carry independent structure that cannot fully collapse into $\Delta P$. The global $\Delta P^{0.5}$ exponent is itself a mean-field approximation; when $P_{\mathrm{inj}}$ and $P_{\mathrm{ch}}$ are omitted, any local residual non-linearities in the condition space are left out of the network's reach. Nozzle diameter $d_n$, however, is a different case: it exists at only six discrete levels in the dataset and suffers from severe confounding in the condition matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:sparse-support-topology}), making it an unreliable continuous input even if reintroduced. It was therefore retained inside $A$, letting the H--A prior carry its effect.

Building on this, the final design adopted a seven-feature scaling-plus-residual-pressure formulation
\[
[\,t_{\mathrm{norm}},\;\theta_{\mathrm{tilt},z},\;n_{\mathrm{plumes},z},\;t_{i,z},\;P_{\mathrm{cb},z},\;\log P_{\mathrm{inj},z},\;\log P_{\mathrm{ch},z}\,],
\]
where $A$ continues to absorb the dominant diameter and dimensional pressure dependencies, while $\log P_{\mathrm{inj}}$ and $\log P_{\mathrm{ch}}$ are fed in as separate inputs so the network can learn residual pressure structure beyond the fixed H--A exponents. Compared to the five-feature baseline, Stage 1 test MAE dropped from \SI{8.05}{mm} to \SI{2.91}{mm}, and Stage 2 test MAE dropped from \SI{9.28}{mm} to \SI{5.63}{mm}, confirming that the residual pressure features provided information obscured by the fixed H--A exponents. Figure~\ref{fig:mlp-architecture} summarizes the resulting network and the role of $A$ in the physical reconstruction.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{images/mlp_architecture_overview.png}
\caption{Seven-feature amplitude-scaled MLP architecture. A three-layer \([512,512,128]\) Swish trunk with dropout rate 0.3 predicts \((\hat{\mu},\log\hat{\sigma}^{2})\). The fixed amplitude prior \(A=\Delta P^{0.5}\rho_a^{-0.25}d_n^{0.5}\) restores physical outputs through \(\mu_S=A\hat{\mu}\) and \(\sigma_S=A\hat{\sigma}\).}
\label{fig:mlp-architecture}
\end{figure}

Before any optimization, a scaling validation routine tests the scaling hypothesis on the actual dataset: each trajectory is divided by its own $A$, and the linear regression $R^2$ of $\hat{S}(t)$ against the sparse features is calculated. In the seven-feature production run, the sparse feature $R^2$ drops from $0.32$ for the physical target $S$ to $0.016$ for the scaled target $\hat{S}$, so the check passes. If the check fails, training aborts by default, making the physical prior falsifiable rather than merely asserted. The 0.016 reflects variance in $\hat{S}$ not explained by the sparse non-time features alone; it does not indicate that the full model has weak signal, since time itself is the dominant predictor of penetration magnitude.

Stage 1 and Stage 2 also share a group-aware data split: each operating-condition group, defined jointly by nozzle identity, injection pressure, chamber pressure, and injection duration, is assigned as a whole to the training, validation, or test set to prevent leakage between curves that share the same operating point. Without this constraint, a random trajectory-level split would let the network interpolate between nearly identical samples drawn from the same operating condition seen during training; the holdout error would then reflect within-condition smoothness rather than generalization across the condition space. Therefore, group assignment is not a mere implementation detail but a structural constraint that ensures the reported test metrics measure interpolation across different operating conditions, not memorization within a condition. Stage 2 warm-starts from Stage 1's engineering run directory and reuses its scaler states, ensuring that variant selection and $A$ scaling remain consistent across the two stages.

\subsubsection{Validating the Physics Scaling Formula Against Uncensored Observations}
\label{sec:pointlevel-scaling-verification}

The construction of Dataset \#2 starts by unrolling the individual $(t,S)$ discrete observation points from the cleaned CDF wide-format sequence table. Even after excluding points from trajectories that reached the spatial FOV cap, the remaining discrete points exhibit condition-dependent late-time observability loss: although a spray plume can remain within the field of view, the Mie scattering signal may fall below the detection threshold because of droplet breakup, spatial dilution, and local drops in droplet number density. Some conditions enter this signal-loss regime at $t<\SI{0.5}{ms}$, while others survive until significantly later. To address this, a point table generation tool was constructed (codebase identifiers listed in Appendix~\ref{app:implementation_mapping}) to determine condition-level truncation thresholds for each of the \num{592} operating condition groups using two statistical/optical metrics: (i)~the smoothed trajectory count per bin drops below a fixed fraction of its intra-condition peak (density-drop signal-loss criterion), or (ii)~the per-bin $p_{95}$ penetration reaches $95\%$ of the estimated FOV cap (FOV-cap criterion). Figure~\ref{fig:censor-mechanisms} illustrates one representative example of each. The first bin satisfying either criterion, along with all subsequent observation points, is discarded, leaving $N_{\mathrm{unc}}=\num{506634}$ censor-truncated points for downstream log-log regression use.

\textbf{Per-Condition Temporal Balance.}
For any regression on time-indexed penetration observations, a direct concern is that trajectories surviving into late time bins represent a progressively self-selected subset of the population---potentially biasing the sample toward slow or wall-restricted plumes. Per-condition truncation mitigates this concern. Before truncation, the median coefficient of variation for the per-bin trajectory count across time is $\mathrm{CV}_{\mathrm{raw}}=0.52$, reflecting attrition as trajectories either hit the FOV cap or lose detectable Mie support. After truncating each condition at its selected threshold, the median intra-condition CV drops to $\num{0.052}$ (interquartile range $[0.033,\;0.082]$), and the median ratio of minimum to average bin count within a condition is $0.92$. Within each surviving window, point counts are thus approximately uniform in time: the depleted tails are removed rather than reweighted, ensuring each retained time bin contributes statistically comparable evidence to the downstream regression.

\textbf{Diameter Exponent and H--A Prior.}
The $c_{d_n}$ column in Table~\ref{tab:loglog-regression-uncensored} is extremely large, ranging from $1.7$ to $6.0$; these values lack physical meaning. Two structural reasons prevent the diameter exponent from being identified from this dataset. First, the range for the six nozzle hole diameters is only $0.333$--$\SI{0.384}{mm}$ (a ratio of $1.15$, yielding a log-leverage of $0.14$), which is roughly three times smaller than the log-leverage available across the injection pressure range ($0.45$). Second, pressure and diameter do not vary independently in the design matrix: the full six-diameter coverage occurs only within a single injection pressure family ($P_{\mathrm{inj}}=\SI{2000}{bar}$, $P_{\mathrm{cb}}=\SI{4}{bar}$), and within that family, the nozzle sets differ not only in hole diameter but also in hole count and umbrella angle. Running a controlled check within this matched-pressure family---regressing the per-condition median penetration against $\log d_n$ at fixed $\Delta P$ and $P_{\mathrm{ch}}$---yielded $c_{d_n}\approx 0$ and $R^2<0.01$ across the entire mid-trajectory window, confirming that causal diameter effects cannot be separated from geometric confounders in this dataset. Therefore, the $d_n^{0.5}$ term is retained in $A$ as a Hiroyasu--Arai physically motivated prior; the two empirically supported components, $\Delta P^{0.5}$ and $\rho_a^{-0.25}$, are corroborated by both the $k_{1/4}$ regressions and the current point-level checks on the right-censored dataset.

\subsubsection{Which Input Features Help the Model Generalize to New Nozzle Families?}
\label{sec:stage1-lono-ablation}

The single-split improvements reported earlier in this section (Stage 1 test MAE dropping from \SI{8.05}{mm} to \SI{2.91}{mm}) confirm that within the same group-aware split, the seven-feature design outperforms the pure-scaling baseline. To justify this feature design before moving to multi-stage training, a dedicated Stage 1 leave-one-nozzle-out (LONO) ablation evaluated all nine variants of the engineered feature design in a five-fold split. Each fold held out one nozzle family (nozzle 0, nozzle 1, nozzle 2, nozzle 4, or nozzle 5) from the training pool, evaluating holdout generalization under varying feature configurations. Every cell of the ablation used the same deterministic Stage 1 MSE objective (described in Section~\ref{sec:mlp-curriculum-en}) under a single fixed seed (42) to isolate feature effects. The final multi-stage model evaluations and baseline comparisons are reserved for Chapter 5.

The nine variants span three orthogonal design axes: the amplitude exponent (\(\alpha=0.25\) vs. \(\alpha=0.5\) in \(A=\Delta P^{\alpha}\rho_a^{-0.25}d_n^{0.5}\)), the presence of log-pressure residuals (\(\log P_{\mathrm{inj},z}\), \(\log P_{\mathrm{ch},z}\)), and the presence of \(d_n\) as an independent input. A legacy nine-feature reference configuration (where raw \(\log P_{\mathrm{inj}}\), \(\log P_{\mathrm{ch}}\), \(\log\Delta P\), and \(d_n\) were fed directly to the network, targeting \(S\) in millimeters) predating the amplitude scaling was included as a baseline. Table~\ref{tab:stage1-lono-ablation-summary} reports the mean physical MAE across the five folds, and Table~\ref{tab:stage1-lono-ablation-per-fold} breaks down the results per fold (the row labels correspond to variant identifiers in the codebase to facilitate archival reproduction).

Across the nine variants, three design conclusions are stable. First, \(\alpha=0.5\) has lower mean LONO MAE than \(\alpha=0.25\) in every matched feature pairing, although the smallest difference (\SI{14.26}{mm} versus \SI{14.48}{mm}) is treated as unresolved rather than a robust ordering. Second, the log-pressure residuals provide a modest additional gain. Third, re-exposing \(d_n\) as an independent input is associated with a \SIrange{3.6}{6.8}{mm} increase in mean MAE across the matched amplitude-scaled variants. Because \(d_n\) is already encoded in \(A\) and has sparse, confounded support, this result is consistent with a redundant nozzle-correlated channel encouraging family-specific fitting; it does not directly demonstrate memorization. Tables~\ref{tab:stage1-lono-ablation-summary} and~\ref{tab:stage1-lono-ablation-per-fold} retain the exact aggregate and per-fold values.

The production configuration (\(\alpha=0.5\), with log-pressure residuals and no independent diameter) was selected because it achieved the lowest unweighted five-fold mean LONO MAE, \SI{7.46}{mm}, not because it dominated every fold or minimized cross-fold dispersion. Its cross-fold standard deviation is \SI{6.52}{mm}, compared with \SI{6.08}{mm} for the pure-scaling \(\alpha=0.5\) variant. Nozzle~0 is the largest-error fold; although its recorded diameter, plume count, and umbrella angle match Nozzle~1, Chapter~5 identifies it as a factory-fresh reference injector outside the prototype design lineage, so the fold combines operating-condition extrapolation with an injector-population shift. Its larger group count affects the precision and composition of that fold estimate but does not give it additional weight in the equal-fold arithmetic mean. Every ablation cell uses seed~42, so sub-\SI{0.3}{mm} differences are treated as unresolved using the nearest available multi-seed variability estimate, not as a formal significance test.

\subsection{Three-Stage Neural Surrogate Training Curriculum}
\label{sec:mlp-curriculum-en}

The ordering of the three stages is deliberate, not incidental, and follows a curriculum-learning strategy \cite{BengioCurriculumLearning2009}. Each stage addresses a specific limitation established by the censoring audit: trajectory-level noise, inter-plume variability, and late-time observability loss. Stage~1 gives Stage~2 a well-conditioned starting point so that the heteroscedastic fit is not asked to learn the mean trend and inter-plume spread from scratch simultaneously.

The MLP surrogate model is trained as a curriculum across three data populations rather than a single monolithic regression. Stage~1 learns a stable condition-level mean from the representative SGQR trajectories in Dataset~\#3. Stage~2 expands to the full post-fit-shielded SGQR archive and learns the heteroscedastic inter-plume spread, yielding the Stage~2 teacher. Stage~3 then reintroduces the Dataset~\#1 raw CDF observations where coverage is reliable, falling back to that teacher where FOV-cap hits and density-drop signal loss render direct raw supervision unsafe. Figure~\ref{fig:mlp-curriculum} summarizes the curriculum and the data population feeding each stage.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{images/mlp_training_curriculum.png}
\caption{The three-stage training curriculum. Stage~1 uses representative SGQR trajectories, Stage~2 uses the full post-fit-shielded SGQR archive, and Stage~3 uses raw CDF points partitioned by coverage. Warm starts propagate weights and scaler states; the frozen Stage~2 teacher supplies distillation targets where direct raw supervision is unreliable.}
\label{fig:mlp-curriculum}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Stage 1: Deterministic Baseline on Amplitude-Scaled Targets}

Stage 1 trains a deterministic mean model on a representative trajectory subset drawn from Dataset \#3. For each file-level operating condition group, the fitted curves are evaluated at \(t_{\mathrm{far}}=\SI{5}{ms}\), the group median penetration is calculated, and the nearest actual SGQR fitted trajectory is retained as the Stage 1 representative. This is not the P50/SGQR oracle from Dataset \#2. It yields 599 representative trajectories, divided by group into 421/89/89 training/validation/test trajectories.

The loss applied to the amplitude-scaled target $\hat{S}$ is
\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{stage1}}
=\;&\operatorname{MSE}\!\left(\hat{\mu},\hat{S}\right)
+\lambda_{\mathrm{var}}
\mathbb{E}\!\left[\left(\log \hat{\sigma}^2-c_0\right)^2\right]\\
&+\lambda_{1}
\mathbb{E}\!\left[\operatorname{ReLU}\!\left(-\frac{\partial \mu_S}{\partial t}\right)^2\right]
+\lambda_{2}
\mathbb{E}\!\left[g(t)\operatorname{ReLU}\!\left(\frac{\partial^2 \mu_S}{\partial t^2}\right)^2\right],
\end{aligned}
\]
where $c_0=-2$ is the log-variance prior center: since Stage 1 trains on a single representative trajectory per condition, there is no inter-plume spread to learn, so $\log\hat{\sigma}^2$ is regularised toward the constant $e^{c_0}\approx 0.135$ to provide a numerically stable initialisation for Stage 2 warm-starting rather than a data-estimated uncertainty. The one-sided derivative penalties encode two physical constraints on spray penetration: (i)~$\operatorname{ReLU}(-\partial\mu_S/\partial t)^2$ penalises only negative slopes---the negative sign selects the violation direction (a decrease in penetration is unphysical), and the square scales the penalty with the severity of the violation while leaving correct non-negative slopes with zero gradient; (ii)~$\operatorname{ReLU}(\partial^2\mu_S/\partial t^2)^2$ penalises only positive second derivatives---positive curvature means the spray is still accelerating, which is unphysical once the initial momentum-driven transient has passed. The gate $g(t)=\sigma((t-t_c)/\tau_c)$ suppresses the concavity penalty during the early transient where acceleration is expected, activating it progressively only beyond $t_c$. Both penalties are evaluated on the physical reconstruction $\mu_S=A\,\hat{\mu}$ so that the constraints act on the observable millimetre scale rather than on the scaled surrogate output. In the discrete implementation on the time grid, these derivatives are approximated by finite differences: a first-order forward difference for the slope and the standard three-point (central) stencil for the curvature. The network is a three-layer MLP of dimensions $[512,512,128]$ with Swish gated activations, a random unit masking rate of 0.3, and a decoupled weight decay AdamW optimizer with gradient clipping and early stopping based on validation loss. The Stage 1 run achieved a test physical MAE of \SI{2.91}{mm}; the holdout metrics are summarized alongside the other two stages in Table~\ref{tab:model-summary}, and Figure~\ref{fig:stage1-loss-curves} shows the corresponding training curves.

This test MAE is not directly comparable to the Stage 3 results reported in Chapter 5: it is measured on a single curated representative trajectory per condition under the amplitude-scaled, shape-constrained objective, not the common fixed-table CDF protocol used downstream. Stage 1's contribution to the curriculum is accordingly the well-behaved warm-start checkpoint from which Stage 2 learns the conditional spread, not a stand-alone accuracy claim.

\subsubsection{Stage 2: Learning the Full Plume Population and Its Conditional Spread}

Stage~2 warm-starts from the Stage~1 checkpoint and expands the training set from the representative subset to the post-fit-shielded SGQR trajectories in Dataset~\#3, replacing the MSE data-fitting term with a heteroscedastic Gaussian NLL over the scaled target \cite{KendallGal2017}:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{stage2}}
&=
\mathbb{E}\!\left[
\frac{1}{2}
\left(
\log \hat{\sigma}^{2}
+\frac{(\hat{S}-\hat{\mu})^{2}}{\hat{\sigma}^{2}}
\right)\right]
\\
&\quad
+\lambda_{1}\,
\mathbb{E}\!\left[
\operatorname{ReLU}\!\left(-\frac{\partial \mu_S}{\partial t}\right)^{2}
\right]
+\lambda_{2}\,
\mathbb{E}\!\left[
g(t)\operatorname{ReLU}\!\left(\frac{\partial^{2}\mu_S}{\partial t^{2}}\right)^{2}
\right],
\\
\hat{\sigma}^{2}
&=\exp(\log\hat{\sigma}^{2})+\varepsilon.
\end{aligned}
\]
The first term is the heteroscedastic NLL on the scaled target $\hat{S}$. The derivative penalties remain on the physical space reconstruction $\mu_S=A\,\hat{\mu}$. The row table expands to 71,700 post-fit-shielded trajectories, retaining rows that pass the fit-level mask and the far-time robust gate for inter-group spread. The split is performed at the grouped case level rather than the individual plume trajectory level, so trajectories from the same physical case do not leak across training, validation, and testing partitions. Each trajectory has 512 time samples over \SIrange{0}{5}{ms}, and the Stage~2 training set contains 36,710,400 point-wise samples: training on 49,908 trajectories, validating on 10,961, and testing on 10,831.

The retained trajectories span the operating range covered by the production training set: control back pressure from 1 to \SI{4}{bar}, injection pressure from 1,400 to \SI{2,200}{bar}, injection duration from 340 to \SI{1,200}{\micro s}, and fitted far-time penetration from \SI{26.2}{mm} to \SI{217.3}{mm}. Thus, Stage~2 sees more than a single median curve per condition; it observes the residual intra-condition plume and repeat variability that survives post-fit shielding. The heteroscedastic output separates the conditional mean trajectory from its conditional spread, while retaining the known mean--variance coupling limitations of variance-network training \cite{Detlefsen2019VarianceNetworks,Seitzer2022PitfallsHeteroscedastic}.

Training reuses Stage 1's feature mappings and scaler states. The input vector comprises normalized time coordinate, \(\theta_z\), \(N_{\mathrm{plume},z}\), \(\Delta t_{\mathrm{inj},z}\), \(P_{\mathrm{cb},z}\), \(\log P_{\mathrm{inj},z}\), and \(\log P_{\mathrm{ch},z}\) (seven features in total); the architecture remains a three-layer MLP of dimensions $[512,512,128]$, warm-started from the Stage 1 checkpoint. The Stage 2 run uses the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of \(8\times10^{-4}\), decoupled weight decay coefficient of \(10^{-4}\), batch size of 96, gradient clipping norm of 1.0, log-variance bounds of \([-10,6]\), and early stopping with a patience of 35. Therefore, the only intentional modification in the data fitting term is the replacement of deterministic MSE with a Gaussian NLL and its learned variance output.

\subsubsection{Stage 3: Blending Direct Observations with Teacher Guidance}\label{sec:stage3-distillation-en}
The NLL stage produces heteroscedastic predictions that closely track the fitted training targets, but the fitted curve is not equally faithful across the entire time axis. Near the start of injection, absolute penetration is small, so any timing offset, fit delay ambiguity, or smoothness prior yields a relatively large fractional error; the earliest portion of each predicted curve is shaped primarily by the fitted target prior rather than directly observed raw plume dynamics. Figure~\ref{fig:stage2-toy-inference-early-time} illustrates this in a representative toy inference sweep.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{images/stage2_toy_inference_2000bar_5bar.png}
\caption{Toy inference of the Stage 2 NLL model for a representative \(\SI{2000}{bar}\), \(\SI{5}{bar}\) case. Predictions are generally smooth and well-behaved, but the earliest portion of the trajectory is still shaped predominantly by the fitted target prior, motivating subsequent distillation refinement.}
\label{fig:stage2-toy-inference-early-time}
\end{figure}

To refine early-time dynamics using raw CDF penetration observations while preventing collapse in late-time sparse regions, a knowledge distillation stage is introduced atop the Stage~2 teacher \cite{HintonVinyalsDean2015,KangKang2024KDRegression}. In population terms, Stage~3 blends the Dataset~\#1 raw observations with the Stage~2 teacher learned from Dataset~\#3. The student network inherits the teacher weights and undergoes further training under the joint guidance of raw CDF penetration and the teacher.

\textbf{CDF Penetration Coverage Intervals.} Because the raw CDF penetration sequences are right-censored (by \(t\approx\SI{3}{ms}\), many operating conditions have lost over 70\% of their measurements), direct raw supervision cannot be applied uniformly across time. For each operating condition group, raw observations are binned over \([0,5]\,\si{ms}\) at \(\Delta t=\SI{0.1}{ms}\) intervals. The smoothed sample count per bin dictates three mutually exclusive intervals: high-confidence raw interval (smoothed coverage \(\geq70\%\) of peak), uncertain raw interval (coverage between 20\% and 70\%), and teacher-only extrapolation interval (coverage below 20\% or fewer than 4 samples per bin). Interval transitions require at least two consecutive bins to remain in the same interval, preventing isolated dips from triggering premature interval changes.

The 70\%/20\% coverage boundaries are conservative engineering rules rather than optimal values drawn from a full grid search. Archived single-seed five-fold LONO ablations provide only directional support for disabling the Stage~3 anchor and retaining mild KD guidance in the reliable interval; the differences are small relative to cross-fold dispersion (Appendix~\ref{app:evaluation-diagnostics}, Table~\ref{tab:stage3-ablation-ranking}). A systematic sensitivity analysis of the coverage boundaries and \(\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}}\) remains outside the current scope.

\textbf{Supervision Weights.} Each grid point receives two independent weight channels:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Interval & Raw Weight $w_{\mathrm{raw}}$ & KD Weight $w_{\mathrm{kd}}$ \\
\midrule
High-confidence raw interval  & 1.0 & 0.25 \\
Uncertain raw interval & 0.0 & 1.0  \\
Teacher-only extrapolation interval  & 0.0 & 0.75 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Both channels are further modulated by a global time bin rebalancing weight (inverse frequency, clipped to \([0.5,2.0]\)). In the high-confidence raw interval bins, the raw NLL is the primary signal, accompanied by mild KD regularization. In the uncertain raw interval bins, raw supervision is dropped, and the teacher KD MSE dominates. In the teacher-only extrapolation interval bins, the KD weight is slightly reduced to acknowledge teacher extrapolation uncertainty. Two single-run checks support this interval filter: substituting the default cleaned wide-format CDF sequence table with a pre-truncated raw source yielded negligible RMSE change (\(\Delta\mathrm{RMSE}\approx-\SI{0.003}{mm}\)), whereas disabling the low-confidence raw weight filter degraded RMSE by roughly \(\SI{0.065}{mm}\). The latter effect (\(\approx\SI{0.065}{mm}\)) exceeds this model's seed-to-seed RMSE variation (\(\approx\SI{0.040}{mm}\) across five seeds) and is comparable in magnitude to the MLP--SVGP in-domain point-accuracy margin (\(\SI{0.072}{mm}\)); the single run is therefore read as directionally consistent and numerically non-negligible relative to these margins, though not statistically resolved on its own. It corroborates---but does not on its own prove---the first-principles argument that treating right-censored late frames as ordinary raw supervision is worse than relying on the teacher in these bins.

\textbf{Composite Loss.} The training objective is
\[
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{raw}} + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KD}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{onset}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{onset}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{anchor}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{anchor}} + \lambda_{d_1}\,P_{d_1} + \lambda_{d_2}\,P_{d_2},
\]
The KD component is
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KD}} = \operatorname{MSE}(\mu_{\mathrm{student}},\,\mu_{\mathrm{teacher}}) + \lambda_\sigma\,\operatorname{MSE}(\log\sigma^2_{\mathrm{student}},\,\log\sigma^2_{\mathrm{teacher}}),\qquad \lambda_\sigma = 5.0;
\]
This form was not the first candidate. Because both teacher and student output univariate Gaussians, the Kullback--Leibler divergence between them has a closed-form expression,
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(\mathcal{N}(\mu_1,\sigma_1^2)\,\|\,\mathcal{N}(\mu_2,\sigma_2^2)\right)
=\log\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1}+\frac{\sigma_1^2+(\mu_1-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2}-\frac{1}{2},
\]
and the forward direction \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathrm{teacher}}\|p_{\mathrm{student}})\) (teacher \(\equiv 1\), student \(\equiv 2\)) was the initial candidate, chosen for its algebraic consistency with the Stage~2 NLL objective: minimising Gaussian NLL is itself equivalent to minimising \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathrm{data}}\|p_{\mathrm{model}})\), so the same forward direction in Stage~3 preserves this training-objective consistency.
This direction, however, opens a subtle optimisation loophole. The teacher--student mean mismatch enters only through \((\mu_1-\mu_2)^2/(2\sigma_2^2)\)---divided by the student's own predicted variance. Holding the means fixed, minimising over \(\sigma_2\) yields the stationary point \((\sigma_2^{\star})^{2}=\sigma_1^{2}+(\mu_1-\mu_2)^{2}\): forward KL can reward the student for absorbing uncorrected mean error into inflated variance rather than reducing the error itself. Near the field-of-view boundary, where surviving raw observations pull the student mean downward, this uncertainty-hedging behaviour makes it cheaper to widen \(\sigma_2\) than to lift \(\mu_2\) back toward the teacher mean, drifting toward an over-broad student with a systematically under-predicted late-stage mean---a pattern confirmed empirically by the Stage~3 distillation diagnostics in Chapter~5.
The production loss above removes this shortcut: separating the KL term into a direct mean MSE and a log-variance MSE means the mean penalty no longer divides by \(\sigma_2^2\), so inflating the variance can no longer discount it, while the log-variance term pins \(\sigma_2\) directly to the teacher \(\sigma_1\) instead of leaving it as a free slack variable.

The terms have distinct roles:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{raw}}$ is the physical-space Gaussian NLL on raw CDF observations, weighted by $w_{\mathrm{raw}}$.
  \item $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KD}}$ matches the Stage~2 teacher mean and log-variance in physical space, weighted by $w_{\mathrm{kd}}$, with $\lambda_\sigma=5.0$.
  \item $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{onset}}$ is a ramp-target binary cross-entropy active for $t\leq\SI{0.2}{ms}$, with $\lambda_{\mathrm{onset}}=0.1$. $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{anchor}}$ pulls $\mu(t\approx0)$ toward zero over the first \SI{0.15}{ms}. Baseline exploration used $\lambda_{\mathrm{anchor}}=0.01$, whereas the selected checkpoint uses $\lambda_{\mathrm{anchor}}=0$. In the archived single-seed five-fold LONO ablation, disabling the anchor reduced the mean bias magnitude and achieved the highest $2\sigma$ coverage, although the uncertain-regime mixed variant obtained the lower RMSE and MAE (Appendix~\ref{app:evaluation-diagnostics}, Table~\ref{tab:stage3-ablation-ranking}).

\subsection{Sparse Variational Gaussian Process as an Alternative Surrogate Backbone}
\label{sec:svgp-alternative-backbone-en}

The Sparse Variational Gaussian Process (SVGP) model should be interpreted as a strong alternative surrogate backbone rather than a weak baseline. It solves the identical low-dimensional regression problem as the MLP: targeting the amplitude-scaled penetration $\hat S=S/A$, transforming predictions back to millimeters via $A\hat\mu$ and $A\hat\sigma$, and evaluating on the same CDF observations and fixed tables. It is the inductive bias of the Gaussian Process that differs, not the physical objective.

The implemented Gaussian Process employs an "a-only" five-feature set,
\[
[\,t_{\mathrm{norm}},\;\theta_{\mathrm{tilt},z},\;n_{\mathrm{plumes},z},\;t_{i,z},\;P_{\mathrm{cb},z}\,],
\]
with the primary pressure, density, and diameter dependencies absorbed into $A$. It is sparse and heteroscedastic: one SVGP learns the conditional mean, and a second learns the log-residual variance. Both use a Mat\'ern $5/2$ kernel with Automatic Relevance Determination lengthscales, while inducing-point variational inference renders the million-point-scale Stage~3 tables computationally feasible \cite{RasmussenWilliams2006,HensmanFusiLawrence2013,Titsias2009SparseGP}. Both heads instantiate $M=256$ inducing points initialized by $k$-means clustering on $50{,}000$ subsampled training inputs, with \texttt{learn\_inducing\_locations=True} allowing further refinement during optimization; the variational posterior uses a Cholesky parameterization that retains the full $M\times M$ covariance rather than a mean-field approximation. Training uses Adam with learning rate $10^{-2}$, mini-batch size $1024$, and $80$ epochs on each head.

This architecture is well suited to the low-dimensional, smooth regression task. In the refreshed in-domain and LONO comparisons reported in Chapter~5, the SVGP is the stronger point predictor. Outside densely sampled regions, its kernel formulation supplies an explicit support-dependent prior, but this does not turn a held-out nozzle family into an interpolation problem.

Three implementation choices define the SVGP inductive bias: ARD Mat\'ern-$5/2$ kernels, a fixed $M=256$ inducing-point budget, and $k$-means initialization on the training inputs. Together they produce a smooth, support-dependent sparse-kernel predictor and are plausible contributors to the LONO behavior reported in Chapter~5. However, the present experiments do not ablate these mechanisms separately, and $k$-means initialization does not guarantee that a held-out cross-family point lies near multiple inducing supports. The evidence therefore supports an architecture-level empirical comparison with the MLP, not a causal attribution of the transfer gain to ARD, variational capacity, or inducing-point placement individually. The SVGP was also not evaluated with the full seven-feature set, so the effect of that restriction remains untested.

The MLP is retained as the primary protocol instantiation for three reasons. First, the Stage~3 student carries an onset side-branch used by the screening interface; the current SVGP replaces the penetration $(\mu,\sigma)$ regressor but lacks this side channel. Second, the MLP directly implements the regime-aware raw/KD curriculum. Third, the MLP supports the feature, anchor, and distillation ablation matrices within the available workstation budget. The Chapter~5 comparison therefore separates point-accuracy performance from these deployment and diagnostic capabilities rather than treating either architecture as universally preferable.

\subsection{Physical Baselines and Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:baseline-fitting-protocol-en}

The Hiroyasu--Arai and Naber--Siebers baselines reported in the results chapter are evaluated on the same canonical clean tables as the learned surrogates, rather than fitted directly to the raw image pixels. Therefore, the comparison tests different penetration function families under identical observation definitions and evaluation tables; it does not conflate differing image-processing methods.

Upstream, the canonical tables are generated by the common workflow described in Chapter 3: nozzle center calibration, polar plume strip extraction, background and gradient enhancement, axial intensity projection per plume, CDF front quantile extraction, anomalous jump truncation, short-segment removal, pixel-to-millimeter conversion, and SOI/hydraulic delay time alignment. The fixed-table evaluator then supplies the baseline models with point-wise penetration $S(t)$, the corresponding normalized operating-condition features, and canonical physical quantities derived from the test-matrix JSON file, such as $d_n$, $\Delta P$, $\rho_a$, and $A$.

The Hiroyasu--Arai baseline uses the two-stage fittable function
\[
S_{\mathrm{HA}}(t)=
\begin{cases}
k_v \left( \dfrac{2\Delta P}{\rho_l} \right)^{1/2}t,
& t<t_b,\\[6pt]
k_p \left( \dfrac{\Delta P}{\rho_a} \right)^{1/4}(d_n t)^{1/2},
& t\ge t_b,
\end{cases}
\qquad
t_b=k_b\,\frac{\rho_l d_n}{(\rho_a\Delta P)^{1/2}},
\]
where $d_n$ is the nozzle hole diameter, $\Delta P=P_{\mathrm{inj}}-P_{\mathrm{ch}}$, and $\rho_l,\rho_a$ are the fuel and ambient gas densities \cite{HiroyasuArai1990}. The "calibrated" row in the results table does not simply recycle literature constants; it fits the logarithmic parameters $(k_v,k_p,k_b)$ on the training split using Huber robust least squares, and subsequently evaluates the fixed parameters on the validation and test splits. The logarithmic parameterization ensures that the gains and breakup timescale remain positive, while the Huber loss mitigates the impact of minor image-derived outliers on the global empirical law.

The Naber--Siebers baseline employs the delayed square-root form $S_{\mathrm{NS}}(t)=k_{\mathrm{NS}}A_{\mathrm{NS}}\sqrt{\max(t-\tau,0)}$, where $A_{\mathrm{NS}}=(\Delta P/\rho_a)^{1/4}d_n^{1/2}$ is the classical Naber--Siebers scaling group---distinct from the production amplitude divisor $A$ of Section~\ref{sec:feature-engineering-en}. The reported row fits $\log k_{\mathrm{NS}}$ and $\tau$, with the delay constrained to $[-0.1,0.8]\,\si{ms}$; angle correction exists only as an optional implementation path, and the final comparison uses neither the training-set cone angle nor the oracle cone angle.

To make the physical baselines usable in probabilistic evaluations, their uncertainty is not assigned as an arbitrary constant. Post-fitting, the training/validation residuals are time-binned at $\SI{0.1}{ms}$ intervals, and the robust spread is estimated using $1.4826\,\mathrm{MAD}$, with a floor set at $\SI{0.5}{mm}$ to prevent unrealistically narrow bands in sparse early intervals. This $\sigma(t)$ allows the empirical laws to report $1\sigma$ and $2\sigma$ coverage and CRPS, while preserving their traditional penetration-related mean functions. The SVGP described in Section~\ref{sec:svgp-alternative-backbone-en} is evaluated on the exact same tables, but is construed as an alternative probabilistic surrogate backbone rather than a traditional physical baseline.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Protocol and Metrics}
\label{sec:evaluation-protocol-en}

All models are scored on common fixed tables loaded from explicit cleaned dataset roots. The evaluation pipeline does not rerun image processing or create new training, validation, and test partitions; it applies frozen checkpoints and pre-fitted baseline parameter sets to canonical point tables generated upstream. The refreshed Chapter~5 results are therefore common-table comparisons, not newly isolated untouched-test evaluations. A separate archived Stage~3 diagnostic sweep compared KD formulations and $\lambda_\sigma$ values on a censoring-inclusive fixed table; because that table informed the design choice, those diagnostics remain design-selection evidence rather than independent confirmation.

Four evaluation perspectives are kept separate because they address different questions. The conservative uncensored CDF protocol removes intervals beyond the density-drop signal-loss or FOV-cap thresholds from Dataset~\#2 and serves as the primary physical point-level metric for censor-controlled comparisons. The full-clean CDF protocol measures overall point accuracy on the common cleaned CDF set including right-censored regions, and is reported as a censoring-inclusive supplementary check. The P50 observation protocol collapses each condition to the median observed penetration points, testing the robustness of the mean head against inter-plume spread. The SGQR extrapolation protocol evaluates predictions over the companion SGQR grid fitted to the P50 trajectories; it is an extrapolation diagnostic, not an independent ground truth.

\textbf{Point Error Metrics.} Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) measure the accuracy of the predicted conditional mean $\mu(t)$ against the point-wise penetration observations in each fixed evaluation table. Both are computed across the full time horizon represented by those trajectories. Because errors within a single trajectory are temporally correlated, the effective sample size is much smaller than the raw point count; the reported values describe the average per-point error across a heterogeneous population of operating conditions and time points, rather than statistically independent samples.

\textbf{Empirical Coverage.} A heteroscedastic model outputs a conditional mean $\mu(t)$ and conditional standard deviation $\sigma(t)$. The primary calibration question is whether the predicted uncertainty intervals are empirically credible: under a Gaussian predictive distribution, 68.3\% of observations should fall within the $[\mu-\sigma,\,\mu+\sigma]$ interval, and 95.4\% within the $[\mu-2\sigma,\,\mu+2\sigma]$ interval. The empirical $1\sigma$ coverage on each fixed evaluation table is the principal calibration metric for each probabilistic model: values above 68.3\% indicate conservative (over-dispersed) intervals, while lower values signify overconfident (under-dispersed) predictions. For physical baselines, $\sigma(t)$ is the time-binned robust residual spread estimated during baseline fitting; for MLP stages, $\sigma(t)$ is the direct network output.

\textbf{Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS).} The CRPS evaluates the predicted cumulative distribution function $F$ against a scalar observation $y$ \cite{GneitingRaftery2007CRPS}, defined as
\[
\mathrm{CRPS}(F,y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\!\left(F(z)-\mathbf{1}[z\geq y]\right)^2\mathrm{d}z.
\]
Under a Gaussian predictive distribution with mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$, this simplifies to a closed-form expression that rewards both accuracy (small $|\mu-y|$) and calibration (a $\sigma$ that is neither too wide nor too narrow). Lower CRPS is better; a perfectly sharp and accurate point prediction yields $\mathrm{CRPS}=0$.

\textbf{Reliability, ECE, and PIT.} For each prediction, the observation can be transformed into a predictive percentile,
\[
u_i=\Phi\!\left(\frac{y_i-\mu_i}{\sigma_i}\right).
\]
If the Gaussian predictive distribution is calibrated, the \(u_i\) values should be uniformly distributed over \([0,1]\). Reliability diagrams compare nominal lower-tail probabilities against the empirical fraction of observations below each percentile; the expected calibration error (ECE) here summarizes the absolute deviations over the chosen probability grid, adapting the calibration-error construction used in neural-network calibration \cite{Guo2017Calibration}. Probability Integral Transform (PIT) histograms portray the same information: U-shaped histograms indicate under-dispersion, hump-shaped histograms indicate over-dispersion, and skewness indicates mean bias. Because the evaluation table contains hundreds of thousands of time-correlated points, the KS p-value is treated as a sensitivity indicator rather than a binary pass/fail label; the histogram shape, ECE, and coverage curves carry the engineering interpretation.

\textbf{Leave-One-Nozzle-Family-Out Evaluation.} The standard group-aware split retains all nozzle families in the training and validation partitions and evaluates interpolation across the supported domain of pressure and duration combinations. To probe out-of-distribution robustness in a physically meaningful direction, a secondary LONO evaluation is performed: all operating conditions belonging to one nozzle family are removed from the training and validation splits, the model is retrained on the remaining families, and performance is evaluated solely on that family. LONO uses five representative families---nozzles 0, 1, 2, 4, and 5, matching the Stage~1 feature ablation in Section~\ref{sec:stage1-lono-ablation}---rather than all nine, bounding retraining compute while including Nozzle~0 as the sole cross-design-lineage fold. The protocol therefore probes a deliberately difficult cross-family transfer and extrapolation scenario. Results for both evaluation protocols are reported in Chapter~5.

\subsection{Probabilistic Screening Interface}
\label{sec:screening-interface-en}

The screening outputs below assume in-domain deployment; cross-family calibration degradation is addressed in Chapter 5. The discussion of spray-wall impingement mechanisms in the literature review concluded that the appropriate downstream application for an uncertainty-aware penetration surrogate is a probabilistic screening proxy rather than a binary pass/fail rule, and that penetration should be projected onto the engine geometry plane as a rotating 2D Gaussian before integrating against the cylinder wall and piston crown masks. The GUI prototype realizes this proxy by coupling a Stage 3 student checkpoint with a parameterized piston crown, cylinder wall, and slider-crank displacement model, outputting spatial snapshots, probabilistic time histories, cumulative exposure, and animations. The MLP is the most complete plug-and-play model for this interface: the onset-bearing production configuration (Section~\ref{sec:mlp-curriculum-en}, Table~\ref{tab:training-hyperparameters}) provides both penetration \((\mu,\sigma)\) and the onset side-branch usable in the diagnostic plots, whereas the later residual-adapter lineage (Chapter~5) drops the onset side-branch and exposes penetration only. The SVGP from Section~\ref{sec:svgp-alternative-backbone-en} could replace the penetration mean and variance in the same geometric integration, but it has no side model under any configuration and so cannot provide the onset branch. The default configuration uses the Design A piston, \(\mathrm{RPM}=250\), crank phase \(0^{\circ}\), a time window of 0--\SI{5}{ms}, and a 60-frame time grid.

\textbf{Spray Probability Density.} Let each plume's spray axis be rotated by tilt angle \(\theta_{\mathrm{tilt}}\) from radial, and let \(\alpha\) denote the half-cone angle. In the plume-aligned coordinate system, the student prediction supplies the axis-parallel standard deviation \(\sigma_{\parallel}(t)\), while the lateral spread is represented by a second Gaussian. The current implementation does not set the lateral standard deviation equal to the geometric half-width; it interprets the fixed half-cone angle as an approximate three-standard-deviation envelope and adds a grid-size floor:
\[
\sigma_{\perp}(t)
=\max\!\left\{
\frac{\max\!\left(\mu_{+}(t),\sigma_{\parallel}(t)\right)\tan(\alpha/2)}{3},
\;2\Delta x
\right\},\qquad
\mu_{+}(t)=\max(\mu(t),0).
\]
Here $\Delta x$ is the grid spacing, defaulting to \(\SI{0.25}{mm/px}\). The lower bound prevents the lateral PDF from non-physically collapsing near the start of injection when \(\mu(t)\) is small, and the \(1/3\) factor treats the geometric cone angle as an approximate \(3\sigma\) envelope---an engineering convention for probabilistic collision screening rather than a value calibrated against measured spray widths. This fixed-\(\alpha\) assumption serves only for early screening and ranking, not as a validated time-varying spray envelope model.

This same $\mu_{+}(t)$ construction also resolves a broader consistency concern: because the underlying per-time Gaussian prediction $(\mu,\sigma)$ has unbounded support, it nominally assigns nonzero density to non-physical negative-penetration positions. This does not leak probability mass in the deployed screening interface, however, since the density is evaluated on a canvas grid anchored at the physical nozzle exit and centered using $\mu_{+}(t)$ rather than $\mu(t)$; density nominally corresponding to $S<0$ therefore falls outside the sampled grid and mask domain and is never included in the discretized wall-hit integral below.

The joint density
\[
p(x,y,t)=\mathcal{N}\!\Bigl((x,y);\,\mathbf{c}(t),\,\mathbf{R}(\theta_{\mathrm{tilt}})\,\operatorname{diag}\!\bigl(\sigma_{\parallel}^{2}(t),\sigma_{\perp}^{2}(t)\bigr)\,\mathbf{R}(\theta_{\mathrm{tilt}})^{\top}\Bigr),
\]
centered at $\mathbf{c}(t)=\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{nozzle}}+\mathbf{R}(\theta_{\mathrm{tilt}})\,(\mu_{+}(t),0)^{\top}$, is evaluated on a regular canvas grid (default \(\SI{0.25}{mm/px}\), window \(125\times\SI{200}{mm}\), cylinder wall at \(x=\SI{120}{mm}\)) and renormalized per frame so that $\iint p\,\mathrm{d}A=1$. The student's scaled outputs are un-normalized into physical space via the empirically calibrated amplitude $A=\Delta P^{0.5}\cdot\rho_a^{-0.25}\cdot d_n^{0.5}$ introduced for the engineered feature architecture, ensuring $\mu$ and $\sigma_{\parallel}$ enter the PDF in millimeters.

\textbf{Piston Geometry Scheduler.} The prototype exposes three interchangeable crown profiles. The default Design A is a piecewise circular arc structure, assembling a bowl-shaped crown from five arc radii---outer bowl, inner bowl, crown, lip, and bottom---with rigid closure constraints that maintain geometric consistency whenever any radius is altered. Design B is a dual-arc bowl profile with tangential constraints. The Hermite variant replaces the arc structure with a \emph{quintic Hermite spline}, defined by a small set of control points, each carrying position, tangent vector, and second derivative vector in the (radial, depth) cross-sectional plane. Matching position, slope, and curvature at each node provides $C^{2}$ continuity across segment boundaries, meaning the crown surface lacks curvature jumps---a property relevant to the collision integration grids used here, where sudden curvature changes can alias onto rasterized masks.

This parameterization also simplifies systematic parameter sweeps: altering a single scalar---e.g., bowl depth---continuously deforms the entire bowl profile, while the rest of the control point structure adapts via splines without requiring the re-solving of constraint equations. Figure~\ref{fig:hermite-crown} illustrates the baseline four-point crown with its tangent arrows (panel a) alongside a seven-level depth sweep (panel b), showcasing continuous, smooth deformation induced by changing one parameter. All three designs return a binary piston mask on the identical canvas grid as the spray density; recent default GUI artifacts use Design A, so the black crown shown in default screening images is not the Hermite swept profile.

\textbf{Piston Kinematics.} The crown axial position follows the simplified slider-crank form \cite{HeywoodICE}
\[
h_{\mathrm{crown}}(t)=h_{0}-\tfrac{s}{2}\cos(\omega t+\varphi),\qquad \omega=\tfrac{2\pi\,\mathrm{RPM}}{60},
\]
implemented with \(t\) passed in seconds to the angular velocity term, while the GUI displays the time axis in milliseconds. This expression is the slider-crank limit as $r/l\to 0$, omitting higher-order connecting rod corrections. The phase $\varphi$ situates the reference event at $t=0$. Default prototype settings ($\mathrm{RPM}=250$, $s/2=\SI{55}{mm}$, $h_{0}=\SI{75}{mm}$, $\varphi=0$) correspond to a short injection window near top dead center, during which the crown moves less than \SI{1}{mm}; within this regime, connecting rod corrections are negligible.

\textbf{Per-Frame Collision Integration.} The instantaneous collision probability and cumulative exposure over the injection window are
\[
P_{\mathrm{hit}}(t)=\iint p(x,y,t)\,\mathbf{1}_{\Omega_{\mathrm{piston}}(t)}(x,y)\,\mathrm{d}A,\qquad E=\int_{0}^{T} P_{\mathrm{hit}}(t)\,\mathrm{d}t,
\]
where $\mathbf{1}_{\Omega_{\mathrm{piston}}(t)}$ is the binary indicator function for the instantaneous piston mask; wall probability $P_{\mathrm{wall}}(t)$ is computed by integrating the same PDF over the wall mask $x\geq\SI{120}{mm}$. Spatial integration executes over the canvas grid, while cumulative exposure is computed via trapezoidal quadrature over the time grid. When the loaded checkpoint carries the onset side-branch, its probability output is evaluated on the identical time grid and plotted alongside $P_{\mathrm{hit}}(t)$ and \(P_{\mathrm{wall}}(t)\), allowing near-SOI onset confidence and geometric interception probability to be read separately; checkpoints without the onset side-branch omit this overlay.

\textbf{Default Input Conventions.} Recent GUI artifacts use \(d_n=\SI{0.34}{mm}\), injection pressure \SI{2000}{bar}, chamber pressure \SI{15}{bar}, control back pressure \SI{4}{bar}, 10 plumes, injection duration \SI{800}{\micro s}, fixed cone angle \(20^{\circ}\), tilt angle \(20^{\circ}\), and a 0--\SI{5}{ms} window. These settings define the archived demonstration; the resulting probability ranges, peak interception score, cumulative exposure, and geometry sensitivity are reported in Chapter~5, Section~\ref{sec:screening-boundary-rewrite}.

\textbf{Claimed Scope.} The rotating Gaussian envelope, analytical cone angle spreading, and slider-crank crown model are engineering simplifications, not resolved droplet-scale physics. This prototype is the probabilistic screening proxy argued for in the literature review; it is not a collision predictor validated against experimental high-speed cinematography or CFD impingement fields.
