\section{Censoring-Aware Penetration Surrogate Modeling and Probabilistic Screening}

This chapter begins with the plume-resolved observations generated by the image processing workflow, transforming these discrete, partially right-censored measurements into a trainable target, an uncertainty-aware surrogate model, and a simplified geometric screening proxy. The separation between "video-to-observation" and "observation-to-predictor" is intentional, allowing the assumptions and limitations of each layer to be examined independently.

Short pilot injections exacerbate the tension between measured trajectories and trainable targets. Classical empirical penetration laws remain physically valuable, but their applicability narrows when the observation window is dominated by the start-of-injection (SOI) and end-of-injection (EOI) transients. The right-censoring problem identified in Chapter 2 is treated here as a target-construction constraint that propagates through the fitting, heteroscedastic uncertainty, and distillation stages.

\paragraph{Terminological Conventions.} Three framing concepts are used repeatedly in this chapter and the subsequent two chapters; their operational definitions are provided here once to avoid repetitive explanations in the text:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Censoring-aware target construction}: Treating field-of-view right-censoring as a source of label-level target bias, addressed progressively across three tiers---fitting, teacher training, and raw supervision mixing---rather than merely applying point-level filtering during the evaluation phase.
  \item \emph{Conservative coverage}: The model's empirical 1$\sigma$/2$\sigma$ coverage on the holdout set is higher than the nominal Gaussian level (68.3\%/95.4\%); this bias is an intentional design choice oriented toward early engineering screening, not a calibration defect.
  \item \emph{Ablation affordability}: Under a master's-level computational budget, the selected architecture permits a full leave-one-nozzle-out (LONO) $\times$ variant $\times$ seed grid search across multiple orthogonal design axes, thereby providing traceable marginal evidence for each design decision.
\end{itemize}
Whenever these three terms reappear hereafter, they should be understood according to these definitions. In addition, this chapter introduces the following four operational naming conventions so that readers can build the necessary associations before encountering them in context:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{q1 reduced-order fit}: A three-parameter smooth target obtained by fitting each plume trajectory with a sigmoid-gated quarter-root single branch, $S(t)=\sigma\!\left((t-t_0)/s\right)k_{1/4}\,t^{1/4}$; ``q'' stands for quarter-root and ``1'' for single branch. Whenever ``q1 trajectory'' or ``q1 archive'' appears in the text, it refers to fitted curves or parameter sets produced by this model, not to the statistical first quartile~(q1).
  \item \emph{Amplitude scaling factor $A$}: $A=\Delta P^{0.5}\cdot\rho_a^{-0.25}\cdot d_n^{0.5}$, a physics-motivated combination of operating conditions using Hiroyasu--Arai exponents as a prior and corrected by regression, used to non-dimensionalize the penetration target ($\hat{S}=S/A$) and to recover physical predictions at inference time ($\mu_S=A\hat{\mu}$). Wherever the text refers to ``dimensionless target'' or ``scaled space'', division by this factor is implied.
  \item \emph{Three derived data populations (Datasets \#1/\#2/\#3)}: Three structurally distinct populations read from the same cleaned CDF archive---\#1 is the raw frame-level observations containing right-censored entries, \#2 is a statistically truncated uncensored point set (including the P50/q1 oracle, used only for evaluation), and \#3 is the per-trajectory q1 fitted archive (the training source for Stages~1 and~2). The three populations serve different roles in downstream training and evaluation and are not interchangeable.
  \item \emph{LONO (Leave-One-Nozzle-Out evaluation)}: An evaluation protocol in which all operating conditions belonging to one nozzle family are removed entirely from training and validation, and holdout error is reported exclusively on that family---distinguishing it from the standard group-aware test set drawn within the training domain; the latter measures in-domain interpolation, while LONO measures generalization to nozzle geometries never seen during training.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Problem Reframing and Observation Conventions}
\label{sec:surrogate-problem-framing-en}

The modeling task in this chapter is intentionally reframed as a low-dimensional probabilistic regression problem rather than a raw image prediction problem. Chapter 3 has already converted the top-view Mie records into plume-resolved temporal observations. Therefore, the surrogate model receives operating conditions, geometric descriptors, and time, and predicts the conditional penetration distribution,
\[
x=\{t,\theta_{\mathrm{tilt}},N_{\mathrm{plume}},t_{\mathrm{inj}},P_{\mathrm{cb}},P_{\mathrm{inj}},P_{\mathrm{ch}},d_n,\rho_a\}
\;\longmapsto\;
\{\mu_S(t),\sigma_S(t)\}.
\]
The scalar target \(S(t)\) is measured in millimeters, and \(\sigma_S(t)\) is interpreted as a heteroscedastic conditional spread rather than a single global residual band.

Among the penetration descriptors exported by the image processing workflow, CDF penetration is used as the primary training and evaluation observation. Binary axial penetration \(S_{\mathrm{BW,x}}\) and polar/boundary penetration surrogate \(R_{\mathrm{BW}}\) remain important consistency checks and geometric diagnostics, but they inherit thresholding, morphological, and boundary interpretation choices from the binary support images. The CDF front, in contrast, tracks the axial cumulative support of the cleaned plume strips, making it less sensitive to isolated leading pixels and aligning more directly with the time-series target required by the surrogate. Therefore, unless explicitly marked otherwise, all training and evaluation protocols below use the CDF penetration convention.

This reframing also clarifies the role of uncertainty. The objective is not merely to fit a smooth mean curve through every measured point; the downstream screening task requires a distribution over plausible penetrations at each time point. Consequently, this chapter processes the archive in three complementary ways: as a collection of raw time-indexed measurements, as a statistically truncated point-level evaluation set, and as an archive of smoothed fitted trajectories that can serve as a smooth teacher. The remainder of this chapter will define these data populations, construct the fitting targets, and then compare neural network-based and kernel-based method backbones on the same scaled regression problem.

Before constructing fitting targets, we quantify the severity of the CDF penetration right-censoring problem directly from the image processing output. This audit defines censoring spatially, rather than based on the subsequent \(\SI{5}{ms}\) surrogate model horizon. For each nozzle family \(n\), all positive raw CDF penetration values derived from the multi-hole Mie workflow are pooled, and a field-of-view (FOV) cap \(C_n\) is estimated as the \(q=0.999\) quantile. A plume trajectory's raw or cleaned CDF trace is flagged as spatially right-censored if it reaches \(C_n-\epsilon_n\), where \(\epsilon_n=\max(\SI{1}{mm},0.01C_n)\). This audit is implemented in the spatial right-censoring audit script, which compares the raw Mie output in the image processing directory with the fitting targets in the synthetic data export directory (codebase identifiers are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:implementation_mapping}).

The resulting scalar diagnostic is the spatial censoring rate,
\[
\mathrm{SCR}
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\mathbf{1}\!\left[
\max_t S_i^{\mathrm{raw/clean}}(t)
\ge C_{n(i)}-\epsilon_{n(i)}
\right],
\]
where \(n(i)\) is the nozzle family of trajectory \(i\).

The cleaned split contains \(\num{\scrNTotal}\) CDF penetration trajectories, of which \(\num{\scrNCensored}\) satisfy this spatial right-censoring criterion, yielding a spatial censoring rate of \(\scrRatePct\%\). The median nozzle-specific cap is \(\SI{92.52}{mm}\). Among the censored cleaned trajectories, the median time to first hit the cap is \(\SI{1.76}{ms}\), equivalent to \(44.4\%\) of the raw positive observation span. If a naive target construction method simply held the final cleaned CDF penetration value forward, its median gap relative to the accepted q1 continuation at \(\SI{5}{ms}\) would be approximately \(\SI{\scrNaiveHoldGapMedianMm}{mm}\); if the cap itself were treated as a terminal saturation value, the corresponding median gap would still be \(\SI{43.96}{mm}\). These continuation gaps are not viewed as errors against a wide-field ground truth. They quantify the magnitude of target bias that would be introduced by reading cap-hitting trajectories as physical saturation observations.

\subsubsection{Three Populations Derived from the Cleaned CDF Archive}
\label{sec:three-derived-populations-en}

The cleaned CDF archive is not used as a single homogeneous dataset. Downstream code reads it through three structurally distinct populations, each with its own selection biases and role in the surrogate protocol.

\textbf{Dataset \#1: Raw Cleaned CDF Observations.}
This population consists of the per-frame \((t,S)\) observations that passed Chapter 3's CDF extraction, hydraulic delay alignment, jump truncation, and short-segment filtering. It is stored in long and wide formats in the cleaned CDF sequence exports. The cleaned archive contains approximately \(24{,}316\) accepted trajectories and \(703{,}592\) individual frame observations. Its advantage is point-wise fidelity: these rows are the closest objects in the pipeline to the measured penetration records, and they preserve the time-varying noise and inter-plume spread. Its limitation is the censoring quantified above: late-time bins are depleted by FOV hits and signal decay, so raw supervision is only trustworthy when coverage remains high. Thus, Stage 3 uses this population directly only in the reliable raw CDF regime.

\textbf{Dataset \#2: Statistically Truncated Points and P50/q1 Oracle.}
This population is derived by aggregating the observations in Dataset \#1 per operating condition. A point-level table bins time across \(0\)--\(\SI{5}{ms}\) at \(\Delta t=\SI{0.1}{ms}\) intervals, then truncates each condition at the first time bin satisfying either a density drop criterion or a FOV saturation criterion: the smoothed trajectory count per bin drops below a fixed fraction of its intra-condition peak, or the per-bin \(p_{95}\) penetration reaches \(95\%\) of the estimated FOV cap. The resulting uncensored point set contains about \(506{,}634\) points spanning \(592\) operating conditions. An accompanying P50/q1 oracle is constructed by taking the per-bin median penetration for each surviving condition and fitting a q1 to this median trace; it covers \(495\) conditions and provides a condition-level reference curve. This dataset is not used for training. It serves as a lower-bias evaluation yardstick for uncensored CDF accuracy, P50 observation behavior, Q1 extrapolation diagnostics, leave-one-nozzle-out (LONO) evaluation, and the comparison between MLP and Sparse Variational Gaussian Process (SVGP).

\textbf{Dataset \#3: Per-Trajectory q1 Fitted Archive.}
For every accepted plume trajectory, the reduced-order q1 model described in Section~\ref{sec:q1-target-construction-en} stores the fitted parameters and associated fit diagnostics. This population provides smooth, differentiable, and extrapolatable targets over the surrogate model horizon. Stage 1 selects a representative fitted trajectory from this population for each operating condition group, and Stage 2 trains on all filtered q1 trajectories that pass robust post-fit gating. The same filtering makes Dataset \#3 usable as a clean teacher source but also endows it with an explicit selection bias: it favors optically clean, monotonic trajectories that are well-represented by q1.

\subsection{Censoring-Aware q1 Target Construction}
\label{sec:q1-target-construction-en}

\subsubsection{Trajectory Cleaning and Time Alignment}

The prerequisite for fitting and learning is that all penetration curves are aligned with the start of injection (SOI) as time zero: whether it is a polynomial empirical model or a neural network, its fitted parameters are highly sensitive to the time origin. If the start times of individual plumes are scattered across different frames, families of curves under the same operating conditions will exhibit systematic offsets, preventing the model from learning the true penetration dynamics.

The learning model is not directly trained on the raw exported trajectories. Instead, the cleaning and fitting routines first clean and align them. For each plume, the routine finds the earliest valid penetration segment, takes the first two positive samples after onset, fits a line to these early non-zero points, and uses the \(x\)-axis intercept of this line as the sub-frame onset delay estimate if the fitted slope is positive. If an area-based onset marker exists (projected area derived by summing foreground pixels of the binarized spray image per frame and converting via pixel-to-physical scale), this frame anchors the delay sweep; otherwise, it falls back to the first positive penetration frame. The resulting raw delay is then clipped to a small, median-centered window (across plumes) so that a single anomalous plume does not excessively shift the entire group.

This delay estimate is used in two coupled ways. The penetration array itself is shifted left by the nearest integer number of frames, while the aligned time vector retains the fractional residual through an exact offset subtraction. This pipeline performs plume onset registration, preserving sub-frame temporal information prior to the subsequent reduced-order fitting. After alignment, sudden unphysical jumps are removed, and low-quality fits are flagged via a set of rule-based masks. The residual offset between plumes post-alignment typically does not exceed three frames; hydraulic delay (the time difference between the electrical signal and spray appearance) is not formally statistically analyzed across operating conditions, but the temporal magnitude corresponding to the aforementioned frame-level spread at the current frame rate is much smaller than the injection duration.

\subsubsection{Two-Stage Candidate Model}

Hiroyasu and Arai described diesel spray penetration as two regimes: pre-breakup growth proportional to \(t\), and post-breakup quasi-steady growth proportional to \(t^{1/2}\), with the breakup time \(t_b\) marking the transition \cite{HiroyasuArai1990}. In pilot diesel injection conditions, however, \(t_b\) falls very early in the observable window---empirically only a few camera frames---so the pre-breakup linear regime occupies too little of the signal to fit reliably. Zhou et al.\ further established that the SOI acceleration phase follows an uncertain exponent that is difficult to characterise from short, noisy Mie trajectories \cite{ZhouLiYi2021}; in practice, the fitting window begins effectively in the post-\(t_b\) regime. From this starting point, two functional primitives are chosen: \(\sqrt{t}\) to span the early post-breakup quasi-steady regime familiar from the H--A framework, and \(t^{1/4}\) to capture the empirically observed late-stage deceleration associated with post-injection dynamics \cite{ZhouLiLaiWei2019}. Rather than imposing an explicit delayed clock \((t-t_i)\)---which would require reliably identifying the end-of-injection moment from noisy per-plume records---a sigmoid gating weight \(w(t)\) interpolates continuously between the two branches, making the transition differentiable and independent of any external physical feature.

Each accepted trajectory is then fitted with a smooth two-stage model,
\[
S(t)=\left(1-w(t)\right)k_{\mathrm{sqrt}}\sqrt{t}
+w(t)k_{\mathrm{quarter}}t^{1/4},
\qquad
w(t)=\sigma\!\left(\frac{t-t_0}{s}\right),
\]
where \(k_{\mathrm{sqrt}}\), \(k_{\mathrm{quarter}}\), \(t_0\), and \(s\) are optimized in log-space. Specifically, the fitting problem is framed as a robust nonlinear least squares estimation,
\[
\min_{\theta}
\sum_i \rho_\delta\!\left(S(t_i;\theta)-y_i\right),
\qquad
\theta=
\left[\log k_{\mathrm{sqrt}},\log k_{\mathrm{quarter}},\log t_0,\log s\right],
\]
using the Huber penalty function
\[
\rho_\delta(r)=
\begin{cases}
\tfrac{1}{2}r^2, & |r|\le \delta,\\
\delta\left(|r|-\tfrac{1}{2}\delta\right), & |r|>\delta.
\end{cases}
\]
where \(r = S(t_i;\theta)-y_i\) is the fit residual, and \(\delta\) is the threshold where the penalty transitions from squared error to absolute error. This objective is solved via trust-region nonlinear least squares with \(\delta=1\), while the logarithmic parameterization preserves the positivity of the fit gains and transition scale\cite{Huber1964,NocedalWright2006}.

Therefore, this model should be read as a temporal parameterization rather than an explicit physical law. Its purposes are twofold: to provide credible extrapolation far from the EOI (downstream surrogate models require stable targets) and acceptable interpolation near the SOI (where relative errors are large and early apparent physics are hard to constrain from the image trajectory itself). The sigmoid gating is important for this role. Rather than imposing a hard breakpoint between two empirical branches, it creates a differentiable transition, which maintains the smoothness of the fitted curve and makes it more compatible with subsequent neural network stages. In other words, early transient physics are not forced into a strict closed-form law here; they are merely approximated well enough to allow the subsequent network to learn the remaining condition-dependent behavior from the full operating point inputs.
When compared to the extended EOI expressions in the spray literature, this interpretation becomes clearer. The post-EOI model of Zhou et al. distinguishes between a global injection clock \(t\) and a delayed clock \((t-t_i)\), because the late-stage penetration branch is activated only after the end-of-injection dynamics propagate downstream\cite{ZhouLiLaiWei2019}. The present pipeline handles this delay in two layers. First, the preprocessing stage estimates a plume-specific onset delay based on the early linear extrapolation described above, shifting the observed sequence to a common \(t\approx 0\) reference. Second, the reduced-order fit itself still does not incorporate an explicit delayed clock within the parameter branch. Instead, it uses
\[
w(t)=\sigma\!\left(\frac{t-t_0}{s}\right)
\]
to suppress the quarter-root branch prior to the learned transition time \(t_0\), allowing the \(t^{1/4}\) term to dominate only after this transition. Algebraically, this is not equivalent to replacing \(t^{1/4}\) with \((t-t_i)^{1/4}\). Rather, it is an engineering reparameterization: over a finite observation window, a delayed quarter-root branch can typically be adequately represented by a gated, unshifted quarter-root branch with adjusted amplitude and transition placement, especially when the early portion of that branch is strongly attenuated by \(1-w(t)\) or a near-zero \(w(t)\). In this sense, the physical clock of the delay is absorbed into the learned switching parameters \((t_0,s)\) and the branch amplitude, rather than being imposed as a fixed, known constant.

In summary, the engineering treatment above is reasonable for the goals of this thesis, but its applicability has clear boundaries. From an engineering perspective, compressing the cleaned discrete observations into a few parameters via curve fitting not only smooths out measurement noise but also reconstructs $y = f(t)$ at any arbitrary time bin density, permitting extrapolation within reasonable limits---something that cannot be done directly with discrete observations. Its justification lies in three points: the fit acts here as a smooth compressor for noisy trajectories, not as a final mechanistic spray law; it preserves the differentiability of the curve, facilitating subsequent learning; and it delegates the operating condition dependencies to the neural network modeling, eliminating the need to hard-code every physical effect into a closed-form analytical expression. However, this treatment should be understood as a reduced-order surrogate modeling strategy inspired by empirical penetration theory, rather than a proof that shifted-time physical effects have vanished. The ablation experiment below involving a quarter-root-only branch explicitly contrasts this.

\subsubsection{Quarter-Root Dominance and Selection of the q1 Production Model}

If \(k_{\mathrm{sqrt}}\) is virtually zero in practice, then retaining this degree of freedom is not merely redundant---it could actively harm performance on difficult trajectories. A quantitative inspection across the entire accepted fitting archive directly corroborates the former; the ablation experiment below confirms the latter.

Direct quantitative inspection of the \(14{,}563\) accepted plume fits in the polar binary penetration archive explicitly confirms this. At the fit transition time \(t_0\), the amplitudes of the two branches are \(k_{1/4}\cdot t_0^{1/4}\) (quarter-root) and \(k_{\mathrm{sqrt}}\cdot t_0^{1/2}\) (square-root); their ratio is
\[
r = \frac{k_{\mathrm{sqrt}}\cdot t_0^{1/4}}{k_{1/4}}.
\]
Across all accepted fits, the median ratio is \(r_{\mathrm{med}}=2.9\times10^{-9}\), with an interquartile range of \([9.0\times10^{-10},\;1.1\times10^{-8}]\). In \(99.8\%\) of accepted fits, \(r<0.5\); in \(100\%\), \(r<1\). Expressed in absolute magnitude, the median quarter-root branch contributes \(\SI{69.2}{mm}\) at \(t_0\), while the median square-root branch contributes \(2.0\times10^{-7}\,\si{mm}\)---a difference of eight orders of magnitude. Figure~\ref{fig:ksqrt-ratio} shows the complete distribution of \(\log_{10}(r)\) (panel a) and the quarter-root amplitude distribution (panel b). In practice, for every fit in this archive, the square-root term is negligible at the transition time, causing successful fits to degenerate entirely into a quarter-root dominated curve. This is acceptable for its intended use: physical drivers are later handed over directly to the neural network, while the fit itself remains adaptive and physics-light.

This observation directly motivates a structural ablation experiment: if successful fits already converge to a quarter-root dominated curve, the remaining \(k_{\mathrm{sqrt}}\) degree of freedom might act as a destructive degree of freedom on harder trajectories. When the solver cannot find a well-constrained minimum on a short or noisy trajectory, an unconstrained \(\sqrt{t}\) term might grow during optimization and drive the far-time extrapolation to physically unreasonable values, to the point that the shielding pipeline rejects the sequence, even though the quarter-root component alone might have fitted it adequately.

A simplified single-branch model, dubbed q1 (for quarter-root, 1-branch), eliminates this risk by replacing the two-stage mixture with a single sigmoid-gated quarter-root branch,
\[
S_{q1}(t) = \sigma\!\left(\frac{t-t_0}{s}\right)\,k_{1/4}\,t^{1/4},
\]
reducing the number of parameters from four to three: \([\log k_{1/4},\,\log t_0,\,\log s]\). An equivalent shielding pipeline was built in parallel for q1: evaluating the fitted curve at \(t_{\mathrm{far}}=\SI{5}{ms}\) and checking the same physical plausibility window \([18,\,300]\,\si{mm}\); applying the same \(t_0 < \SI{0.8}{ms}\) and \(s < \SI{1}{ms}\) bounds; and computing identical grouped robust \(z\) outlier scores on \(t_0\), RMSE, and cost-per-point. When a sequence passes the full shielding pipeline and additionally satisfies \(\mathrm{RMSE} < \SI{3}{mm}\), it is counted as a successful fit, making the success criteria for both models symmetric and directly comparable.

Out of the full \(23{,}495\) sequences in the nine-nozzle CDF penetration campaign (after discarding those shorter than ten valid frames), the four-parameter main model achieved a \(61.3\%\) success rate, while the q1 model achieved \(90.0\%\)---an absolute improvement of 28.6 percentage points, without a single nozzle showing a regression. On the cleaned subset that passed both models, the median RMSE was \(\SI{1.23}{mm}\) for the main model and \(\SI{1.27}{mm}\) for q1, confirming there is no cost to removing \(k_{\mathrm{sqrt}}\) on well-behaved data. On the flagged subset, the contrast is starker: the median RMSE dropped from \(\SI{8.12}{mm}\) to \(\SI{1.08}{mm}\), a reduction of roughly \(7.5\times\). The largest gains were in the hardest-hit datasets---nozzle 0 improved by 37.6 percentage points, and nozzle 7 by 28.7 percentage points---precisely the conditions where the post-impingement deceleration phase makes the \(t^{1/4}\) form the dominant descriptor, rendering the \(\sqrt{t}\) branch physically uninformative.

Based on this ablation, q1 was promoted to the production fitting target, and the smooth targets used by Stage 1, Stage 2, and Stage 3 are all generated by q1 rather than the four-parameter candidate model. The four-parameter results are retained merely as a historical baseline for structural diagnostics and to motivate the model reduction step. Consequently, the practical purpose of this fit is not to claim a universal penetration law, but rather to compress noisy plume trajectories into smooth, differentiable, and trainable targets, while preserving the heteroscedastic variations within the dataset for the subsequent learning stages.

\subsubsection{q1 Diagnostics: Scaling, Residuals, and Identifiability}

\textbf{Empirical Scaling Check.} After fitting each trajectory, three parameter values \((k_{1/4},\,t_0,\,s)\) are obtained. Because the fit constraints force the gating parameters \((t_0,s)\) to make the sigmoid product converge to near 1 within a finite time window, the end of the observable time period and the extrapolation segment satisfy \(S(t)\approx k_{1/4}\cdot t^{1/4}\); therefore, \(k_{1/4}\) is not an abstract fit amplitude, but the asymptotic linear scaling coefficient of the \(t^{1/4}\) penetration law, which can be directly compared with the proportionality coefficients of classical empirical formulas. Performing an ex-post log-log regression on it allows testing whether the fitting results follow the power-law expected by classical penetration theory with respect to the operating conditions. Regressing across \(24{,}316\) accepted fits via Ordinary Least Squares:
\[
  \log k_{1/4} = a\,\log\Delta P + b\,\log\rho_a + c\,\log d_n + \text{constant}
\]
yields exponents \(a\approx 0.52\), \(b\approx -0.23\), \(c\approx 0.26\), and \(R^2\approx 0.27\).

Comparison against classical Hiroyasu--Arai theory reveals varying degrees of agreement. The density exponent \(b\approx-0.23\) almost perfectly matches the \(-1/4\) in the H--A post-breakup form \(S\propto\Delta P^{1/4}\rho_a^{-1/4}(d_n t)^{1/2}\), serving as a positive signal for the physical plausibility of the fitted parameter. The injection pressure exponent \(a\approx 0.52\), however, is markedly steeper than the H--A predicted \(1/4\); the exact reason for this deviation is uncertain---the condition coverage of the dataset, the q1 functional form, and the possibility that the \(t^{1/4}\) branch absorbed contributions beyond the free-jet phase are all compatible with this result. The diameter exponent \(c\approx 0.26\) is much lower than the classical \(1/2\), most likely reflecting collinearity between nozzle diameter and other operating variables in the experimental matrix (independently confirmed in Section~\ref{sec:pointlevel-scaling-verification}). The regression \(R^2\) for the gating parameters \(t_0\) and \(s\) are only \(0.17\) and \(0.14\), respectively---which is expected: the sigmoid switching parameters are not directly predicted by classical theory; they mainly absorb residual onset delay variations after preprocessing alignment.

As a cross-check, direct log-log regressions on the raw discrete observations within fixed \(\SI{0.1}{ms}\) time bins yielded an \(R^2\) of roughly \(0.47\)--\(0.51\) in the mid-trajectory window (\(0.35\)--\(0.75\) ms), confirming that the three physical variables indeed explain a meaningful portion of the raw penetration variance in the well-sampled region. The lower \(R^2\approx0.27\) obtained from the compressed \(k_{1/4}\) parameter reflects the integral effect over noisy onset and post-injection regions rather than a model failure; the qualitative trends of the \(\Delta P\) exponent (\(0.44\)--\(0.65\), between the Bernoulli value \(0.5\) and H--A value \(0.25\)) and the chamber pressure exponent (\(\approx-0.25\)) remain consistent across both analyses.

\textbf{Residual Structure and Heteroscedasticity.} Diagnostics of the temporal distribution of the fit residuals reveal the systematic behavior of the q1 model across different trajectory stages, providing the physical rationale for the three-stage training and mixed supervision design. Reconstructing the residual \(\hat S(t)-y(t)\) at each observation frame for every cleaned trajectory and binning by \(\Delta t=\SI{0.1}{ms}\) yields the empirical envelope in Figure~\ref{fig:q1-residual-structure}, revealing two distinct regions of differing nature. In the central window \(t\in[0.3, 1.0]\,\si{ms}\), the median residual remains below \(\SI{0.4}{mm}\), and the robust standard deviation \(\sigma_{\mathrm{robust}}(t)=1.4826\,\mathrm{MAD}(t)\) reaches a minimum of \(\sim\SI{0.60}{mm}\) around \(t=\SI{1.0}{ms}\)---indicating that q1 is essentially unbiased and tight in the well-sampled mid-trajectory region.

Systematic biases exist at both boundary ends: an early positive bias of \(\sim\SI{+1.7}{mm}\) at \(t=\SI{0.05}{ms}\) with \(\sigma_{\mathrm{robust}}\approx\SI{1.8}{mm}\), stemming from imprecise onset alignment and the steep transition of the sigmoid gate near \(t_0\); and a late negative bias of \(\sim\SI{-1.6}{mm}\) at \(t=\SI{1.55}{ms}\) with \(\sigma_{\mathrm{robust}}\approx\SI{0.7}{mm}\), arising from the right-censoring of the surviving plume population---where the frame count per bin drops drastically from \(\sim 65{,}000\) at \(t=\SI{0.15}{ms}\) to fewer than \(200\) frames by \(t=\SI{1.55}{ms}\). Thus, the envelope is U-shaped rather than monotonically growing. This dual-end bias is the core motivation for introducing mixed supervision in Stage 3: the q1 fitting target exhibits structural errors in both the early pre-injection segment and the late observable tail, which ultimately need to be corrected via direct supervision signals rather than relying solely on the extension of the fitted curve. Moreover, the significant temporal variation of the residual scale indicates that a fixed-variance loss is inappropriate, necessitating the use of input-dependent \(\sigma\) in Stage 2's negative log-likelihood (NLL) loss.

\textbf{Parameter Identifiability.} The third diagnostic examines whether the three q1 parameters can be independently determined by the data---if strong mutual compensation exists among parameters, the individual parameter values themselves will be hard to interpret, and regressions against operating conditions will be weak. To this end, using the trust-region Jacobian \(J\) returned by SciPy, the asymptotic covariance \(\Sigma\approx(J^TJ)^{-1}\hat\sigma^2\) is computed, yielding pairwise parameter correlations. The median results across all cleaned fits are: \(\overline{\mathrm{corr}}(\log k_{1/4},\log s)\approx0.76\), \(\overline{\mathrm{corr}}(\log k_{1/4},\log t_0)\approx0.56\), \(\overline{\mathrm{corr}}(\log t_0,\log s)\approx0.21\).

These correlations suggest that the three q1 parameters are not independent physical knobs, but rather a coupled 3D smooth surface with considerable soft redundancy. This is the price paid for introducing the differentiable sigmoid gate (in contrast to the rigid stage breakpoints of Hiroyasu--Arai), and it also explains why the gating parameters showed weak scaling relationships against operating conditions in the earlier regression: the data constrains the surface as a whole, rather than constraining each axis individually. Regarding downstream design, the surrogate model (Section~\ref{sec:mlp-curriculum-en}) is trained to reproduce the entire evaluation curve \(\hat S(\cdot)\) rather than the parameter triplet itself, so strong parameter coupling does not propagate into the input-output mapping of the network.

\subsubsection{Outlier Shielding and Filtration Selection Bias}

A stricter post-fit shielding stage is also an important component of the engineering contribution. The robust outlier mask first evaluates the fitted curve at \(t_{\mathrm{far}}=\SI{5}{ms}\) to form a far-time penetration surrogate. The row is retained only if this far-time value remains within a broad physical plausibility window, and if the fit parameters pass basic sanity checks on \(t_0\), \(s\), point count, and fit cost. Robust grouped outlier scores are then computed using the median absolute deviation,
\[
z_{\mathrm{robust}}(x)=0.6745\,\frac{x-\operatorname{median}(x)}{\operatorname{MAD}(x)+\epsilon},
\qquad
\operatorname{MAD}(x)=\operatorname{median}\!\left(\lvert x-\operatorname{median}(x)\rvert\right),
\]
applied to \(t_0\), \(\log(1+\mathrm{RMSE})\), and \(\log(1+\mathrm{cost\_per\_point})\) within each file group. Rows with \(|z_{\mathrm{robust}}|>3\) on any of these diagnostics are flagged as outliers and excluded from the cleaned archive.

This shielding process introduces intentional engineering trade-offs, and its primary mechanism shifted alongside improvements in the upstream computer vision (CV) pipeline. In early processing, uneven illumination or inter-plume occlusion sometimes led the CV pipeline to mistake background noise for spray tips, generating physically impossible jumps that were subsequently truncated into severely shortened sequences by explicit upward-difference thresholds; the \(n \ge 10\) frame lower limit and \(t_0 < \SI{0.8}{ms}\) sanity gate were primarily designed to intercept these degenerate trajectories. Following CV enhancements, the vast majority of candidate sequences now pass the full shielding pipeline (per-nozzle survival rates are reported in Figure~\ref{fig:filter-survival-cdf-nozzle}). Flagged sequences are typically *longer* trajectories whose \(t_0\), RMSE, or \(\mathrm{cost\_per\_point}\) exceed the three-sigma robust threshold within their group, rather than short noise-truncated sequences stemming from CV misidentification. As a result, the shielding logic now serves more as a fit-quality gate than a CV artifact cleaner.

Therefore, the filtering process introduces selection bias along two dimensions. The first is optical quality bias: the surviving dataset favors well-illuminated, unoccluded, high-contrast plumes and does not represent true geometric asymmetry or late-time signal decay. The second is injection profile regularity bias: q1 is structurally limited to monotonically increasing smooth penetration curves, while trajectories with non-monotonic behaviors (arising from bi-modal injection or spray re-acceleration) carry higher intra-group RMSE and are flagged by the robust z-score outlier gate. The per-nozzle flag rate quantifies the resulting coverage gap: on the CDF source, the flag rate for nozzle 3 is 11.9\%, roughly double that of nozzles 4 and 6 (both 5.9\%), which is consistent with the spray re-acceleration events observed for nozzle 3 in the Stage 3 diagnostics. Thus, Stage 1 and Stage 2 jointly inherit both biases: their training sets lean toward optically ideal plumes and trajectories whose injection profiles are sufficiently regular to be adequately represented by the monotonic q1 form.

Figure~\ref{fig:filter-survival-cdf-nozzle} illustrates the q1 filter survival rates for CDF penetration per nozzle, summarizing the total sequence counts, cleaned sequence counts, and passing ratios. The purpose of this plot is not to argue that the filter is unbiased, but to clarify the footprint of that bias. Upon regenerating the report against the recovered PC archive, the nine-nozzle campaign spans a CDF penetration survival band of 88.1\%--94.1\%. The recovered nozzle 0 archive contributed \(\num{15475}\) candidate CDF penetration sequences, of which \(\num{14274}\) passed the clean fit filter (92.2\%), making it part of the reported coverage. Because no nozzle comes close to being completely rejected or completely retained, Stage 2 can be interpreted as a clean-plume teacher over the recovered archive, while still inheriting the explicit spatial and late-time selection biases documented here.

This trade-off is deliberate but bounded. By excluding ambiguous data, the pipeline provides Stage 2 with a clean teacher target that is less susceptible to optical vanishing effects, background reflections, and incorrect hydraulic delay alignment. Stage 3 then recombines this clean prior with the raw, unfiltered CDF penetration data: it trusts the raw observations in high-confidence intervals and falls back to the Stage 2 teacher where coverage collapses. The result is a controlled interpolation between raw signal fidelity at early times and physical consistency at late times.

This design does not erase the selection bias introduced by the filtering. The teacher's late-time prior is still conditioned on well-illuminated, unoccluded plumes; when Stage 3 relies on it, the output should be interpreted as a continuation of a clean plume rather than a debiased ensemble across all nozzle geometries.

\textbf{Overall Assessment of the q1 Reduced-Order Surrogate Model.} The synthesized evidence positions \(S(t)=\sigma((t-t_0)/s)\,k_{1/4}\,t^{1/4}\) as a numerically robust reduced-order trajectory surrogate model, not a new closed-form penetration law. The kinematic exponent \(t^{1/4}\) inherits the stage-shifting shape used by Hiroyasu and Arai\cite{HiroyasuArai1990} and the post-EOI extension by Zhou et al.\cite{ZhouLiLaiWei2019,ZhouLiYi2021}, while the sigmoid gating replaces their hard breakpoints to provide a differentiable transition---a structural prerequisite for using the fitted curve as a target in the downstream neural network surrogate. The empirical \(k_{1/4}\) scaling aligns very closely with the Hiroyasu--Arai free-jet prediction in terms of \(\rho_a\) and \(d_n\); the steeper \(\Delta P\) exponent is reported as an empirical observation rather than a confirmed mechanistic shift. The four-parameter mixture collapses to \(k_{\mathrm{sqrt}}\!\approx\!0\) in 99.8\% of fits, indicating that the data itself selects the quarter-root branch, and the central-window robust spread of \(\sim\SI{0.60}{mm}\) approaches the optical noise floor of the imaging chain.

Two limitations are worth stating explicitly. First, the three q1 parameters are not independent physical knobs---the soft redundancy between the branch amplitude and the sigmoid gate makes \((k_{1/4}, t_0, s)\) a coupled smooth surface rather than three separate coefficients. Second, the dimensional group \((\Delta P, \rho_a, d_n)\) explains only \(R^2\!\approx\!0.27\) of the log-space variance of \(k_{1/4}\). Both issues are absorbed by the surrogate model stack design: Stages 1-3 train the network to reproduce the evaluation curve \(\hat S(\cdot)\) rather than the parameter triplet, so the coupling does not propagate to the trained input-output map; and the unexplained trajectory-level variance is precisely the heteroscedastic residual that Stage 2's NLL and Stage 3's distillation are introduced to handle. The primary usage regime of \(0\)--\(\SI{1.5}{ms}\) lies well within the data-dominated region of the q1 fit, whereas boundary inflation and parameter coupling primarily affect the far-field \(>\SI{2}{ms}\), where Stage 3 already leans on the teacher's conservative envelope.

In the population terminology of Section~\ref{sec:three-derived-populations-en}, the q1 fits define Dataset \#3. This is the smooth archive from which the Stage 2 teacher learns, including both its useful clean-plume priors and its filtration biases. Stage 3 subsequently reintroduces Dataset \#1 during reliable coverage intervals to recover raw early-time behavior without treating the right-censored tail as fully observed values.

\subsection{Physics-Guided Feature Engineering}
\label{sec:feature-engineering-en}

Before the engineered feature architecture was finalized, an early nine-feature MLP trained using direct pressure and diameter inputs revealed a structural problem. Nozzle diameter exists at only six levels in the dataset, and these are not uniformly available across all pressure combinations---all six diameter levels appear exclusively within the family \((P_{\mathrm{inj}}=\SI{2000}{bar},\;P_{\mathrm{cb}}=4,\;P_{\mathrm{ch}}\in\{5,10,15\}\,\si{bar})\)---whereas injection pressure has only four levels. Figure~\ref{fig:sparse-support-topology} makes this discretely sampled grid explicit: most cells in the (chamber pressure, injection pressure, diameter) grid contain zero or one observation, and full six-diameter coverage exists only along a single pressure family.

Left: observed injection pressure support counts on the (chamber pressure, control back pressure) plane, with available levels annotated inside each cell. \end{figure}

Because a smooth MLP treats these as continuous inputs, it generates derivative structures along the diameter and pressure axes that the data does not constrain. Figure~\ref{fig:sparse-diameter-interpolants} shows the result of dense diameter sweeps at \(t=\SI{0.85}{ms}\): between adjacent observation levels, the predicted mean curve undergoes five to six gradient sign reversals per slice, and autodiff gradients peak at \(\sim\!\SI{4e4}{}\) over a support span of about \(\SI{0.05}{mm}\), equating to a curvature norm on the order of \(10^7\). The same diagnostic on injection pressure showed non-monotonic, unsupported extrapolation over sparsely supported slices. The root issue is structural---a smooth MLP is asked to learn continuous laws from what is effectively a discrete grid---so adding regularization can only suppress the instability at the cost of useful capacity. Therefore, the revised architecture addresses the problem at the source.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{images/fig_sparse_diameter_interpolants.png}
\caption{Dense MLP interpolation under the direct-feature baseline, interpolating across the nozzle diameter axis at \(t=\SI{0.85}{ms}\) for three operating points (columns). Top row: predicted mean (blue) compared with the observational level secant (black dashed) through six diameter levels (orange). \label{fig:sparse-diameter-interpolants}
\end{figure}

The revised surrogate model replaces the four weakly supported pressure and diameter inputs with a single hard-coded scaling group. The Hiroyasu--Arai post-breakup branch\cite{HiroyasuArai1990} provides the dimensional motivation:
\[
S(t)\;\propto\;K_p\!\left(\frac{\Delta P}{\rho_a}\right)^{1/4}d_n^{1/2}\,t^{1/2},
\]
where the H--A exponent assigns \(\Delta P^{1/4}\) to the pressure ratio group. However, the empirical scaling regression reported earlier found a \(\Delta P\) exponent of roughly \(0.52\) in the mid-injection window---about twice the H--A value---so the amplitude scale is updated according to the data:
\[
A\;=\;\Delta P^{0.5}\cdot\rho_a^{-0.25}\cdot d_n^{0.5},
\]
computed from the canonical feature table exported alongside the test matrix JSON file. This form retains the H--A \(\rho_a^{-1/4}\) and \(d_n^{1/2}\) exponents (which empirical regression showed good agreement with), while adopting \(\Delta P^{0.5}\) in place of the H--A \(\Delta P^{1/4}\). For nozzles 1 through 8, the chamber density is taken directly from the logged ambient gas density; for nozzle 0, where only ambient chamber pressure was logged, \(\rho_a\) is reconstructed using a linear law \(\rho_a=\SI{5}{kg/m^3}\times P_{\mathrm{ch}}/\SI{4.42}{bar}\) calibrated against the nozzle 1-8 data.
The exponent shift should not be read as a rejection of the Hiroyasu--Arai asymptote. Time-windowed log-log regressions on the observed CDF penetrations (Figure~\ref{fig:time-windowed-exponents}) reveal a transition: the \(\Delta P\) exponent is close to the Bernoulli/orifice value near the dense mid-trajectory window (\(a\approx0.48\)--\(0.58\) around \(t=\SI{0.4}{ms}\)--\(\SI{0.6}{ms}\)) and approaches the H--A value of \(0.25\) near \(t=\SI{1.0}{ms}\). Thus, the representative training mass is not purely dominated by the late-time entrainment asymptote; it still carries a strong imprint of transition dynamics. The chosen \(\Delta P^{0.5}\) scale is an empirical compromise tailored to the current short-pilot archive, while \(\Delta P^{0.25}\) remains the appropriate late-time physical reference.

Before any MLP weights are updated, a pre-training collapse check makes this assumption falsifiable. This diagnostic reconstructs the trajectories by dividing each trace by its own \(A\), and measures whether the sparse pressure/diameter axes still explain the scaled target. Comparing \(\Delta P^{0.25}\) and \(\Delta P^{0.5}\), both passed the weak criterion that the post-scaling spread remains below the pre-scaling spread after \(\SI{0.5}{ms}\), but \(\Delta P^{0.5}\) collapsed the archive more strongly: the median collapse ratio after \(\SI{0.5}{ms}\) dropped from \(0.378\) to \(0.057\), and the sparse feature \(R^2\) ratio (scaled/physical) dropped from \(0.130\) to \(0.055\). This serves as the pre-training counterpart to the LONO feature ablation discussed below.

In the first design iteration, all four sparse inputs were fully merged into $A$, yielding a five-feature pure-scaling baseline
\[
[\,t_{\mathrm{norm}},\;\theta_{\mathrm{tilt},z},\;n_{\mathrm{plumes},z},\;t_{i,z},\;P_{\mathrm{cb},z}\,],
\]
where the subscript $z$ indicates that a feature has been standardized by subtracting the training-set mean and dividing by the training-set standard deviation (z-score normalization), $t_{\mathrm{norm}}$ denotes time rescaled by a fixed min-max mapping from the $[0,\,\SI{5}{ms}]$ window to $[0,1]$, and both sets of scaler parameters are estimated from the training split only and held fixed for validation and test to prevent leakage.
$A$ was used solely as a target divisor and did not enter the network. The training target was reparameterized as dimensionless penetration $\hat{S}(t)=S(t)/A$; the network predicted $\hat{\mu}$ and $\log\hat{\sigma}^2$, and physical predictions were recovered during inference via $\mu_S=A\,\hat{\mu}$ and $\sigma_S=A\,\hat{\sigma}$. This baseline achieved a Stage 1 test MAE of \SI{8.05}{mm} and a Stage 2 test MAE of \SI{9.28}{mm}.

However, fully absorbing $P_{\mathrm{inj}}$ and $P_{\mathrm{ch}}$ into $A$ eliminated their independent explanatory power beyond what is captured by the fixed $\Delta P^{0.5}$ exponent. Regressions also estimated non-zero chamber pressure coefficients at fixed $\Delta P$ (roughly \(-0.21\) to \(-0.27\) in the mid-injection window), meaning $P_{\mathrm{inj}}$ and $P_{\mathrm{ch}}$ carry independent structure that cannot fully collapse into $\Delta P$. The global $\Delta P^{0.5}$ exponent is itself a mean-field approximation; when $P_{\mathrm{inj}}$ and $P_{\mathrm{ch}}$ are omitted, any local residual non-linearities in the condition space are left out of the network's reach. Nozzle diameter $d_n$, however, is a different case: it exists at only six discrete levels in the dataset and suffers from severe confounding in the condition matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:sparse-support-topology}), making it an unreliable continuous input even if reintroduced. It was therefore retained inside $A$, letting the H--A prior carry its effect.

Building on this, the final design adopted a seven-feature scaling-plus-residual-pressure formulation
\[
[\,t_{\mathrm{norm}},\;\theta_{\mathrm{tilt},z},\;n_{\mathrm{plumes},z},\;t_{i,z},\;P_{\mathrm{cb},z},\;\log P_{\mathrm{inj},z},\;\log P_{\mathrm{ch},z}\,],
\]
where $A$ continues to absorb the dominant diameter and dimensional pressure dependencies, while $\log P_{\mathrm{inj}}$ and $\log P_{\mathrm{ch}}$ are fed in as separate inputs so the network can learn residual pressure structure beyond the fixed H--A exponents. Compared to the five-feature baseline, Stage 1 test MAE dropped from \SI{8.05}{mm} to \SI{2.91}{mm}, and Stage 2 test MAE dropped from \SI{9.28}{mm} to \SI{5.63}{mm}, confirming that the residual pressure features provided information obscured by the fixed H--A exponents.

Before any optimization, a scaling validation routine tests the scaling hypothesis on the actual dataset: each trajectory is divided by its own $A$, and the linear regression $R^2$ of $\hat{S}(t)$ against the sparse features is calculated. In the seven-feature production run, the check passes, with the sparse feature $R^2$ dropping from $0.32$ for the physical target $S$ to $0.016$ for the scaled target $\hat{S}$, indicating that $A$ indeed explains the primary diameter and pressure variations, leaving the network to learn the residual structure along the $\log P_{\mathrm{inj}}$ and $\log P_{\mathrm{ch}}$ axes. If the check fails, training aborts by default, making the physical prior falsifiable rather than merely asserted: the empirically calibrated exponents in $A = \Delta P^{0.5}\cdot\rho_a^{-0.25}\cdot d_n^{0.5}$ carry an empirically testable prediction that the training protocol confirms or rejects prior to any weight updates.

Stage 1 and Stage 2 also share a group-aware data split: the entire operating condition group defined by nozzle identity, injection pressure, chamber pressure, and injection duration is assigned together to the training, validation, or test set, preventing leakage between curves sharing the same operating point. Without this constraint, a random trajectory-level split would allow the network to interpolate between near-identical curves from the same operating condition seen during training, and the holdout error would reflect intra-condition smoothness rather than generalization across the condition space. Therefore, group assignment is a structural guarantee that the reported test metrics describe interpolation across different operating conditions, not intra-condition memorization. Stage 2 warm-starts from Stage 1's engineering run directory and reuses its scaler states, ensuring that variant selection and $A$ scaling remain consistent across the two stages.

\subsubsection{Cross-Population Scaling Verification on Dataset \#2}
\label{sec:pointlevel-scaling-verification}

The construction of Dataset \#2 starts by unrolling the individual $(t,S)$ discrete observation points from the cleaned CDF wide-format sequence table. Even after excluding points from trajectories that reached the spatial FOV cap, the remaining discrete points still exhibit systematic right-censoring, and the onset of censoring varies by operating condition: although the spray plume remains within the field of view, the Mie scattering signal gradually falls below the detection threshold due to droplet breakup, spatial dilution, and local drops in droplet number density, until the signal becomes indistinguishable from the CV-subtracted background both mathematically and visually. Some conditions enter this signal-loss regime at $t<\SI{0.5}{ms}$, while others survive until significantly later. To address this, a point table generation tool was constructed (codebase identifiers listed in Appendix~\ref{app:implementation_mapping}) to automatically determine condition-level censoring thresholds for each of the \num{592} operating condition groups based on two statistical/optical metrics and assign binary censoring labels: (i)~the smoothed trajectory count per bin drops below a fixed fraction of its intra-condition peak (density drop criterion), and (ii)~the per-bin $p_{95}$ penetration reaches $95\%$ of the estimated FOV cap (FOV saturation criterion); Figure~\ref{fig:censor-mechanisms} illustrates one representative example of each. The first bin satisfying either criterion, along with all subsequent observation points, is discarded, leaving $N_{\mathrm{unc}}=\num{506634}$ uncensored points for downstream log-log regression use.

\textbf{Per-Condition Temporal Balance.}
For any regression on time-indexed penetration observations, a direct concern is that trajectories surviving into late time bins represent a progressively self-selected subset of the population---potentially biasing the sample toward slow or wall-restricted plumes. Per-condition truncation eliminates this concern. Before applying the right-censoring labels, the median coefficient of variation for the per-bin trajectory count across time is $\mathrm{CV}_{\mathrm{raw}}=0.52$, reflecting the gradual attrition as trajectories hit the FOV boundary. After truncating each condition at its censoring threshold, the median intra-condition CV drops to $\num{0.052}$ (interquartile range $[0.033,\;0.082]$), and the median ratio of minimum to average bin count within a condition is $0.92$. Within each surviving window, point counts are thus approximately uniform in time: the depleted tails are removed rather than reweighted, ensuring each retained time bin contributes statistically comparable evidence to the downstream regression.

\textbf{Diameter Exponent and H--A Prior.}
The $c_{d_n}$ column in Table~\ref{tab:loglog-regression-uncensored} is extremely large, ranging from $1.7$ to $6.0$; these values lack physical meaning. Two structural reasons prevent the diameter exponent from being identified from this dataset. First, the range for the six nozzle hole diameters is only $0.333$--$\SI{0.384}{mm}$ (a ratio of $1.15$, yielding a log-leverage of $0.14$), which is roughly three times smaller than the log-leverage available across the injection pressure range ($0.45$). Second, pressure and diameter do not vary independently in the design matrix: the full six-diameter coverage occurs only within a single injection pressure family ($P_{\mathrm{inj}}=\SI{2000}{bar}$, $P_{\mathrm{cb}}=\SI{4}{bar}$), and within that family, the nozzle sets differ not only in hole diameter but also in hole count and umbrella angle. Running a controlled check within this matched-pressure family---regressing the per-condition median penetration against $\log d_n$ at fixed $\Delta P$ and $P_{\mathrm{ch}}$---yielded $c_{d_n}\approx 0$ and $R^2<0.01$ across the entire mid-trajectory window, confirming that causal diameter effects cannot be separated from geometric confounders in this dataset. Therefore, the $d_n^{0.5}$ term is retained in $A$ as a Hiroyasu--Arai physically motivated prior; the two empirically supported components, $\Delta P^{0.5}$ and $\rho_a^{-0.25}$, are corroborated by both the $k_{1/4}$ regressions and the current point-level checks on the right-censored dataset.

\subsubsection{LONO Ablation of the Engineered Feature Design}
\label{sec:stage1-lono-ablation}

The single-split improvements reported earlier in this section (Stage 1 test MAE dropping from \SI{8.05}{mm} to \SI{2.91}{mm}) confirm that within the same group-aware split, the seven-feature design outperforms the pure-scaling baseline. A more challenging test is whether the design choices generalize to nozzle families never seen during training. A dedicated leave-one-nozzle-out (LONO) ablation evaluated all nine variants of the engineered feature design in a five-fold split, each time holding out one of nozzle 0, nozzle 1, nozzle 2, nozzle 4, and nozzle 5, while the remaining four families formed the training pool. Every cell of the ablation used the same deterministic Stage 1 MSE objective (described in Section~\ref{sec:mlp-curriculum-en}), identical network architecture and optimizer, and a single fixed seed (42), so the only varying axis is the feature configuration.

The nine variants span three orthogonal design axes: the amplitude exponent (\(\alpha=0.25\) vs. \(\alpha=0.5\) in \(A=\Delta P^{\alpha}\rho_a^{-0.25}d_n^{0.5}\)), the presence of log-pressure residuals (\(\log P_{\mathrm{inj},z}\), \(\log P_{\mathrm{ch},z}\)), and the presence of \(d_n\) as an independent input. A legacy nine-feature reference configuration (where raw \(\log P_{\mathrm{inj}}\), \(\log P_{\mathrm{ch}}\), \(\log\Delta P\), and \(d_n\) were fed directly to the network, targeting \(S\) in millimeters) predating the amplitude scaling was included as a baseline. Table~\ref{tab:stage1-lono-ablation-summary} reports the mean physical MAE across the five folds, and Table~\ref{tab:stage1-lono-ablation-per-fold} breaks down the results per fold (the row labels correspond to variant identifiers in the codebase to facilitate archival reproduction).

Three patterns remain consistent across the design axes:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Exponent \(\alpha=0.5\) outperforms the Hiroyasu--Arai \(\alpha=0.25\) across every level of residual features.} Pairing by "with/without pressure residuals" and "with/without independent diameter," the mean MAEs for \(\alpha=0.5\) vs. \(\alpha=0.25\) are \SI{7.46}{mm} vs. \SI{8.22}{mm} (with pressure residuals), \SI{8.27}{mm} vs. \SI{9.83}{mm} (no residuals), \SI{12.06}{mm} vs. \SI{13.41}{mm} (with diameter), and \SI{14.26}{mm} vs. \SI{14.48}{mm} (with both). The data-supported exponent absorbs more physical variance into \(A\), leaving less unexplained residual for the network---this is the LONO counterpart to the point-level scaling regressions in Table~\ref{tab:loglog-regression-uncensored}.
\item \textbf{Adding log-pressure residuals provides small but consistent LONO gains over pure-scaling variants.} For \(\alpha=0.5\), mean MAE dropped from \SI{8.27}{mm} to \SI{7.46}{mm}; for \(\alpha=0.25\), from \SI{9.83}{mm} to \SI{8.22}{mm}. This gain aligns with the non-zero chamber pressure coefficients at fixed \(\Delta P\) reported earlier: \(P_{\mathrm{inj}}\) and \(P_{\mathrm{ch}}\) carry residual structure beyond what is captured by the fixed \(\Delta P^{0.5}\) exponent.
\item \textbf{Reintroducing \(d_n\) as an independent input is strictly harmful under LONO.} Incorporating normalized diameter as an independent input alongside any amplitude scaling variant increases mean MAE by \(\SI{4}{}\)--\(\SI{6}{mm}\): adding diameter without pressure residuals hits \SI{12.06}{mm}, while the two variants adding both diameter and pressure residuals are the worst performers in the table (\(>\SI{14}{mm}\)), performing slightly worse than even the legacy nine-feature reference. Because \(d_n\) is already encoded in \(A\), and exists at only six discrete levels with severe confounding (Figure~\ref{fig:sparse-support-topology}), the redundant channel allows the network to memorize nozzle identities rather than generalizing across families. This provides the strongest direct evidence supporting the decision to leave \(d_n\) absorbed inside \(A\) instead of re-exposing it to the network.
\end{enumerate}

The production configuration (\(\alpha=0.5\), with log-pressure residuals, no independent diameter) achieved the lowest mean LONO MAE (\SI{7.46}{mm}) and the most consistent per-fold performance of all nine configurations tested, with no single-fold result exceeding \SI{19}{mm}; the legacy nine-feature reference baseline hit \SI{27.29}{mm} on its worst fold (nozzle 4). Even the simplest amplitude scaling variant (\(\alpha=0.25\), no residuals, no independent diameter) improved upon the legacy baseline by \(\sim\!\SI{4.6}{mm}\) in mean LONO, confirming that the amplitude scaling step is the load-bearing element of the engineered feature design. The nozzle 0 fold dominates the mean, contributing 1{,}038 file-level sample groups---roughly five times more than any other fold---while sharing the same nominal nozzle geometry as Nozzle 1 (\(d_n=\SI{0.384}{mm}\), \(N=10\), \(\theta_{\mathrm{umb}}=140°\)); any residual performance gap reflects operating-condition extrapolation rather than a designed geometric departure. Yet even on this fold, the production configuration performed within \(\SI{0.21}{mm}\) of the best amplitude-scaled variant lacking an independent \(d_n\).

\subsection{MLP Curriculum: Stage 1 to Stage 3}
\label{sec:mlp-curriculum-en}

The MLP surrogate model is trained as a curriculum across three data populations rather than a single monolithic regression. Stage 1 learns a stable condition-level mean from the representative q1 trajectories in Dataset \#3. Stage 2 expands to the full filtered q1 archive and learns the heteroscedastic inter-plume spread, yielding a clean teacher. Stage 3 then reintroduces the Dataset \#1 raw CDF observations where coverage is reliable, falling back to the Stage 2 teacher where FOV censoring and signal decay render direct raw supervision unsafe.

\subsubsection{Stage 1 MSE Baseline on Dimensionless Targets}

Stage 1 trains a deterministic mean model on a representative trajectory subset drawn from Dataset \#3. For each file-level operating condition group, the fitted curves are evaluated at \(t_{\mathrm{far}}=\SI{5}{ms}\), the group median penetration is calculated, and the nearest actual q1 fitted trajectory is retained as the Stage 1 representative. This is not the P50/q1 oracle from Dataset \#2. It yields 599 representative trajectories, divided by group into 421/89/89 training/validation/test trajectories.

The loss applied to the dimensionless target $\hat{S}$ is
\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{stage1}}
=\;&\operatorname{MSE}\!\left(\hat{\mu},\hat{S}\right)
+\lambda_{\mathrm{var}}
\mathbb{E}\!\left[\left(\log \hat{\sigma}^2-c_0\right)^2\right]\\
&+\lambda_{1}
\mathbb{E}\!\left[\operatorname{ReLU}\!\left(-\frac{\partial \mu_S}{\partial t}\right)^2\right]
+\lambda_{2}
\mathbb{E}\!\left[g(t)\operatorname{ReLU}\!\left(\frac{\partial^2 \mu_S}{\partial t^2}\right)^2\right],
\end{aligned}
\]
where $c_0=-2$ is the log-variance prior center: since Stage 1 trains on a single representative trajectory per condition, there is no inter-plume spread to learn, so $\log\hat{\sigma}^2$ is regularised toward the constant $e^{c_0}\approx 0.135$ to provide a numerically stable initialisation for Stage 2 warm-starting rather than a data-estimated uncertainty. The one-sided derivative penalties encode two physical constraints on spray penetration: (i)~$\operatorname{ReLU}(-\partial\mu_S/\partial t)^2$ penalises only negative slopes---the negative sign selects the violation direction (a decrease in penetration is unphysical), and the square scales the penalty with the severity of the violation while leaving correct non-negative slopes with zero gradient; (ii)~$\operatorname{ReLU}(\partial^2\mu_S/\partial t^2)^2$ penalises only positive second derivatives---positive curvature means the spray is still accelerating, which is unphysical once the initial momentum-driven transient has passed. The gate $g(t)=\sigma((t-t_c)/\tau_c)$ suppresses the concavity penalty during the early transient where acceleration is expected, activating it progressively only beyond $t_c$. Both penalties are evaluated on the physical reconstruction $\mu_S=A\,\hat{\mu}$ so that the constraints act on the observable millimetre scale rather than on the dimensionless surrogate output. The network is a three-layer MLP of dimensions $[512,512,128]$ with Swish gated activations, a random unit masking rate of 0.3, and a decoupled weight decay AdamW optimizer with gradient clipping and early stopping based on validation loss. The Stage 1 run achieved a test physical MAE of \SI{2.91}{mm}; the holdout metrics are summarized alongside the other two stages in Table~\ref{tab:model-summary}.

\subsubsection{Stage 2 Heteroscedastic NLL Expansion}

Stage 2 warm-starts from the Stage 1 checkpoint and expands the training set from the representative subset to the filtered q1 trajectories in Dataset \#3, replacing the MSE data-fitting term with a heteroscedastic Gaussian NLL over the scaled target:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{stage2}}
&=
\mathbb{E}\!\left[
\frac{1}{2}
\left(
\log \hat{\sigma}^{2}
+\frac{(\hat{S}-\hat{\mu})^{2}}{\hat{\sigma}^{2}}
\right)\right]
\\
&\quad
+\lambda_{1}\,
\mathbb{E}\!\left[
\operatorname{ReLU}\!\left(-\frac{\partial \mu_S}{\partial t}\right)^{2}
\right]
+\lambda_{2}\,
\mathbb{E}\!\left[
g(t)\operatorname{ReLU}\!\left(\frac{\partial^{2}\mu_S}{\partial t^{2}}\right)^{2}
\right],
\\
\hat{\sigma}^{2}
&=\exp(\log\hat{\sigma}^{2})+\varepsilon.
\end{aligned}
\]
The first term is the heteroscedastic NLL on the scaled target $\hat{S}$. The derivative penalties remain on the physical space reconstruction $\mu_S=A\,\hat{\mu}$. The row table expands to 71,700 filtered trajectories, keeping all rows that pass the fit-level mask and the far-time z-score filter for inter-group spread. The split is performed at the grouped case level rather than the individual plume trajectory level, so trajectories from the same physical case do not leak across training, validation, and testing partitions. Each trajectory has 512 time samples over \SIrange{0}{5}{ms}, and the Stage 2 training set contains 36,710,400 point-wise samples: training on 49,908 trajectories, validating on 10,961, and testing on 10,831.

The retained trajectories span the operating range covered by the production training set: control back pressure from 1 to \SI{4}{bar}, injection pressure from 1,400 to \SI{2,200}{bar}, injection duration from 340 to \SI{1,200}{\micro s}, and fitted far-time penetration from \SI{26.2}{mm} to \SI{217.3}{mm}. Thus, Stage 2 sees more than just a single median curve per condition; it observes the residual intra-condition plume and repeat variability that survives outlier removal. The heteroscedastic output is used to decouple two effects that a pure MSE objective conflates: the conditional mean trajectory and its surrounding conditional spread.

Training reuses Stage 1's feature mappings and scaler states. The input vector comprises normalized time coordinate, \(\theta_z\), \(N_{\mathrm{plume},z}\), \(\Delta t_{\mathrm{inj},z}\), \(p_{\mathrm{back},z}\), \(\log P_{\mathrm{inj},z}\), and \(\log P_{\mathrm{ch},z}\) (seven features in total); the architecture remains a three-layer MLP of dimensions $[512,512,128]$, warm-started from the Stage 1 checkpoint. The Stage 2 run utilizes the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of \(8\times10^{-4}\), decoupled weight decay coefficient of \(10^{-4}\), batch size of 96, gradient clipping norm of 1.0, log-variance bounds of \([-10,6]\), and early stopping with a patience of 35. Therefore, the only intentional modification in the data fitting term is the replacement of deterministic MSE with a Gaussian NLL and its learned variance output.

\subsubsection{Stage 3 Raw CDF Distillation and Onset Refinement}
The NLL stage produces heteroscedastic predictions that closely track the fitted training targets, but the fitted curve is not equally faithful across the entire time axis. Near the start of injection, absolute penetration is small, so any timing offset, fit delay ambiguity, or smoothness prior yields a relatively large fractional error; the earliest portion of each predicted curve is shaped primarily by the fitted target prior rather than directly observed raw plume dynamics. Figure~\ref{fig:stage2-toy-inference-early-time} illustrates this in a representative toy inference sweep.

To refine early-time dynamics using raw CDF penetration observations while preventing collapse in late-time sparse regions, a knowledge distillation stage is introduced atop the Stage 2 teacher. In population terms, Stage 3 blends the Dataset \#1 raw observations with the Stage 2 teacher learned from Dataset \#3. The student network inherits the teacher weights and undergoes further training under the joint guidance of raw CDF penetration and the teacher.

\textbf{CDF Penetration Coverage Intervals.} Because the raw CDF penetration sequences are right-censored (by \(t\approx\SI{3}{ms}\), many operating conditions have lost over 70\% of their measurements), direct raw supervision cannot be applied uniformly across time. For each operating condition group, raw observations are binned over \([0,5]\,\si{ms}\) at \(\Delta t=\SI{0.1}{ms}\) intervals. The smoothed sample count per bin dictates three mutually exclusive intervals: high-confidence raw interval (smoothed coverage \(\geq70\%\) of peak), uncertain raw interval (coverage between 20\% and 70\%), and teacher-only extrapolation interval (coverage below 20\% or fewer than 4 samples per bin). Interval transitions require at least two consecutive bins to remain in the same interval, preventing isolated dips from triggering premature interval changes.

The 70\%/20\% coverage boundaries are conservative engineering rules rather than optimal values drawn from a full grid search. The completed Stage 3 ablation experiments support two conclusions: disabling the early anchor reduces global under-prediction bias, and retaining a mild KD mix in the reliable raw interval performs better than completely removing teacher guidance. A systematic sensitivity analysis of the coverage boundaries and \(\sigma_{\mathrm{ref}}\) lies beyond the current ablation scope and is acknowledged as a limitation.

Both channels are further modulated by a global time bin rebalancing weight (inverse frequency, clipped to \([0.5,2.0]\)). In the high-confidence raw interval bins, the raw NLL is the primary signal, accompanied by mild KD regularization. In the uncertain raw interval bins, raw supervision is dropped, and the teacher KD MSE dominates. In the teacher-only extrapolation interval bins, the KD weight is slightly reduced to acknowledge teacher extrapolation uncertainty. Two single-run checks support this interval filter: substituting the default cleaned wide-format CDF sequence table with a pre-truncated raw source yielded negligible RMSE change (\(\Delta\mathrm{RMSE}\approx-\SI{0.003}{mm}\)), whereas disabling the low-confidence raw weight filter degraded RMSE by roughly \(\SI{0.065}{mm}\). The latter is numerically small but directionally significant: treating right-censored late frames as ordinary raw supervision performs demonstrably worse than relying on the teacher in these bins.

\textbf{Composite Loss.} The training objective is
\[
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{raw}} + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KD}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{onset}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{onset}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{anchor}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{anchor}} + \lambda_{d_1}\,P_{d_1} + \lambda_{d_2}\,P_{d_2},
\]
where $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{raw}}$ is the Gaussian NLL targeting the raw CDF penetration in physical space $(\mu,\sigma^2)$, weighted by $w_{\mathrm{raw}}$; $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KD}}$ is a mixed MSE on mean and log-variance in physical space, weighted by $w_{\mathrm{kd}}$:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KD}} = \mathrm{MSE}(\mu_s,\,\mu_t) + \lambda_\sigma\,\mathrm{MSE}(\log\sigma^2_s,\,\log\sigma^2_t),\qquad \lambda_\sigma = 5.0;
\]
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{onset}}$ is the binary cross-entropy loss on the new onset logit output against a ramp target $\min(t/t_{\mathrm{ramp}},1)$, active only for $t\leq\SI{0.2}{ms}$ ($\lambda_{\mathrm{onset}}=0.1$); $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{anchor}}$ is a quadratic penalty pulling $\mu(t\approx0)$ toward zero, tapering off over the first \SI{0.15}{ms}. This anchor term was retained in general exploration and baseline runs ($\lambda_{\mathrm{anchor}}=0.01$), but was set to $\lambda_{\mathrm{anchor}}=0$ in the final selected checkpoint after ablation evidence showed the anchor introduced a small systematic under-prediction. $P_{d_1}=\operatorname{ReLU}(-\Delta\mu)^2$ enforces monotonicity; $P_{d_2}=\operatorname{ReLU}(+\Delta^2\mu)^2$ enforces concavity for $t\geq\SI{0.5}{ms}$.

\subsection{Sparse Heteroscedastic Gaussian Process as an Alternative Surrogate Backbone}
\label{sec:svgp-alternative-backbone-en}

The Sparse Heteroscedastic Gaussian Process model should be interpreted as a strong alternative surrogate backbone rather than a weak baseline. It solves the identical low-dimensional regression problem as the MLP: targeting the dimensionless penetration $\hat S=S/A$, transforming predictions back to millimeters via $A\hat\mu$ and $A\hat\sigma$, and evaluating on the same CDF observations and fixed tables. It is the inductive bias of the Gaussian Process that differs, not the physical objective.

The implemented Gaussian Process employs an "a-only" five-feature set,
\[
[\,t_{\mathrm{norm}},\;\theta_{\mathrm{tilt},z},\;n_{\mathrm{plumes},z},\;t_{i,z},\;P_{\mathrm{cb},z}\,],
\]
with the primary pressure, density, and diameter dependencies absorbed into $A$. It is sparse and heteroscedastic: one Sparse Variational Gaussian Process learns the conditional mean, and a second learns the log-residual variance. Both use a Mat\'ern $5/2$ kernel with Automatic Relevance Determination lengthscales, while inducing-point variational inference renders the problem computationally feasible on the million-point-scale Stage 3 training tables.

This architecture is exceptionally natural for the current task because the input space is low-dimensional and the expected function is smooth. In the refreshed in-domain comparisons and LONO pressure tests (reported in Chapter 5), the SVGP acts as a stronger point predictor. Its kernel prior also offers a more graceful failure mode when a holdout nozzle family falls outside the support of the training matrix: far from the data, the posterior is constrained by the kernel and inducing point structure, rather than allowing a learned nonlinear surface to freely extrapolate.

The MLP is still retained as the primary protocol instantiation for three reasons. First, the Stage 3 student carries an onset side-branch utilized by the screening interface; the current SVGP replaces the penetration $(\mu,\sigma)$ regressor but lacks this side channel. Second, the MLP directly implements the regime-aware raw/KD training curriculum, simplifying the combination of Dataset \#1 raw observations and the Dataset \#3 teacher within a lightweight network. Third, the MLP enables feature, anchor, and distillation ablation matrices to run feasibly on a performance workstation PC. Therefore, the engineering conclusion is conditional: when point RMSE/MAE during interpolation or nozzle-family shift is the priority, select the SVGP; when conservative screening, onset output, deployment simplicity, and design attribution ablations are the priority, select the MLP stack.

\subsection{Physical Baselines and Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:baseline-fitting-protocol-en}

The Hiroyasu--Arai and Naber--Siebers baselines reported in the results chapter are constructed against the identical external clean evaluation table, rather than fitted directly to the raw image pixels. Therefore, the comparison tests the performance of different penetration function families under identical observation definitions and data splits; it does not conflate differing image processing methods.

On the imaging side, inputs first traverse the common workflow described in Chapter 3: nozzle center calibration, polar plume strip extraction, background and gradient enhancement, axial intensity projection per plume, CDF front quantile extraction, anomalous jump truncation, short-segment removal, pixel-to-millimeter conversion, and SOI/hydraulic delay time alignment. The baseline models then receive the point-wise penetration $S(t)$, the corresponding normalized operating condition features, and canonical physical quantities derived from the test matrix JSON file, such as $d_n$, $\Delta P$, $\rho_a$, and $A$.

The Hiroyasu--Arai baseline uses the two-stage function $S_{\mathrm{HA}}(t)$ detailed in the literature review. The "calibrated" row in the results table does not simply recycle literature constants; it fits the logarithmic parameters $(k_v,k_p,k_b)$ on the training split using Huber robust least squares, and subsequently evaluates the fixed parameters on the validation and test splits. The logarithmic parameterization ensures that the gains and breakup timescale remain positive, while the Huber loss mitigates the impact of minor image-derived outliers on the global empirical law.

The Naber--Siebers baseline employs the delayed square-root form $S_{\mathrm{NS}}(t)=k_{\mathrm{NS}}A\sqrt{\max(t-\tau,0)}$, where $A=(\Delta P/\rho_a)^{1/4}d_n^{1/2}$. The reported row fits $\log k_{\mathrm{NS}}$ and $\tau$, with the delay constrained to $[-0.1,0.8]\,\si{ms}$; angle correction exists only as an optional implementation path, and the final comparison uses neither the training-set cone angle nor the oracle cone angle.

To make the physical baselines usable in probabilistic evaluations, their uncertainty is not assigned as an arbitrary constant. Post-fitting, the training/validation residuals are time-binned at $\SI{0.1}{ms}$ intervals, and the robust spread is estimated using $1.4826\,\mathrm{MAD}$, with a floor set at $\SI{0.5}{mm}$ to prevent unrealistically narrow bands in sparse early intervals. This $\sigma(t)$ allows the empirical laws to report $1\sigma$ and $2\sigma$ coverage and CRPS, while preserving their traditional penetration-related mean functions. The sparse Gaussian process described in Section~\ref{sec:svgp-alternative-backbone-en} is evaluated on the exact same tables, but is construed as an alternative probabilistic surrogate backbone rather than a traditional physical baseline.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Protocol and Metrics}
\label{sec:evaluation-protocol-en}

All models are evaluated on a common holdout table exported from the aforementioned external clean evaluation process. This process reruns the entire image processing and target construction chain on a frozen image archive, yielding penetration tables that are then grouped into training, validation, and test splits. The test set is never used for model selection or hyperparameter tuning; the reported test set figures thus represent single-shot evaluations on data the model has never influenced.

Four evaluation perspectives are kept separate as they address different questions. The full-clean CDF protocol measures overall point accuracy on the common cleaned CDF set, including right-censored regions. The conservative uncensored CDF protocol removes intervals exceeding the density drop or FOV saturation thresholds from Dataset \#2, serving as the primary physical point-level metric for censor-controlled comparisons. The P50 observation protocol collapses each condition to the median observed penetration points, testing the robustness of the mean head against inter-plume spread. The Q1 extrapolation protocol evaluates model predictions over the companion q1 grid fitted to the P50 trajectories; it is an extrapolation diagnostic, not an independent ground truth.

\textbf{Point Error Metrics.} Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) measure the accuracy of the predicted conditional mean $\mu(t)$ against holdout point-wise penetration observations. Both are computed across the full time horizon covered by the test trajectories. Because errors within a single trajectory are temporally correlated, the effective sample size is much smaller than the raw point count; the reported values describe the average per-point error across a heterogeneous population of operating conditions and time points, rather than statistically independent samples.

\textbf{Empirical Coverage.} A heteroscedastic model outputs a conditional mean $\mu(t)$ and conditional standard deviation $\sigma(t)$. The primary calibration question is whether the predicted uncertainty intervals are empirically credible: under a Gaussian predictive distribution, 68.3\% of observations should fall within the $[\mu-\sigma,\,\mu+\sigma]$ interval, and 95.4\% within the $[\mu-2\sigma,\,\mu+2\sigma]$ interval. The empirical $1\sigma$ coverage on the holdout test set acts as the principal calibration metric for each probabilistic model: values above 68.3\% indicate conservative (over-dispersed) intervals, while lower values signify overconfident (under-dispersed) predictions. For physical baselines, $\sigma(t)$ is the time-binned robust residual spread estimated from training residuals; for MLP stages, $\sigma(t)$ is the direct network output.

\textbf{Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS).} The CRPS evaluates the predicted cumulative distribution function $F$ against a scalar observation $y$, defined as
\[
\mathrm{CRPS}(F,y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\!\left(F(z)-\mathbf{1}[z\geq y]\right)^2\mathrm{d}z.
\]
Under a Gaussian predictive distribution with mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$, this simplifies to a closed-form expression that rewards both accuracy (small $|\mu-y|$) and calibration (a $\sigma$ that is neither too wide nor too narrow). Lower CRPS is better; a perfectly sharp and accurate point prediction yields $\mathrm{CRPS}=0$.

\textbf{Reliability, ECE, and PIT.} For each prediction, the observation can be transformed into a predictive percentile,
\[
u_i=\Phi\!\left(\frac{y_i-\mu_i}{\sigma_i}\right).
\]
If the Gaussian predictive distribution is calibrated, the \(u_i\) values should be uniformly distributed over \([0,1]\). Reliability diagrams compare nominal lower-tail probabilities against the empirical fraction of points falling below that percentile, and Expected Calibration Error (ECE) averages the absolute deviations over a grid of probability levels. Probability Integral Transform (PIT) histograms visually portray the same information: U-shaped histograms indicate under-dispersion, hump-shaped indicate over-dispersion, and skewness indicates mean bias. Because the full-clean table contains hundreds of thousands of time-correlated points, the KS p-value is treated as a sensitivity indicator rather than a binary pass/fail label; the histogram shape, ECE, and coverage curves carry the engineering interpretation.

\textbf{Leave-One-Nozzle-Family-Out Evaluation.} The standard group-aware split retains all nozzle families in the training and validation partitions and evaluates interpolation across the supported domain of pressure and duration combinations. To probe out-of-distribution robustness in a physically meaningful direction, a secondary LONO evaluation is performed: all operating conditions belonging to one nozzle family are removed from the training and validation splits, the model is retrained on the remaining families, and performance is evaluated solely on the held-out family. This process is repeated for each nozzle family. Since nozzle families differ in hole diameter, plume count, and early transient spray formation dynamics, the LONO test asks whether the staged architecture can generalize to nozzle geometries never seen during training---the most challenging interpolation scenario the current dataset can offer. Results for both evaluation protocols are reported in Chapter 5.

\subsection{Probabilistic Screening Interface}
\label{sec:screening-interface-en}

The discussion of spray-wall impingement mechanisms in the literature review concluded that the appropriate downstream application for an uncertainty-aware penetration surrogate is a probabilistic screening proxy rather than a binary pass/fail rule, and that penetration should be projected onto the engine geometry plane as a rotating 2D Gaussian before integrating against the cylinder wall and piston crown masks. The current GUI prototype realizes this proxy by coupling the final Stage 3 student checkpoint with a parameterized piston crown, cylinder wall, and slider-crank displacement model, outputting spatial snapshots, probabilistic time histories, cumulative exposure, and animations. The MLP is currently the most complete plug-and-play model for this interface, providing both penetration \((\mu,\sigma)\) and the onset side-branch utilized in the diagnostic plots. The SVGP from Section~\ref{sec:svgp-alternative-backbone-en} could replace the penetration mean and variance in the same geometric integration, but lacking the extra side model, it cannot directly replace the onset branch. The default configuration uses the Design A piston, \(\mathrm{RPM}=250\), crank phase \(0^{\circ}\), a time window of 0--\SI{5}{ms}, and a 60-frame time grid.

\textbf{Spray Probability Density.} Let each plume's spray axis be rotated by tilt angle \(\theta_{\mathrm{tilt}}\) from radial, and let \(\alpha\) denote the half-cone angle. In the plume-aligned coordinate system, the student prediction supplies the axis-parallel standard deviation \(\sigma_{\parallel}(t)\), while the lateral spread is represented by a second Gaussian. The current implementation does not set the lateral standard deviation equal to the geometric half-width; it interprets the fixed half-cone angle as an approximate three-standard-deviation envelope and adds a grid-size floor:
\[
\sigma_{\perp}(t)
=\max\!\left\{
\frac{\max\!\left(\mu_{+}(t),\sigma_{\parallel}(t)\right)\tan(\alpha/2)}{3},
\;2\Delta x
\right\},\qquad
\mu_{+}(t)=\max(\mu(t),0).
\]
Here $\Delta x$ is the grid spacing, defaulting to \(\SI{0.25}{mm/px}\). The lower bound prevents the lateral PDF from non-physically collapsing near the start of injection when \(\mu(t)\) is small, and the \(1/3\) factor treats the geometric cone angle as an approximate \(3\sigma\) envelope---an engineering convention for probabilistic collision screening rather than a value calibrated against measured spray widths. This fixed-\(\alpha\) assumption serves only for early screening and ranking, not as a validated time-varying spray envelope model. The joint density
\[
p(x,y,t)=\mathcal{N}\!\Bigl((x,y);\,\mathbf{c}(t),\,\mathbf{R}(\theta_{\mathrm{tilt}})\,\operatorname{diag}\!\bigl(\sigma_{\parallel}^{2}(t),\sigma_{\perp}^{2}(t)\bigr)\,\mathbf{R}(\theta_{\mathrm{tilt}})^{\top}\Bigr),
\]
centered at $\mathbf{c}(t)=\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{nozzle}}+\mathbf{R}(\theta_{\mathrm{tilt}})\,(\mu_{+}(t),0)^{\top}$, is evaluated on a regular canvas grid (default \(\SI{0.25}{mm/px}\), window \(125\times\SI{200}{mm}\), cylinder wall at \(x=\SI{120}{mm}\)) and renormalized per frame so that $\iint p\,\mathrm{d}A=1$. The student's scaled outputs are un-normalized into physical space via the empirically calibrated amplitude $A=\Delta P^{0.5}\cdot\rho_a^{-0.25}\cdot d_n^{0.5}$ introduced for the engineered feature architecture, ensuring $\mu$ and $\sigma_{\parallel}$ enter the PDF in millimeters.

\textbf{Piston Geometry Scheduler.} The prototype exposes three interchangeable crown profiles. The default Design A is a piecewise circular arc structure, assembling a bowl-shaped crown from five arc radii---outer bowl, inner bowl, crown, lip, and bottom---with rigid closure constraints that maintain geometric consistency whenever any radius is altered. Design B is a dual-arc bowl profile with tangential constraints. The Hermite variant replaces the arc structure with a *quintic Hermite spline*, defined by a small set of control points, each carrying position, tangent vector, and second derivative vector in the (radial, depth) cross-sectional plane. Matching position, slope, and curvature at each node provides $C^{2}$ continuity across segment boundaries, meaning the crown surface lacks curvature jumps---a property relevant to both CNC machining (where curvature discontinuities translate to tool acceleration steps) and collision integration grids (where sudden curvature changes can alias onto rasterized masks).

This parameterization also simplifies systematic parameter sweeps: altering a single scalar---e.g., bowl depth---continuously deforms the entire bowl profile, while the rest of the control point structure adapts via splines without requiring the re-solving of constraint equations. Figure~\ref{fig:hermite-crown} illustrates the baseline four-point crown with its tangent arrows (panel a) alongside a seven-level depth sweep (panel b), showcasing continuous, smooth deformation induced by changing one parameter. All three designs return a binary piston mask on the identical canvas grid as the spray density; recent default GUI artifacts utilize Design A, so the black crown shown in default screening images is not the Hermite swept profile.

\textbf{Piston Kinematics.} The crown axial position follows the simplified slider-crank form \cite{HeywoodICE}
\[
h_{\mathrm{crown}}(t)=h_{0}-\tfrac{s}{2}\cos(\omega t+\varphi),\qquad \omega=\tfrac{2\pi\,\mathrm{RPM}}{60},
\]
implemented with \(t\) passed in seconds to the angular velocity term, while the GUI displays the time axis in milliseconds. This expression is the slider-crank limit as $r/l\to 0$, omitting higher-order connecting rod corrections. The phase $\varphi$ situates the reference event at $t=0$. Default prototype settings ($\mathrm{RPM}=250$, $s/2=\SI{55}{mm}$, $h_{0}=\SI{75}{mm}$, $\varphi=0$) correspond to a short injection window near top dead center, during which the crown moves less than \SI{1}{mm}; within this regime, connecting rod corrections are negligible.

\textbf{Per-Frame Collision Integration.} The instantaneous collision probability and cumulative exposure over the injection window are
\[
P_{\mathrm{hit}}(t)=\iint p(x,y,t)\,\mathbf{1}_{\Omega_{\mathrm{piston}}(t)}(x,y)\,\mathrm{d}A,\qquad E=\int_{0}^{T} P_{\mathrm{hit}}(t)\,\mathrm{d}t,
\]
where $\mathbf{1}_{\Omega_{\mathrm{piston}}(t)}$ is the binary indicator function for the instantaneous piston mask; wall probability $P_{\mathrm{wall}}(t)$ is computed by integrating the same PDF over the wall mask $x\geq\SI{120}{mm}$. Spatial integration executes over the canvas grid, while cumulative exposure is computed via trapezoidal quadrature over the time grid. \textbf{Default Output Conventions.} Recent GUI artifacts utilize \(d_n=\SI{0.34}{mm}\), injection pressure \SI{2000}{bar}, chamber pressure \SI{15}{bar}, control back pressure \SI{4}{bar}, 10 plumes, injection duration \SI{800}{\micro s}, fixed cone angle \(20^{\circ}\), and tilt angle \(20^{\circ}\). Within the 0--\SI{5}{ms} window, the non-negative mean plotted spans \(\mu_{+}(t)\in[\SI{1.70}{mm},\,\SI{112.95}{mm}]\), the axial standard deviation spans \(\sigma_{\parallel}(t)\in[\SI{4.39}{mm},\,\SI{17.66}{mm}]\), and the lateral standard deviation spans \(\sigma_{\perp}(t)\in[\SI{0.50}{mm},\,\SI{6.64}{mm}]\). For the default Design A run, piston hit probability peaks at \(P_{\mathrm{hit}}=0.7766\) at \(t=\SI{4.237}{ms}\), with a co-frame wall probability of \(P_{\mathrm{wall}}=0.0660\); the cumulative exposure over the window is \(E=\SI{2.475}{ms}\), and the maximum wall probability is \(0.0857\). These values indicate that the end-to-end chain can propagate penetration distributions into rankable, time-resolved screening scores; they should not be construed as validated in-cylinder droplet impingement probabilities.

\textbf{Claimed Scope.} The rotating Gaussian envelope, analytical cone angle spreading, and slider-crank crown model are engineering simplifications, not resolved droplet-scale physics. This prototype is the probabilistic screening proxy argued for in the literature review; it is not a collision predictor validated against experimental high-speed cinematography or CFD impingement fields.

The modeling workflow presented in this chapter---from cleaned reduced-order targets to staged heteroscedastic training, and finally to probabilistic collision scoring---constitutes the surrogate modeling contribution of this thesis. Quantitative comparisons on holdout test data among Stage 1, Stage 2, Stage 3, physical baselines, and the sparse Gaussian process alternative backbone, including coverage statistics and leave-one-nozzle-family-out pressure tests, will be presented in Chapter 5.
