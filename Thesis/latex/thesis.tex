\setmainabstract{Pilot diesel injections in multi-fuel marine engines are short, highly transient events. If the liquid fuel spray reaches the piston crown or cylinder wall before ignition, it can cause wall wetting, engine oil dilution, and poor combustion. This thesis develops an engineering screening workflow that converts high-speed Mie-scattering spray videos into penetration measurements and trained surrogate models for early spray-wall contact risk assessment. The experimental data consist of top-view recordings from an optical spray combustion chamber under various operating conditions and nozzle designs.

The workflow integrates video processing to extract spray plumes, a reduced-order trajectory fit to handle spatial right-censoring caused by the camera's limited field of view, and a multi-stage neural network. The neural network is trained to predict the spray penetration mean and an uncertainty band around it that widens or narrows depending on the operating condition. On a held-out dataset of 542,565 points, the multilayer perceptron (MLP) model achieves a mean root mean squared error (RMSE) of 4.27 mm and a mean absolute error (MAE) of 3.03 mm. This reduces prediction error by more than 54\% compared to traditional empirical baselines. A sparse variational Gaussian process (SVGP) alternative is also evaluated, showing higher point accuracy but less conservative uncertainty coverage. Finally, a geometric projection maps the predicted penetration distribution onto simplified engine dimensions to calculate probabilistic wall-collision screening scores. This workflow provides a data-driven tool to screen nozzle designs and flag spray-wall impingement risks before proceeding to expensive computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations or engine tests.}
