\setmainabstract{Pilot diesel injections in multi-fuel marine engines are short, strongly transient events. If the liquid envelope reaches the piston crown or cylinder wall before useful ignition develops, wall wetting, lubricant dilution, and poor mixture preparation can follow. This thesis develops an evidence-grounded screening workflow that converts high-speed Mie-scattering videos into penetration-oriented observables and a surrogate model for early spray-wall-risk assessment. The data consist of room-temperature, non-reacting top-view recordings from the W\"artsil\"a Optical Spray Combustion Chamber, covering multiple operating conditions and nozzle geometries. The workflow combines manual nozzle calibration, CUDA-accelerated multihole video processing, plume-wise feature extraction, and a reduced-order trajectory fit that both smooths noisy labels and reduces the downward bias caused by right-censored observations near the field-of-view limit. On these fitted targets, a three-stage neural surrogate is trained. Stage 1 learns a deterministic representative-trajectory baseline. Stage 2 extends the model to heteroscedastic mean-and-variance prediction on the full filtered dataset. Stage 3 refines early-time behaviour against raw CDF observations while retaining conservative teacher guidance where late-time coverage becomes sparse. This staging is designed to prevent the model from mistaking disappearance from the image window for physical saturation. Across 21,470 trajectories from 9 nozzle campaigns, the selected anchor-off Stage 3 model reaches an overall RMSE of 6.68 mm and retains conservative uncertainty coverage of 72.24 percent at 1 sigma and 97.03 percent at 2 sigma. Under the latest external-cleaning protocol, containing 795,561 prediction points and 33,845 trajectories, the transition-region-scaled three-stage model reaches a four-run mean RMSE of 6.094 mm, a seed-to-seed standard deviation of 0.120 mm, an MAE of 4.465 mm, a 95th percentile absolute error of 12.888 mm, and 1-sigma / 2-sigma coverage of 0.783 / 0.982. Compared with calibrated Hiroyasu--Arai, delayed Naber--Siebers, and sparse heteroscedastic Gaussian-process baselines, the model has lower RMSE, MAE, and tail error. A simplified geometric projection then maps the predicted penetration distribution onto a probabilistic collision-screening proxy. The intended use is explicit: the workflow is an early-stage engineering screening tool within its measured operating domain, not a substitute for CFD, engine testing, or validated in-cylinder impingement physics.}
